Jeremiah 23-25: False Shepherds, the Righteous Branch, and Seventy Years

Read Jeremiah 23-25 in the NIV →

Context

Chapter 23 opens with a woe against the shepherds, Judah's kings and leaders, who have scattered the flock, and pivots to a bright promise: God will raise up a Righteous Branch, a king who will reign wisely and be called The Lord Our Righteousness. The chapter then turns to a sustained attack on the false prophets who claim dreams as revelation and say peace where there is no peace.

Chapter 24 gives the vision of two baskets of figs, one good and one rotten, reframing the exiles already taken to Babylon as the good figs whom God will restore. Chapter 25 sets a date stamp on the disaster: seventy years of Babylonian dominion, followed by judgment on Babylon itself, dramatized as a cup of God's wrath that the nations must drink.

Key themes

Shepherds who scatter, a King who gathers. The leaders entrusted with the flock have driven it away, so God will gather the remnant himself and set true shepherds over them. The promise climaxes in the Righteous Branch, a coming Davidic king whose very name declares the righteousness Judah's kings lacked.

The danger of self-generated religion. The false prophets are not crude liars so much as confident dreamers who mistake their own optimism for God's word. Jeremiah's test is sobering: is the word like fire and a hammer that breaks rock, or merely soothing? Truth that costs nothing is suspect.

God's strange mercy in exile. The good figs are not those who stayed in Jerusalem but those carried off to Babylon. Exile, the apparent curse, becomes the place where God sets his eyes on his people for good. Judgment and grace are not always where we expect them.

A measured judgment. The seventy years give the catastrophe a horizon. The exile is real and long, but it is not endless, and Babylon, the instrument of wrath, will in turn drink the cup. God's justice reaches even the empire he used.

Takeaways

  • Leaders are accountable for the flock; those who scatter and exploit will answer to the Shepherd who promises to gather what they drove away.
  • Comforting words are not automatically true words. The genuine word of God confronts as well as consoles, like fire and a hammer.
  • Exile had a horizon of seventy years. God's hardest seasons are bounded, and even the empires he uses for judgment are themselves held to account.

Connections

The Righteous Branch of 23:5-6 develops the Davidic covenant of 2 Samuel 7, read earlier in this plan, and is echoed in Zechariah's Branch and ultimately claimed in the Gospels for Jesus. The good and bad figs anticipate the new-heart promise just chapters later in Jeremiah 31-32. The seventy years become a fixed reference point that Daniel 9 prays over and that 2 Chronicles 36 cites as the reason the land finally enjoys its sabbaths.

2 Samuel 7:12-16 → Zechariah 3:8 → Daniel 9:1-3 → 2 Chronicles 36:21 →

Reflection

It is worth sitting with the basket of good figs. The people who looked cursed, dragged off to a pagan empire, were the ones God called good and promised to restore, while those who congratulated themselves on staying behind were the rotten figs. Our circumstances are a poor read on God's regard for us. The question is not where you have landed but whether his eyes are on you for good, and the seventy-year horizon insists that even long exiles end.
