Epistles Day 50 of 364 • Week 8 • May 17, 2026

Romans 15-16

A Masterwork Complete: Paul's Vision and the Community of Grace

Context: The Grand Finale

After eleven chapters of dense theological argument and four chapters of practical application, Paul now transitions to the most personal section of Romans. Paul turns to his plans to visit Rome. He longs to come and visit the Christians there on his way to preach the gospel in Spain. This conclusion serves multiple purposes: it establishes Paul's apostolic credentials with a church he didn't plant, explains his travel plans, and—through the extensive greetings in chapter 16—demonstrates the interconnected nature of the early Christian movement.

Paul's Apostolic Self-Understanding

The offering of the Gentiles: Romans 15:16 is filled with the language of priesthood. Paul says he serves as a "ministering priest" of Jesus Christ presenting the gospel as a "priestly service" so Gentile converts would be an acceptable sacrifice to God. This priestly metaphor is remarkable: With the Jerusalem temple still standing (pre-AD 70), Paul boldly claims that true priestly service is gospel proclamation and that Gentile converts are acceptable offerings—a radical reimagining of Israel's cult in light of Christ and the Spirit.

Paul made it his aim to preach the gospel, not where Christ was named, lest he should build on another man's foundation. Rather he wanted to do pioneer work for the Lord – not because it was wrong or bad to continue the work begun through another man, but because there was so much to do on the frontiers. Paul often evangelized where no man had gone before. His task, as Apostle to the Gentiles, was to move from place to place among the nations, evangelizing and planting churches.

The Phoebe Phenomenon

Romans 16 opens with a remarkable introduction: Phoebe was a first-century Christian woman mentioned by the Apostle Paul in his Epistle to the Romans, verses 16:1–2. A notable woman in the church of Cenchreae, she was trusted by Paul to deliver his letter to the Romans. What Paul is telling us in Romans 16:1-2 is that the first person to preach on the book of Romans was Phoebe. Phoebe would have taken Paul's letter to the various house churches in Rome, she would have read the letter, then she would have answered any questions that those hearing would have asked. In short, Phoebe was the first person to read and teach on Romans.

Phoebe is the only woman named as deacon (which means "servant" in Greek) in the Bible. Apostle Paul used the Greek diakonos (διάκονος) to designate Phoebe as a deacon. Unfortunately, translation history reveals a bias against this woman simply because she was a woman. In the KJV, every time Paul used the word diakonos to describe a specific person, it was translated as minister except for one instance, this passage in Romans 16:1 about Phoebe. I must stress that there is no biblical warrant to treat Phoebe as a different type of deacon than the men.

A Community of Diverse Gifts

The thirty-five names mentioned in chapter 16 reveal the stunning diversity of the early church. One of the reasons I think we have the long list of greetings in Romans is for us to see the vital role that many women played in the early church. Twenty-nine people are mentioned in Romans 16:1-16; ten of these are women. It is remarkable that so many women deserved special mention in this letter. Beyond gender diversity, we see social diversity: She was a patroness supporting the local church. Paul goes on to say that she even supported his ministry. And so, not unlike the women who followed Jesus with the disciples, who gave of their own means as a stewardship to support Jesus' ministry, apparently Phoebe did this in the church in Cenchrea and in Corinth.

Cross-References: Completing the Arc

This passage beautifully completes themes Paul has developed throughout Romans. The priestly language of 15:16 echoes Day 15's discussion of Romans 5-6, where Paul showed how Christ's redemptive victory transforms us into "slaves of righteousness." Now Paul reveals that this transformation extends to making Gentile converts themselves "acceptable offerings" to God. The pioneer missionary spirit connects to Day 22's Romans 7-8, where the Spirit's liberating power wasn't just for individual transformation but for the global mission Paul describes here. Most significantly, the diverse community pictured in chapter 16 fulfills Day 29's promise from Romans 9-10 about God's righteousness being offered "to everyone who believes"—here we see that promise incarnated in the beautiful tapestry of the Roman church.

Reflection: The Church as God Intended

Paul concludes his theological masterpiece not with abstract doctrine but with concrete relationships. Paul's commendation of Phoebe shows the importance of people to the Apostle Paul, and it commends to us the same attitude—this duty of showing appreciation and gratitude for the people of God. Isn't it encouraging to see the pre-eminent theologian of the New Testament taking up an entire chapter of his theological masterpiece, the Book of Romans, to thank people. The church Paul envisions isn't a hierarchy but a symphony—diverse instruments playing in harmony, each contributing their unique gifts to God's grand composition. Phoebe the deacon-teacher, Priscilla and Aquila the tentmaking theologians, wealthy patrons and freed slaves, Jews and Gentiles—all united not by social status but by shared life in Christ. This is the church as God intended: a community where the gospel doesn't erase differences but sanctifies them for kingdom purposes.
