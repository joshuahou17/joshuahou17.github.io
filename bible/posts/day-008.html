Day 8 of 365

Job 17-20

I Know That My Redeemer Lives

Read Job 17-20 in the NIV →

Context

The second cycle continues with mounting heat. In chapter 17 Job, still answering Eliphaz, sinks into despair, naming the grave as his only remaining home and his friends as men whose minds God has closed to understanding. Chapter 18 brings Bildad, who responds not with comfort but with an elaborate and frightening description of the destruction that overtakes the wicked, every line implying that Job is precisely such a man.

Chapter 19 is the heart of the day and one of the summits of the book. Job protests that even his family, servants and closest friends have abandoned him, and he begs for pity. Then, against all the evidence of his ruined body and shattered life, he utters a confession that has echoed through the centuries: 'I know that my Redeemer lives, and that in the end he will stand upon the earth,' and that in his own flesh he will see God. Chapter 20 returns to Zophar, who insists once more that the triumph of the wicked is brief.

Key themes

Abandonment and isolation. Job's deepest wound in these chapters is relational; he is estranged from kin, household and friends. Suffering compounds when those who should draw near withdraw.

The living Redeemer. Job's confession reaches past his accusers, past even death, to a vindicator who will rise to defend him. Whatever its precise reference, it expresses a defiant trust that the truth of his case will not die with him.

Seeing God for himself. Job longs not only for vindication but for vision: 'I myself will see him with my own eyes.' His hope is finally for unmediated encounter with God, the very thing the book's climax will grant him.

Takeaways

  • Job's confession of a Redeemer arises not from comfort but from the depths of abandonment, showing that faith's boldest claims can be born in its darkest moments.
  • The Hebrew word for 'redeemer' (go'el) names a kinsman obligated to rescue and avenge a relative, framing Job's hope in deeply personal, familial terms.
  • Bildad and Zophar can describe the doom of the wicked vividly but cannot see the righteous sufferer in front of them; accurate doctrine is no substitute for discernment.

Connections

The role of the go'el or kinsman-redeemer that Job invokes is acted out in the book of Ruth, where Boaz redeems Naomi's family line, and it culminates in the New Testament's portrait of Christ as the one who became kin to us in order to redeem. Job's longing to see God 'in my flesh' anticipates the resurrection hope made explicit by Paul and the promise that the pure in heart will see God. His sense of total abandonment foreshadows the Servant of Isaiah 53, despised and rejected, and the suffering of Jesus forsaken by his friends.

Job 19:25-27 → Ruth 4:9-10 → Isaiah 53:3 → Matthew 5:8 → 1 Corinthians 15:20-23 →

Reflection

Job clings to a Redeemer he cannot see while sitting in ashes that say God has abandoned him. What would it mean for you to confess that your Redeemer lives precisely on the days when nothing in your circumstances confirms it?
