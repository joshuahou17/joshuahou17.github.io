Day 327 of 365

Acts 15-16

Grace Without Borders

Read Acts 15-16 in the NIV →

Context

Acts 15 records what is often called the most important meeting in the early church. Some believers from Judea insisted that Gentile converts must be circumcised and keep the law of Moses to be saved. Paul and Barnabas dispute this sharply, and the question is carried to the apostles and elders in Jerusalem.

Peter recounts how God gave the Spirit to Gentiles by faith alone, Paul and Barnabas testify to the signs God did among the Gentiles, and James, leading the council, proposes a verdict grounded in Scripture: Gentiles are not to be burdened with the law, only asked to abstain from a few practices that would needlessly offend Jewish believers. The decision is sent out as a letter, a landmark affirmation that salvation is by grace.

The second journey then begins, but not smoothly. Paul and Barnabas part ways over John Mark, and Paul takes Silas while Barnabas takes Mark. Paul adds young Timothy, and after the Spirit blocks several routes, a vision of a man of Macedonia calls them to Europe. In Philippi the Lord opens Lydia's heart, and after Paul and Silas are beaten and imprisoned, an earthquake leads the jailer and his household to faith.

Key themes

Salvation by grace. The council settles the gospel's central question: Gentiles are saved through the grace of the Lord Jesus, just as Jews are, without first becoming Jews. The law is not a gateway to salvation but a burden no one could bear.

Unity without uniformity. The Jerusalem letter asks Gentiles to honor a few sensitivities so that Jewish and Gentile believers can share table fellowship. The church holds together one people from many backgrounds without erasing their differences.

Divine guidance. The Spirit repeatedly closes doors before the Macedonian vision opens one. God directs the mission's geography, and the team obeys, showing that obstacles can be redirection rather than defeat.

The gospel for all kinds. A wealthy merchant woman, an exploited slave girl, and a Roman jailer all come to faith in Philippi. The good news crosses lines of gender, class, and nation in a single chapter.

Takeaways

  • The earliest church fought hardest to keep the gospel free: salvation is a gift of grace, never a reward for conforming to religious markers.
  • Genuine unity makes room for differences while asking each side to defer in love for the sake of shared fellowship.
  • Closed doors and even sharp disagreements among believers can serve God's larger purpose of carrying the gospel into new places.

Connections

The council's verdict completes the trajectory begun when Peter visited Cornelius in Acts 10 and when Paul turned to the Gentiles in the previous reading of Acts 13. James grounds the decision in the prophet Amos, reading the rebuilding of David's tent as the ingathering of the nations. The dispute itself is the live issue behind Paul's letter to the Galatians, which defends justification by faith apart from the law. Lydia's open heart recalls how the Lord, not human persuasion, opens people to believe, echoing Jesus' teaching in John 6 that the Father draws people to the Son. And Paul and Silas singing in prison embodies the joy in suffering that James, just read, commended.

Reflection

The council shows a church willing to do hard theological work together rather than letting the loudest voices win or splitting into camps. When you face a divisive question among fellow believers, do you seek shared discernment under Scripture, or simply defend the position you arrived with?

Consider too the three Philippian conversions. The gospel reached a businesswoman, a trafficked girl, and a hardened jailer alike. There is no person whose status, past, or hardness puts them beyond the reach of grace, including those you might quietly write off.
