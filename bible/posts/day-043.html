Exodus 39-40

The Glory Fills the Tent

Read Exodus 39-40 in the NIV →

Context

Exodus ends where it has been heading all along. The garments for Aaron and his sons are completed, every component is brought to Moses, and he sees that the people have done it all just as the LORD commanded. Moses blesses them, then erects the tabernacle on the first day of the first year.

The book closes with one of the most charged scenes in the Torah: the cloud covers the tent of meeting and the glory of the LORD fills the tabernacle. The God who spoke from a burning bush and thundered on Sinai now takes up residence in the middle of the camp.

Yet the final note is unsettling. Moses, the one man who spoke with God face to face, cannot enter because the glory is too overwhelming. The closing image points forward to Leviticus, which will explain how a holy God can dwell safely among an unholy people.

Key themes

God comes to dwell with his people. The whole arc of Exodus moves from bondage to presence. The point of rescue was never merely freedom from Egypt but freedom for fellowship with God, who now lives at the center of the camp.

Glory both invites and overwhelms. The descent of the glory is the fulfillment of every promise, yet it also bars Moses from entering. Nearness to holiness is wonderful and dangerous at once, and the tension demands resolution.

Completion and inspection. Moses examines the work and blesses it, echoing the rhythm of creation where God saw all he had made and blessed it. The tabernacle is a small, ordered world where heaven and earth meet.

Takeaways

  • The destination of redemption is God's presence with his people, not merely their escape from danger.
  • Holiness is gloriously good news and genuinely weighty, and a faith that loses the weight has lost something true about God.
  • The unfinished tension of Moses being unable to enter is intentional, teaching us that we need a way to draw near that the next book will begin to provide.

Connections

The glory filling the tabernacle deliberately echoes God filling creation and resting on the seventh day in Genesis 1 through 2; the sanctuary is a portable Eden. The same glory later fills Solomon's temple, and the New Testament claims it in Jesus, the Word who "tabernacled" among us, and finally in Revelation where God dwells with humanity with no temple at all. The cloud guiding Israel's journeys recalls the pillar that led them out of Egypt.

Reflection

Exodus ends with God present but access still restricted. Sit with that tension rather than rushing past it. What does it stir in you to know that the glory you long to be near is also holy enough to keep Moses outside, and how does that deepen your gratitude for the access you have been given in Christ?
