Day 297 of 365

John 9-10

Sight for the Blind and the Voice of the Shepherd

Read John 9-10 in the NIV →

Context

John 9 tells a single story with unusual completeness. A man blind from birth is healed when Jesus makes mud, anoints his eyes, and sends him to wash in the pool of Siloam. What follows is a courtroom drama in miniature: neighbors question, parents hedge in fear, and the authorities interrogate the man twice. As the leaders grow more entrenched, the healed man grows bolder, until he is thrown out and then found by Jesus, whom he worships. The chapter turns the metaphor of sight inside out, for the one born blind comes to see while those who claim to see are shown blind.

John 10 is not a new subject but the verdict on what just happened. Because Israel's shepherds had abused the man, his parents, and the people, Jesus contrasts his own leadership with theirs. Drawing on the common village sheepfold, where one doorkeeper admitted the true shepherds and the sheep followed only their own shepherd's voice, Jesus declares himself both the door and the good shepherd. The discourse later returns to the temple at the Feast of Dedication, where his claim to be one with the Father again brings the crowd to the edge of stoning.

Key themes

Physical sight and spiritual seeing. The healing is real, but John means it to be read on two levels. The man moves from blindness to sight to worship, while the experts who never doubted their own vision are pronounced guilty precisely because they claim to see.

Faith that grows under pressure. The man's understanding deepens with each interrogation, from a man called Jesus, to a prophet, to one from God, to Lord whom he worships. Opposition refines rather than crushes his confession.

The shepherd who knows his own. Jesus knows his sheep by name and they know his voice. This is intimate, personal belonging, set against hirelings who flee and strangers the sheep will not follow.

The shepherd who dies for the sheep. Unlike any ordinary shepherd, the good shepherd lays down his life of his own accord. The protective image becomes a sacrificial one, and the door that means safety is opened by the death of the one who is the door.

Takeaways

  • The man born blind shows that honest, growing faith often matures most under hostile questioning, while settled religious certainty can harden into blindness.
  • Jesus claims an intimate, personal knowledge of his people; the sheep are known by name and respond to a voice they recognize, not to strangers.
  • The Good Shepherd's care is not merely protective but sacrificial; he lays down his life willingly, distinguishing himself from every hireling who runs when danger comes.

Connections

The Good Shepherd discourse is the fulfillment of Ezekiel 34, where God condemns the false shepherds of Israel for fattening themselves while the flock scatters and promises to shepherd his people himself. It echoes the comfort of Psalm 23, the LORD as the shepherd who leads and provides. The motif of seeking the lost sheep also ties back to Jesus' own parable in Matthew 18, read earlier in this plan. And the pool of Siloam, whose name means Sent, quietly reinforces John's theme of Jesus as the one sent by the Father.

Reflection

The healed man knew very little theology. Pressed by the experts, he simply said, one thing I know, that though I was blind, now I see. His witness held not because he could win the argument but because he could not deny what Jesus had done in him. We often think we must have every answer before we can speak. Sometimes the most honest and powerful testimony is the plain account of what we have seen.
