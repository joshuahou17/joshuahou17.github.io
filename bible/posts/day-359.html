Day 359 of 365

2 Peter; Jude

Contending for the Faith Against Corrupt Teachers

Read 2 Peter in the NIV → Read Jude in the NIV →

Context

These two short letters are read together because they share remarkable overlap in content and concern. Second Peter, written as the apostle anticipates his own death, urges believers to make every effort to grow in faith and virtue, grounds their confidence in the eyewitness testimony of the apostles and the prophetic word, and then issues a stern warning against false teachers who exploit the church with greed and sensuality while denying the Lord who bought them.

Jude, the brother of James, set out to write about their common salvation but felt compelled instead to urge his readers to contend for the faith once delivered to the saints, because certain people had crept in unnoticed, perverting grace into license. Both letters reach into Israel's history and Jewish tradition for examples of judgment, the fallen angels, the flood, Sodom and Gomorrah, and figures like Balaam, Cain, and Korah, to show that God has always opposed the proud and the rebellious. Both also end with hope: Peter looks to the day of the Lord and new heavens and a new earth where righteousness dwells, and Jude closes with a soaring doxology to the God who is able to keep his people from stumbling.

Key themes

The threat of corruption from within. The danger in both letters is not outside persecution but teachers who have crept into the community, denying the truth in doctrine and especially in immoral conduct, turning the freedom of grace into an excuse for sin.

God's certain judgment. Both writers stack up examples from the past, the flood, Sodom, rebellious angels, to argue that the God who judged then will judge again, so the scoffers who say nothing ever changes are gravely mistaken about the patient delay of the day of the Lord.

Growth, memory, and perseverance. Against false teaching the answer is not merely defense but growth: adding to faith virtue, knowledge, self-control, and love, remembering the apostles' predictions, building yourselves up in faith, and trusting the God who is able to keep you from falling.

Takeaways

  • Spiritual danger often comes from within the community, dressed as freedom or enlightenment, so discernment about teaching and especially about character is vital.
  • God's apparent delay in judgment is patience, not indifference; he is giving room for repentance, but the day of reckoning is certain.
  • The best defense against error is active growth and dependence on God: keep maturing in faith and love, and trust the One who is able to keep you from stumbling.

Connections

Both letters mine earlier readings for warnings: the flood of Genesis 7, the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah in Genesis 19, the angels who left their proper place, Balaam's greed from Numbers 22 to 24, and Korah's rebellion in Numbers 16. Peter's vision of the heavens passing away and new heavens and a new earth where righteousness dwells echoes Isaiah 65 and looks ahead to Revelation's climax. Jude's reminder that the Lord saved a people out of Egypt and then destroyed those who did not believe recalls the wilderness judgment that Hebrews also invoked in the previous days' readings.

Genesis 19:24-25 → Numbers 16:31-35 → Numbers 22:5-7 → Isaiah 65:17 → Hebrews 3:16-19 →

Reflection

It is unsettling that both Peter and Jude locate the gravest danger not in hostile outsiders but in voices within the church that make sin sound like liberty. That calls for honest discernment, weighing teaching not only by its claims but by the lives it produces. Yet neither writer leaves us merely anxious. Peter says God's delay is mercy, room for repentance, and asks what kind of people we ought to be while we wait. Jude ends not with fear but with worship of the God who is able to keep you from stumbling and present you blameless with great joy. Build yourself up in the faith today, and entrust your perseverance to the One strong enough to hold you.
