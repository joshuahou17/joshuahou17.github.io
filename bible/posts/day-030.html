Day 30 of 365

Exodus 1-3

A New King, a Saved Baby, and a Burning Bush

Read Exodus 1-3 in the NIV →

Context

Exodus opens by bridging from Genesis: the seventy who came down with Jacob have become a vast people, but a new king who did not know Joseph fears them and enslaves them, finally ordering the murder of newborn Hebrew boys. The Hebrew midwives quietly defy him, and a Levite mother hides her son, then places him in a basket on the Nile, where Pharaoh's own daughter draws him out and names him Moses.

Grown and enraged at injustice, Moses kills an Egyptian beating a Hebrew, is found out, and flees to Midian, where he marries and tends flocks. Exodus 3 is the pivot of the book: at Horeb a bush blazes without burning up, and God calls Moses to deliver Israel. When Moses asks God's name, the reply is the enigmatic I AM WHO I AM, the verb of being from which the covenant name Yahweh derives. God promises to be with him and to bring the people to a land flowing with milk and honey.

Key themes

God remembers his covenant. Israel's groaning rises to God, who remembers his promise to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. The deliverance is rooted in the patriarchal covenant of earlier readings, not in Israel's merit.

Quiet defiance and hidden providence. Midwives, a mother, a sister, and a princess, all women, preserve the deliverer's life against the empire's decree, showing God working through humble and unexpected acts of courage.

The God who is and who will be. The unconsumed bush and the name I AM reveal a God self-existent and free, whose presence sustains rather than destroys, and who defines himself by being with his people.

Takeaways

  • God hears the cry of the oppressed and acts out of covenant faithfulness, not because the sufferers have earned rescue.
  • Ordinary acts of courage and defiance, like those of the midwives, can be the means God uses to change history.
  • God's self-revelation as I AM grounds our trust in his unchanging presence rather than in our circumstances.

Connections

The multiplication of Israel fulfills the promise to Jacob in the day 28 reading that God would make him a great nation in Egypt, and God's remembering of the covenant reaches all the way back to Genesis 15. The burning bush at Horeb anticipates Israel's return to that same mountain to receive the law. God's name I AM is taken up by Jesus in John's Gospel, who applies the divine self-designation to himself. Moses, a deliverer rejected and then sent, echoes Joseph and prefigures Christ.

Reflection

God met Moses not in a temple but in the wilderness, in a bush, while he was simply doing his daily work, and called him by name into something far larger. Are you attentive to the possibility that God might be present in the ordinary ground beneath your feet right now? And when, like Moses, you feel unqualified for what you are called to, can you rest in the only credential he offered, I will be with you?
