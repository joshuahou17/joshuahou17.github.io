Matthew 28; Mark 16

Matthew 28; Mark 16: He Is Risen

Read Matthew 28 in the NIV → Read Mark 16 in the NIV →

Context

Matthew 28 and Mark 16 open on the first day of the week, women coming to the tomb at dawn and finding the stone rolled away. Both record an angelic messenger declaring that the crucified one has risen. Matthew adds the guard at the tomb, the earthquake, and the chief priests' bribe to spread a cover story, details that engage directly with the earliest counterclaims about the resurrection.

Mark's Gospel ends famously at verse 8, with the women fleeing in trembling and astonishment, saying nothing because they were afraid. The longer ending found in many manuscripts is widely judged a later addition. This abrupt close leaves the reader, like the women, standing before an empty tomb and an unfinished sentence, having to decide what to do with the news. Matthew, by contrast, ends with the risen Jesus commissioning his disciples on a mountain in Galilee.

Key themes

The fact of the empty tomb. The witnesses are women, whose testimony carried little legal weight in that culture, a detail scholars often note as a mark of authenticity; no one inventing the story would have chosen them. The angel does not argue but points: he is not here, he is risen, come see the place.

Fear before joy. Both Gospels register terror as the first response to resurrection. Mark ends there. The empty tomb is not immediately comforting; it overturns the settled order of death and demands a response the witnesses are barely able to give.

Authority and commission. Matthew's risen Jesus claims all authority in heaven and on earth and sends the disciples to all nations, baptizing and teaching, with the promise that he is with them always. The mission of the church is grounded in the authority of the risen King.

Takeaways

  • The resurrection is announced as event and fact, anchored to a specific empty tomb and named witnesses, not as a metaphor.
  • The first honest response to resurrection is awe and even fear; Mark refuses to tidy it into easy comfort.
  • The risen Jesus commissions his followers to all nations with his abiding presence, turning the empty tomb into a sending.

Connections

Jesus' claim to all authority echoes the Son of Man of Daniel 7, given dominion over all peoples, fulfilling the title Jesus used throughout the Gospels. The command to disciple all nations reaches back to God's promise to Abraham that in him all the families of the earth would be blessed. And the promise, I am with you always, recalls the name Immanuel, God with us, from Matthew's opening chapters, so the Gospel ends where it began, on the assurance of God's presence.

Reflection

Mark's broken-off ending is, in its way, an invitation. The story stops mid-stride and turns to the reader: now what will you do? The women's fear is honest, because resurrection is not a gentle idea but a rupture in the order we have made our peace with. Matthew answers Mark's silence with a sending and a promise. The empty tomb does not leave us standing in the garden; it puts us on the road to the nations, accompanied.
