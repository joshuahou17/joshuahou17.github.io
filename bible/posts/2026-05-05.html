Judges 1-6

When Strength Becomes Weakness: Israel's Spiral from Victory to Defeat

Context: From Joshua's Legacy to Israel's Crisis

These six chapters serve as both bridge and precipice in Israel's history. Following Joshua's death, the nation attempts to complete the conquest of Canaan (chapter 1), but their incomplete obedience becomes the seed of future disaster. The book covers the time between the conquest under Joshua until the rise of the monarchy, characterized by political disorder and violence where "everyone did what was right in his own eyes." The opening chapters establish the theological framework—the introduction and setting before Gideon's debut—while chapters 4-5 showcase Israel's previous victory under Deborah and the subsequent "forty years of rest" that has now ended with chapter 6's opening phrase: "Then the children of Israel did evil in the sight of the LORD."

The Anatomy of Spiritual Decline

The theological architecture of Judges emerges clearly in these chapters through what scholars call the "Judges Cycle." Israel goes through a cycle of (1) committing idolatry, (2) God handing them over to oppression, (3) crying out to God for deliverance, and (4) God raising up a judge to deliver them. This cycle is introduced in 2:6-3:6 and is then carried through the six judges in 3:7-16:31. Yet this is no mere literary device—it's the relational dynamic between God and the children of Israel. Not unlike the parent-teenager relationship, Israel's relationship with Yahweh is fraught with tension, affection, judgment, pleas for rescue. Yahweh turns out to be a parent yearning for relationship—even if it is a contested one.

Chapter 2 provides the theological key to understanding this pattern. Following the death of Joshua and his generation, a new generation arises that is unfamiliar with the miraculous deeds God performed for Israel. This generational disconnect leads to a decline in faithfulness and obedience. The chapter details Israel's descent into idolatry, worshiping Baals and Ashtaroths. This apostasy provokes God's anger, resulting in their subjugation by surrounding nations as divine punishment. Yet, in His mercy, God raises up judges to deliver them from their oppressors.

God's Unlikely Choice: Gideon's Calling

The Midianite oppression described in chapter 6 represents a devastating new low for Israel. The Amalekites and other tribes that lived in the Arabian Desert east of Canaan joined the Midianites in their raids. These desert-dwellers were the "sons of the east." The raids extended all the way to Gaza on the Mediterranean coast, far into Israel. After seven years of these locust-like devastating raids, the Israelites were at their wits end and called out to Yahweh in their misery. Because of the Midianites, the children of Israel made for themselves dens, caves, and strongholds in the mountains. Whenever Israel had sown, Midianites would come up and destroy the produce of the earth as far as Gaza, leaving no sustenance for Israel.

Into this desperate situation, God calls the most unlikely candidate. Gideon is the youngest in his family and a most unlikely warrior. Gideon's own father sanctions Baal-Asherah worship. This text holds yet another example of God's use of unlikely persons to bring about his purposes. Likely, this is the reason why the author of Hebrews celebrates Gideon in the Hall of Faith among the others who became heroes after beginning as "zeroes." We find him threshing wheat in a winepress—both difficult and humiliating. Wheat was threshed in open spaces, typically on a hilltop so the breeze could blow away the chaff. Wheat was not normally threshed in a sunken place like a winepress.

Cross-References: Echoes of Earlier Readings

Today's passage resonates powerfully with previous readings. The incomplete conquest of chapter 1 recalls Joshua's warnings in our Day 24 reading (Joshua 16-20), where the cities of refuge demonstrated both God's justice and mercy. The generational failure described in Judges 2 mirrors the tragic pattern we saw in Genesis 16-19 (Day 30), where human impatience with divine timing led to long-term consequences. Most significantly, Gideon's reluctant calling echoes Moses' protestations in the wilderness, while his request for signs parallels Abraham's interaction with God in Genesis 15.

The theological themes connect directly to Paul's teaching in Romans 5-6 (Day 15) about the cosmic battle between sin and righteousness. The cycle testifies to God's grace. What is undeserved is that God would listen to their cries over and over and keep supplying deliverers for them. The book of Judges is a book of God's grace demonstrated over and over, his longsuffering and patience with his wayward people. God treats us, not on the basis of what we deserve, but with deliverance.

Historical and Cultural Background

The Midianites descended from Abraham through Keturah (Genesis 25:2), making this oppression particularly painful—family turned against family. The Midianites were the descendants of Midian and therefore children of Abraham. They settled in "the land of the east." Most scholars believe the land of Midian was officially on both sides of the Gulf of Aqaba, although the Midianites showed nomadic tendencies. Their use of camels in warfare represented military innovation that gave them devastating advantage over Israel's traditional infantry tactics.

The period of the judges lasted approximately 300-350 years, and God sent an unnamed prophet to explain the reason for their discipline. They had again disobeyed the Lord. Yet now the prophet God sent did not deliver the people, but chastened them. This is another subtle sign that things were getting worse in Israel.

Reflection: The Grace That Pursues Us

These chapters confront us with an uncomfortable truth: spiritual victory can become the seedbed of spiritual complacency. Israel's partial obedience in chapter 1—their decision to put some Canaanites "to tribute" rather than driving them out completely—seemed pragmatic, even merciful. Yet this compromise became the pathway to apostasy.

How often do we, like Israel, negotiate with God's commands? We drive out the "big" sins while allowing seemingly smaller compromises to remain. But Judges reminds us that any accommodations that you make with the things of the flesh will become thorns and snares in your life as well.

Yet the deeper truth is God's relentless pursuit of His wayward people. The God of the bible works through emptiness and weakness to bring about creation and rescue. Such a strategy should reframe our understanding of strength, faith, and power. The God of the scriptures uses weakness, doubt, and the powerless to bring about his purposes. When we find ourselves, like Gideon, hiding in the winepress of our fears and inadequacies, God still calls us "mighty warrior." His strength is perfected in our weakness, and His grace is sufficient for our deepest failures.
