Day 91 of 365

Judges 6-7

Too Many to Boast

Read Judges 6-7 in the NIV →

Context

The Midianite oppression is the worst yet. For seven years raiders sweep in at harvest like locusts, stripping the land bare and driving Israel into caves. When the angel of the Lord finds Gideon, he is threshing wheat inside a winepress, hiding the grain from the enemy. The greeting is almost ironic: mighty man of valor, spoken to a frightened farmer from the weakest clan in Manasseh who calls himself the least in his father's house.

Gideon is a study in hesitant faith. He asks for a sign and watches fire consume his offering. Commanded to tear down his father's altar to Baal, he does it by night because he is afraid. Then, even after the Spirit of the Lord clothes him and the tribes rally, he twice lays out a wool fleece, asking God to make it wet while the ground stays dry, then dry while the ground is wet. God grants both, accommodating a man who keeps needing one more proof.

Chapter 7 reverses the usual math of war. God says Gideon has too many soldiers, lest Israel boast that its own hand saved it. The fearful are sent home; twenty-two thousand leave. Of the rest, only three hundred who lap water from their hands are kept. With trumpets, jars, and torches, and a panicked enemy turning their swords on each other, the three hundred rout a host described as countless.

Key themes

Lest Israel boast. This is the explicit hinge of the story. God deliberately weakens Gideon's force so that no one can credit human strength for the rescue. The reduction is not a tactical accident but a theological statement: the glory must belong to God alone.

Patience with weak faith. Gideon is timid, demanding, and slow to trust, asking for sign after sign. Rather than rebuking him, God meets each request, and even sends Gideon to overhear an enemy's dream when his courage falters. Grace here is gentle with the fearful.

The unlikely deliverer. God chooses the smallest clan and the least in the household, fitting the book's pattern of saving through people the world counts as nothing. The winepress where Gideon hides becomes the place where his call begins.

The deepening spiral. Even the rescue carries warning signs. Gideon needs more reassurance than earlier judges, and the seeds of his later compromise are already visible in his fear and his bargaining, hinting that Israel's leaders, like Israel itself, are sliding downward.

Takeaways

  • God shrank the army on purpose so that victory could only be read as his gift, guarding Israel from the deadly pride of self-made salvation.
  • Weak and wavering faith is not disqualifying; God patiently met Gideon's fleeces and fears, drawing a hesitant man into courage.
  • The story exposes a recurring temptation: to take credit for what God alone accomplished, claiming my own hand has saved me.

Connections

Gideon's call belongs to a familiar biblical shape. Like Moses at the burning bush, read in Exodus, he protests his smallness and asks for signs, and like Moses he is told that the decisive factor is God's presence, not his resume. The pattern of the reluctant called one runs through Scripture.

The deliberate weakening of Gideon's army rhymes with the larger lesson of the conquest under Joshua, where walls fell at a shout and the land was a gift rather than a conquest earned. It looks forward to David refusing Saul's armor and to the prophetic word that victory comes not by might nor by power but by God's Spirit.

Above all, lest Israel boast anticipates Paul's gospel logic that God chose the weak and lowly things, so that no human being might boast before him. The God who saves with three hundred and a handful of clay jars is the God who saves through a cross the world counts as weakness.

Reflection

There is something tender about a God who keeps answering a frightened man's tests. Gideon hides, hesitates, tears down the altar in the dark, and asks for the fleece not once but twice. Most of us would have given up on him. God did not. If your faith feels thin and you find yourself asking for one more sign, take heart that the Lord has been patient with such people before.

But notice the discomfort God deliberately created. He took away Gideon's advantages until winning was humanly impossible. We tend to want a margin of safety, a backup, something we can point to and say we managed it ourselves. God often strips that away, not to abandon us but to make sure we know who actually carried us through.

The line lest Israel boast is worth sitting with. Even good outcomes can quietly become trophies of self-reliance. The same heart that cries out to God in trouble can, once rescued, murmur my own power delivered me. Gideon's three hundred are a standing rebuke to that drift, and an invitation to give the glory back where it belongs.
