2 Chronicles 1; Psalms 72

Ask, and It Will Be Given

Read 2 Chronicles 1 in the NIV → Read Psalms 72 in the NIV →

Context

Second Chronicles 1 opens the Chronicler's account of Solomon's reign, picking up right where 1 Kings 3 left off. Solomon gathers all Israel's leaders and goes to the high place at Gibeon, where the tabernacle Moses had made still stood with its bronze altar. There he offers a thousand burnt offerings, and that night God appears and says, "Ask what I shall give you." The Chronicler is less interested in the bare facts of Solomon's reign than in the principle behind it: a king who begins by seeking the LORD.

Psalm 72 is one of the Psalter's royal psalms, carrying the title "of Solomon." It is a prayer for the king, asking that God give him righteousness so that he judges the people justly and defends the poor. Its hopes swell beyond any single reign into a vision of dominion from sea to sea and tribute from distant kings.

The psalm also marks a structural seam. Its closing doxology rounds off Book II of the Psalter, and the postscript, "The prayers of David son of Jesse are ended," signals an editorial boundary in the collection of David's psalms.

Key themes

Wisdom over wealth. Offered anything he wants, Solomon asks for wisdom and knowledge to govern God's people well. He does not request riches, fame, the death of his enemies, or long life. God grants the wisdom and then adds the wealth and honor he did not ask for, a pattern of grace that rewards rightly ordered desire.

The king as servant of the poor. Psalm 72 measures a good reign not by splendor but by justice for the weak. The petition is that the king defend the cause of the poor, deliver the needy, and crush the oppressor. Royal power is judged by what it does for those who have none.

A throne built on worship. Solomon's first royal act is sacrifice at Gibeon. The Chronicler frames the whole reign as beginning with seeking God rather than securing a throne, so that strength flows from worship and not the reverse.

Beyond the human king. The psalm's idealism outruns Solomon or any son of David. Its prayer for an everlasting, worldwide, peace-giving reign points past the throne in Jerusalem toward the Messiah, the King whose rule actually delivers what these words ask.

Takeaways

  • What we ask God for reveals what we love most; Solomon's request for wisdom to serve others, not for personal gain, is what God delighted to honor.
  • Scripture measures good leadership by its care for the poor and the powerless, not by wealth, reach, or reputation.
  • Psalm 72 prays for a king so just and enduring that no mere human could fulfill it, teaching us to read it as a longing finally answered in Christ.

Connections

The Gibeon scene is the Chronicler's retelling of the dream in 1 Kings 3, which the plan read just days ago in the Solomon material from 1 Kings 1-4. Both accounts turn on the same moment: a young king offered everything who asks only for a discerning heart to govern. Jesus distills the same logic when he tells his hearers to seek first the kingdom of God, promising that the other things will be added.

Psalm 72 stands in the line of the messianic king that runs through Scripture. Its vision of a righteous, peace-bringing ruler echoes Isaiah's prophecy of the shoot from Jesse's stump, and its picture of distant kings bringing gold and tribute is taken up when the magi come to the child in Bethlehem with their gifts. The plan's long arc through David's life and the Davidic covenant in 2 Samuel finds here a prayer for the kind of son David's throne was always promised.

Reflection

God's question to Solomon is also put, in quieter ways, to us: what do you most want? Our honest answer exposes the shape of our hearts. Solomon's instinct was to ask for what he needed to serve others well, and that request unlocked far more than he sought.

Psalm 72 then trains our hope. No earthly leader, not even Solomon at his wisest, ever fully ruled with this kind of justice for the poor and peace for the world. The prayer leans forward, and only one King has answered it. To pray Psalm 72 is to ask for the reign of Christ and to live, now, under the values it praises.
