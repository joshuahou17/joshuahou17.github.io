Acts 24-26

On Trial Before Kings

Read Acts 24-26 in the NIV →

Context

Paul has spent two years in Roman custody in Caesarea, the provincial capital on the Mediterranean coast. These three hearings unfold before a succession of Roman authorities. Felix, the governor, is a freedman with a checkered record who keeps Paul imprisoned partly hoping for a bribe and partly to please the Jewish leaders. His successor Festus, by most accounts a more capable administrator, inherits the case and quickly faces pressure from Jerusalem to send Paul back, where an ambush awaits.

It is to escape that trap that Paul, a Roman citizen, exercises his right to appeal directly to Caesar. The appeal is the legal hinge of the whole section: it commits Rome to transporting Paul to the imperial court and explains how the gospel reaches the empire's center. King Agrippa II, the last of the Herodian dynasty and an expert in Jewish affairs, is brought in to help Festus draft charges he cannot otherwise articulate, since every authority concedes Paul has done nothing deserving death.

Key themes

Innocence repeatedly confirmed. Felix, Festus, and Agrippa each effectively clear Paul, and Agrippa observes that he could have been freed had he not appealed to Caesar. Luke piles up these verdicts to show that the Christian movement is no threat to Rome and that Paul suffers not for crimes but for the resurrection. The pattern mirrors the Roman officials who found no fault in Jesus.

Witness in the seat of power. Paul treats each trial not as a fight for acquittal but as an open door. Before Felix he reasons about righteousness, self-control, and the coming judgment until the governor trembles. Before Agrippa he tells his own story and turns the courtroom into a pulpit. The defendant becomes the evangelist, fulfilling the promise that he would carry the name before kings.

A life rerouted by grace. In his fullest retelling of the Damascus road, Paul recounts the risen Christ asking why he persecutes him and adding that it is hard to kick against the goads. The persecutor is commissioned as the apostle to the Gentiles. Paul insists he was not disobedient to the heavenly vision, framing his whole ministry as obedience to a sovereign call he could not resist.

Takeaways

  • Three Roman authorities found no crime in Paul, underscoring that he suffered for the hope of the resurrection, not for any wrongdoing.
  • Paul used each trial as an opportunity to bear witness, preaching to governors and a king rather than merely defending himself.
  • Agrippa's almost shows that clear truth and powerful testimony can still be resisted when fear of man and worldly loss hold the heart back.

Connections

The chain of authorities declaring Paul innocent deliberately recalls Pilate and Herod finding no guilt in Jesus in Luke's Gospel, binding Paul's suffering to his Lord's. Paul's bold preaching to rulers fulfills the commission spoken at his conversion, that he was a chosen instrument to carry Christ's name before Gentiles and kings, words first recorded earlier in Acts. His insistence that he proclaims nothing beyond what Moses and the Prophets said would come ties the gospel back to the promises running from Genesis through the Prophets, especially the suffering and rising of the Messiah. And the image of kicking against the goads, futile resistance to God's will, echoes the long biblical record of God overruling human rebellion to accomplish his purposes, as with Jonah fleeing his call or Joseph's brothers meaning evil that God meant for good.

Reflection

Agrippa's reply, that in such a short time Paul would persuade him to become a Christian, is one of Scripture's saddest near misses. He stood close enough to the truth to feel its pull and still turned away, likely unwilling to surrender his position and reputation. Paul, by contrast, would rather be in chains and free in Christ than enthroned and lost. When the truth presses on you and the cost of yielding feels high, what is the almost you are tempted to settle for, and what would it take to move from almost to entirely?
