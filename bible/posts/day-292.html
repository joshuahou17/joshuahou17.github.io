Day 292 of 365

Matthew 15; Mark 7

What Comes Out of the Heart

Read Matthew 15 in the NIV → Read Mark 7 in the NIV →

Context

Read Matthew 15 and Mark 7 together as one scene that unfolds in two movements. Scribes and Pharisees travel up from Jerusalem to scrutinize Jesus, and the flashpoint is ritual hand-washing, part of what they call the tradition of the elders. This was not the hygiene we think of today but a body of oral rules built up around the written Law to fence it in. Jesus answers their charge with a counter-charge, exposing how one such tradition, Corban, let a person declare resources dedicated to God and thereby dodge the plain command to honor and support aging parents.

Mark, writing for a non-Jewish audience, pauses to explain the washing customs and adds the editorial note that Jesus declared all foods clean. From there both Gospels move north and west, out of Israel proper, into the region of Tyre and Sidon, where Jesus meets a Gentile woman whose daughter is tormented. The geography matters: having just taught that defilement is internal, Jesus steps among people the tradition counted unclean.

Key themes

Tradition versus the commandment of God. Jesus does not reject reverence or order; he rejects rules that quietly cancel God's own word. The Corban loophole is his case study: a pious-sounding vow used to evade the duty to care for parents. Human tradition becomes dangerous precisely when it lets us feel devout while disobeying.

Defilement begins in the heart. Nothing entering from outside makes a person unclean. The real source of corruption is the heart, out of which come evil thoughts, sexual sin, theft, murder, deceit, and pride. This reframing is seismic: holiness cannot be secured by scrubbing the outside, because the problem is inside.

Faith that crosses every boundary. The Canaanite woman has no claim by birth, yet she presses in, accepting Jesus' hard image of children and dogs and turning it into an appeal for crumbs. Jesus calls her faith great, the same word he elsewhere reserves for a Roman centurion, and her daughter is healed at a distance.

Takeaways

  • Devotion measured only by external observance can mask a heart that is far from God; Jesus locates true purity and true defilement within.
  • No tradition, however venerable, is allowed to override the clear commands of God, especially the obligation to love and care for one's own family.
  • Faith that refuses to give up, even when met with apparent silence or testing, is honored by Jesus regardless of the seeker's background.

Connections

Jesus quotes Isaiah's indictment of worship that honors God with the lips while the heart is far away, tying the Pharisees directly to a prophet the earlier reading already introduced. The Corban dispute reaches back to the command in Exodus and Deuteronomy to honor father and mother, one of the Ten Words given at Sinai. The list of sins flowing from the heart echoes the diagnosis of human corruption in Genesis, where every intention of the heart was evil, and anticipates the new-covenant promise in Jeremiah of a heart transformed from within. The Canaanite woman foreshadows the gathering of the nations promised to Abraham and the wide welcome later worked out in Acts.

Reflection

It is easier to wash hands than to examine a heart, and easier to keep a rule than to keep a relationship. Where have I let a religious habit, even a good one, become a cover for failing someone I am actually called to love? And when God seems slow to answer, will I, like the Canaanite woman, keep coming back, certain that even his crumbs are enough?
