Day 239 of 365

Ezekiel 1-4

The Glory Comes to the River

Read Ezekiel 1-4 in the NIV →

Context

Ezekiel was a priest deported to Babylon with the first wave of exiles around 597 BC, before Jerusalem's final fall. He prophesied among fellow exiles settled along the Kebar canal, an irrigation channel near the city of Nippur. The book opens by dating his call to the fifth year of that exile, away from the temple where, as a priest, he would have expected to serve.

Chapter 1 records his inaugural vision: a storm out of the north, four living creatures each with four faces and four wings, wheels within wheels full of eyes, and above them a throne with a figure gleaming like fire and surrounded by a rainbow, the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the Lord. Chapters 2 and 3 are his commission. God addresses him as son of man, hands him a scroll written with lament and woe, and tells him to eat it. He is appointed a watchman to a rebellious house, warned that he is accountable for sounding the alarm whether or not anyone listens. Chapter 4 begins a series of sign-acts dramatizing the coming siege of Jerusalem.

Key themes

God is not confined to Jerusalem. The most startling fact of the vision is its location: the glory of God appears in Babylon, among defeated exiles, far from the temple. The throne is mobile, riding a chariot that can go in any direction, declaring that the God of Israel is present even in the land of captivity.

Transcendence and reverence. Ezekiel piles up words like appearance and likeness because the vision strains language. He cannot describe God directly, only what God resembled. The response to such glory is to fall on his face, a reminder that awe precedes service.

Eating the word. Before Ezekiel can speak God's message he must internalize it, taking the scroll into himself until it is sweet as honey in his mouth. The prophet does not invent his words; he ingests them and is changed by them.

The watchman's burden. Ezekiel is made responsible to warn, and his accountability does not depend on the people's response. Faithfulness, not results, is the measure of his calling.

Takeaways

  • God met his people in exile, which means there is no place so far from home or so marked by failure that his presence cannot reach it.
  • Speaking for God begins with taking his word inside until it becomes part of you. There is no shortcut around being shaped by the message you carry.
  • The watchman is responsible to sound the warning faithfully, not to control how others respond. That distinction frees us from measuring obedience by outcomes.

Connections

The reading plan has just walked through the fall of Jerusalem in Jeremiah and the grief of Lamentations; Ezekiel speaks to the same catastrophe from the other side, among those already carried away. His throne-chariot vision recalls the cherubim over the ark and the glory that filled the tabernacle in Exodus and Solomon's temple, except now that glory has come out to the exiles. The eating of the scroll mirrors Jeremiah's testimony that he found God's words and ate them. Centuries later John eats a little scroll in Revelation, sweet then bitter, and the four faces of the living creatures reappear around God's throne. The title son of man, used here for a mortal man under God's commission, takes on fuller weight when Jesus claims it for himself.

Reflection

Ezekiel's first response to glory was to fall on his face, and only then was he raised up and sent. We often want the sending without the trembling. Where in your life have you assumed God is absent because the circumstances look like defeat, when in fact he may be present in the very place of exile? And consider the scroll: is God's word something you merely read, or something you take in until it becomes part of who you are? Faithfulness may mean ingesting hard truths before you ever speak them.
