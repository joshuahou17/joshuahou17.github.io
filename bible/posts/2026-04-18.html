Gospels Day 21 of 364 • Week 3 • April 18, 2026

Matthew 5-7

The Kingdom Constitution: Jesus Inaugurates the New Covenant

Context: The King's First Discourse

Matthew places the Sermon on the Mount strategically as the first of five major discourses that structure his Gospel, following Jesus' calling of disciples and the crowds gathering to hear Him. This positioning is no accident—the Sermon on the Mount is the most complete record we have of Jesus' announcement about the arrival of God's Kingdom. Coming directly after Jesus' temptation victory and initial ministry successes in Matthew 3-4, this sermon answers the crucial question: what does life look like when God's Kingdom breaks into human history?

The majestic portrayal of Jesus as lawgiver on a mountaintop at the beginning of the Sermon on the Mount are an indication that Matthew portrays Jesus as a New Moses. Yet this new Moses doesn't simply repeat the old law—he fulfills it and reveals its deeper spiritual reality. The crowds' astonishment at Jesus teaching "as one having authority, and not as the scribes" demonstrates both what He said and how He said it represented something entirely unprecedented.

Key Themes: The Upside-Down Kingdom

The sermon's three chapters develop a coherent vision of kingdom life through several interconnected themes. First, the reversal of worldly values dominates from the opening Beatitudes. Jesus says to look where people are poor, where they feel crushed and defeated—God's Kingdom is beginning with those very people. This isn't mere sentiment; blessed are those who realize they are spiritually bankrupt, for this realization turns them to God, without whom they cannot fulfill what they are created to do and be.

Second, the transformation of religious practice runs throughout chapters 5-6. Jesus now explains the internal reality behind the righteousness that makes us salt and light in the world by expounding the Law. The righteousness of the Jewish religious elite is purely external. The six antitheses ("You have heard it said...but I say to you") don't abolish the Torah but reveal its spiritual intent, moving from external compliance to heart transformation.

Third, the priority of the kingdom anchors chapter 6's teaching on money, worry, and prayer. In God's kingdom, receiving our daily bread is a certainty, but in our world marred by sin, daily sustenance is questionable...thus Jesus' first word about wealth or daily provision is this petition, "Give us this day our daily bread". The kingdom perspective reframes all earthly anxieties.

Cross-References: Echoing Previous Readings

This sermon resonates powerfully with our earlier readings. The Beatitudes' vision of blessing recalls God's covenant promises to Abraham in Genesis, while their focus on the "kingdom of heaven" connects to Isaiah 7-11's messianic prophecies about the coming reign of David's son. Like the Psalms 3-5 pattern of moving from crisis to trust, the Sermon begins with the desperate ("poor in spirit") and promises divine blessing.

The sermon's ethical demands echo Job's struggle with authentic righteousness versus superficial religion—Jesus confronts the same tension Job faced between external correctness and internal integrity. Most significantly, Romans 5-6's theology of transformation from "slavery to sin to slavery to righteousness" finds practical expression in Jesus' call for heart-level obedience rather than mere rule-following.

Historical and Cultural Background

In Jesus' day, God's Kingdom is nowhere to be seen. In fact, Israel in the first century is suffering under the thumb of Roman oppressors...many religious leaders, like the scribes and the Pharisees, thought it wasn't coming because Israel wasn't being faithful enough to the Torah. Into this context of political oppression and religious legalism, Jesus proclaims a kingdom that transcends both.

Matthew's Jewish-Christian audience would have immediately recognized the Moses typology, but they would also have been shocked by Jesus' authority claims. The Sermon on the Mount does not deal with salvation as such, but it lays out for the disciple and the potential disciple how regarding Jesus as King translates into ethics and daily living. This wasn't abstract theology but urgent pastoral guidance for communities learning to live as kingdom citizens in hostile territory.

Reflection: The Beautiful Impossibility

The Sermon on the Mount confronts us with what Dietrich Bonhoeffer called "the beautiful impossibility" of Christian living. The following commandments are not meant to weigh anyone down, but they are meant to give life to all who are willing to live such a radical lifestyle. In other words, there is a promise of blessing for those who live like Jesus.

Yet this promise comes with sobering realism. The church in America has allowed folks to come in, sit down, call themselves Christians and completely ignore the beatitudes...The world has a right to question our faith when they see us fretting about our provision...when they hear us sing on Sunday of our love and devotion and trust in God the Father and then we live like we have NO IDEA WHO GOD IS from Monday through Saturday.

The sermon's closing parable of two builders demands a response: "What are you going to do with Jesus' words? You've now heard them. The question that remains is, 'Will you do them?'" This isn't about earning God's favor through moral effort, but about allowing the kingdom's transforming power to reshape every aspect of our lives—from our anger and lust to our money and worry. The Sermon on the Mount isn't a burden to bear but a vision to embrace, painting a picture of human flourishing under God's gracious reign.
