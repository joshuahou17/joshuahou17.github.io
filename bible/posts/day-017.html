Day 17 of 365

Genesis 16-18

The God Who Sees: Hagar, Circumcision, and Laughter

Read Genesis 16-18 in the NIV →

Context

Years pass and the promised child does not arrive. Sarai, following the custom of her day, gives her Egyptian slave Hagar to Abram so that a child might come through her. Hagar conceives, tensions flare, and the pregnant, mistreated woman flees into the desert.

There the angel of the LORD finds her and makes her promises that echo those given to Abram: her son Ishmael will become a great nation. Hagar responds by naming God El Roi, the God who sees me, the only person in Scripture to give God a name.

In chapter 17 God reaffirms the covenant, changing Abram to Abraham and Sarai to Sarah, and giving circumcision as its sign. In chapter 18 three visitors arrive; one is the LORD himself, who promises that Sarah will bear a son within the year. Sarah laughs in disbelief, and the chapter ends with Abraham boldly bargaining with God over the fate of Sodom.

Key themes

The God who sees the overlooked. Hagar is a foreign slave woman with no standing, yet God seeks her out in the wilderness and hears her distress. His care reaches past the edges of the chosen family to those it discarded.

Faith versus self-help. The Hagar episode is the fruit of trying to fulfill God's promise by human scheming. It produces real pain, even as God shows mercy within it. The promise will come God's way, in God's time.

Intercession. Abraham's haggling over Sodom reveals a God who welcomes honest pleading and a man learning to care about justice for others, not just his own household.

Takeaways

  • God sees and hears those the world overlooks, even people pushed to the margins of someone else's story.
  • Trying to force God's promises by our own scheming tends to create lasting wounds, even when God shows mercy in the mess.
  • God invites honest intercession; Abraham's bold bargaining over Sodom is met not with rebuke but with patience.

Connections

The decision to produce an heir through Hagar grows directly out of the long delay introduced in the previous reading, where God promised Abram countless descendants but no child had yet come. Hagar's wilderness encounter foreshadows a second rescue in a coming reading, when she and Ishmael are sent away and God again hears the boy's cries. Sarah's disbelieving laughter is later redeemed in the name Isaac, which means he laughs. Paul draws on Hagar and Sarah as a living illustration of two covenants in Galatians.

Genesis 16:13 → Genesis 17:5-7 → Genesis 18:14 → Genesis 18:23-25 → Galatians 4:22-26 →

Reflection

Hagar's name for God, the God who sees me, may be the tenderest moment in these chapters. She was used, mistreated, and running with nowhere to go, and God found her there and called her by name. Whatever wilderness you may be in, the conviction at the heart of this story is that you are not invisible to God. Where do you most need to believe that you are seen?
