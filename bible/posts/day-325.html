Acts 13-14

The Gospel Goes to the Nations

Read Acts 13-14 in the NIV →

Context

Acts 13 opens a new movement in Luke's narrative. Up to this point the gospel has spread outward from Jerusalem through Peter and the Twelve. Now the church at Antioch in Syria, a mixed congregation of Jews and Gentiles, becomes the sending base. While the prophets and teachers worship and fast, the Holy Spirit sets apart Barnabas and Saul for a specific work, and the church lays hands on them and lets them go.

The journey runs through Cyprus, where Saul (now called Paul) confronts the magician Elymas and the proconsul Sergius Paulus believes, then to the mainland of Asia Minor. At Pisidian Antioch Paul delivers his first recorded sermon, a sweeping rehearsal of Israel's history that lands on Jesus as the promised son of David, raised from the dead, through whom forgiveness comes by faith rather than by keeping the law of Moses.

When jealous opposition rises, Paul and Barnabas announce that they are turning to the Gentiles. The pattern repeats in Iconium, Lystra, and Derbe: bold preaching, signs, then resistance. At Lystra the crowd first hails them as gods, then stones Paul. The two return through the same cities to strengthen the disciples before reporting back to Antioch.

Key themes

The Spirit sends. The mission does not begin with a human strategy meeting but with the Holy Spirit speaking to a worshiping church. Barnabas and Saul are sent by the Spirit through the laying on of hands, framing the entire journey as God's initiative carried out by willing servants.

Promise fulfilled in Jesus. Paul's sermon at Pisidian Antioch reads Israel's whole story as pointing to the resurrected Christ. Forgiveness and justification come through believing in him, a freedom the law of Moses could never supply.

Turning to the Gentiles. When the synagogue largely rejects the word, Paul and Barnabas pivot to the Gentiles, citing Isaiah's call to be a light to the nations. The gospel proves to be for all peoples, not the property of one.

Suffering as the path. Adoration and violence sit side by side, from the worship at Lystra to the stoning that nearly kills Paul. The apostles tell the new disciples plainly that entering the kingdom of God means many hardships.

Takeaways

  • Mission flows from a worshiping, listening community that releases its best people rather than clutching them.
  • The gospel addresses every nation; rejection by some becomes an open door to others, never a dead end for God's purpose.
  • Faithful witness and real suffering travel together, and maturity means telling new believers this honestly from the start.

Connections

Paul's synagogue sermon echoes Stephen's speech in the earlier reading of Acts 7, both retelling Israel's history to show that God's saving plan culminates in Jesus. The turning to the Gentiles fulfills the risen Lord's commission in Acts 1 to be witnesses to the ends of the earth, and quotes Isaiah's servant who is given as a light for the nations. The insistence that justification comes by faith and not the law anticipates the argument Paul will press in Galatians, written to these very churches, and the gracious sending by the Spirit recalls Jesus breathing the Spirit on his disciples in John 20.

Reflection

The Antioch church teaches us that generosity, not self-protection, is the engine of mission; they gave away Barnabas and Saul because they were listening to God more than guarding their own strength. Where are you tempted to hold tightly to gifts or people God may be asking you to release?

Paul also models a refusal to be embittered by rejection. When one door closed he walked through another, trusting that God's word would find the people prepared for it. Consider where a closed door in your own life might be God redirecting rather than denying you.
