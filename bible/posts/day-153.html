Song of Solomon

The Greatest of Songs

Read Song of Solomon in the NIV →

Context

The Song of Songs is the one full-book day in this stretch of reading, so it asks to be taken in as a whole rather than parsed verse by verse. It belongs among the wisdom and poetry books of the Old Testament, and in the Hebrew canon it stands within the Megilloth, the five scrolls read at the great festivals. The Song is chanted at Passover, where the springtime imagery and the story of love between the lover and the beloved sit naturally beside the memory of the exodus.

The opening line gives the book its name and its character. "Song of Songs" is a Hebrew superlative, the same construction as "holy of holies" or "king of kings." It means the greatest of all songs. The attribution to Solomon places the book in his wisdom tradition; Solomon is named in the poems but never clearly speaks, and the strongest voice throughout is the woman's, often called the Shulammite or simply the beloved.

What follows is not a tidy narrative but a cycle of poems. A bridegroom and a bride speak to and about each other in lush, often startling metaphor, while the daughters of Jerusalem function as a chorus, prompting and witnessing. The effect is a spiraling intensity of longing, separation, search, and union.

Key themes

Desire as good. The Song treats romantic and physical love as a gift to be enjoyed, not a danger to be managed. The couple praise each other's bodies in unembarrassed detail. In a Bible that elsewhere warns sharply against misused sexuality, this book insists that desire rightly placed is part of what it means to be made well.

Love that waits. Three times the refrain returns: do not arouse or awaken love until it so desires. The warning sits inside the celebration. Love this strong is not to be forced, rushed, or counterfeited; it has its own proper time, and to seize it early is to cheapen it.

The strength of love. The book's climax in chapter 8 names love as strong as death and jealousy as fierce as the grave. Many waters cannot quench it, nor can it be bought. Here the poetry reaches for something beyond a single couple, describing a bond as serious and unyielding as the deepest forces of human life.

Mutual belonging. "I am my beloved's and my beloved is mine" captures the Song's vision of love as wholehearted, exclusive, and reciprocal. Neither party is consumed; both are claimed. The poems hold delight and commitment together rather than setting them against each other.

Takeaways

  • The Song reads first as exactly what it appears to be: a frank and joyful celebration of love and desire between a man and a woman, treated by Scripture as good.
  • Its name, the greatest of songs, and its threefold refrain about not awakening love too soon, signal that this love is to be honored and given its proper time.
  • A long Jewish and Christian tradition also hears the Song as a window onto a greater love, God for Israel and Christ for the church, without erasing its plain human meaning.

Connections

The Song reaches back to the garden of Genesis 2, where the man and woman become one flesh and are naked without shame; the poetry of mutual delight reads like that union restored and sung. It belongs with the wisdom literature already read, the hard questions of Job and the many Psalms of longing, and it follows naturally on the Solomon narrative just covered in 1 Kings 3-4 and 2 Chronicles 1, where Solomon is given wisdom and becomes the patron of Israel's songs and proverbs.

The allegorical tradition did not arise from nowhere. The prophets speak of God as Israel's husband in Hosea and Isaiah, where God rejoices over his people as a bridegroom over a bride. The New Testament takes up the same picture: Paul reads the one-flesh union of marriage as a sign of Christ and the church, and the story closes with the marriage supper of the Lamb in Revelation 19. Read in that company, the Song's celebration of covenant love becomes a small, earthly rehearsal of a far larger wedding.

Reflection

The Song lets us hold two true things at once. It honors the plain joy of human love, refusing to treat the body or desire as shameful, and it teaches us to give such love its proper time and weight rather than awakening it carelessly. At the same time, the longing it describes points past itself. If love like this is as strong as death, where does that strength finally come from, and who loves us with a love that many waters cannot quench?
