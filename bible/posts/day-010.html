Day 10 of 365

Job 24-28

Where Shall Wisdom Be Found?

Read Job 24-28 in the NIV →

Context

Today's reading spans the disintegration of the friends' case and a famous interlude. Chapter 24 is Job's catalogue of the injustices God appears to tolerate: the poor robbed, the orphan's donkey seized, murderers and adulterers working in the dark. Chapter 25 is Bildad's third speech, and it is strikingly brief, only a handful of verses asserting that no mortal can be pure before God. The third cycle is breaking down, with Zophar dropping out entirely and Bildad reduced to a fragment, a literary sign that the friends have run out of arguments.

Chapters 26-27 continue Job's reply, in which he affirms God's awesome power over creation while maintaining his own integrity to the last. Then chapter 28 stands apart as a serene meditation on wisdom. Humans can mine silver, gold and sapphires from the depths of the earth, the poem marvels, but wisdom itself cannot be dug up or bought. Twice it asks, 'Where shall wisdom be found?', and it closes with the answer: 'the fear of the Lord, that is wisdom.'

Key themes

The collapse of the debate. Bildad's truncated speech and Zophar's silence dramatize that human argument has exhausted itself. Neither side has explained Job's suffering, and the poetry itself signals the dead end.

Wisdom beyond human mining. Chapter 28 celebrates human technological brilliance only to insist that wisdom lies beyond it. We can tunnel into mountains, yet we cannot quarry the deep wisdom by which God orders the world.

The fear of the Lord. The poem's resolution is not a new piece of information but a posture: reverent trust in God and turning from evil. Wisdom is less something we acquire than Someone we revere.

Takeaways

  • The breakdown of the third cycle is intentional; the book lets human wisdom argue itself to silence before turning to a higher answer.
  • Job 28 reframes the whole debate by relocating wisdom away from clever explanation and toward humble reverence, anticipating God's own answer in the whirlwind.
  • Job maintains his integrity not as a claim to sinless perfection but as a refusal to confess crimes he did not commit simply to satisfy a theological system.

Connections

The refrain 'the fear of the LORD is wisdom' ties Job 28 to the heart of Israel's wisdom tradition in Proverbs, where the fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge, and to Ecclesiastes' concluding charge. The poem's praise of a wisdom present at creation, hidden in the deep, anticipates Proverbs 8, where Wisdom is personified at God's side in the making of the world, and the New Testament's identification of Christ as the wisdom of God. Job's awe before the Creator's power over the seas and the heavens recalls the ordering of creation in Genesis 1.

Job 28:28 → Proverbs 9:10 → Proverbs 8:22-31 → Ecclesiastes 12:13 → 1 Corinthians 1:24 →

Reflection

We are extraordinary at digging out what is hidden in the earth, yet wisdom eludes our tools and our wealth. The poem suggests it begins not with mastery but with reverence. Where might awe, rather than answers, be the wisdom you most need right now?
