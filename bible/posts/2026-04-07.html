Joshua 6-10

When Walls Fall and Nations Tremble

Context: The Conquest Begins

These chapters mark the pivotal transition from wandering to warfare. After forty years in the wilderness and the crossing of the Jordan, Israel stands ready to claim their inheritance. The storyline of Joshua is designed with four main movements, and chapters 6-10 represent the opening phase of conquest. Following the previous day's reading of Genesis 4-7, we move from humanity's earliest failures to God's faithfulness in fulfilling ancient promises made to Abraham.

Jericho was a medium-sized (Anchor Bible Commentary suggests it was about 8.5 acres), walled city just west of the Jordan River. It had a spring-fed water source, and thus, was an oasis for weary, thirsty travelers... According to the NIV Study Bible – Jericho "may have a main center for the worship of the moon-god." This first conquest was strategically significant—Jericho controlled the key entry point into Canaan's central highlands.

Divine Strategy Over Human Tactics

The most striking theme is God's unconventional warfare. In the Word Biblical Commentary this chapter is titled, "Faith Fells Fortifications." Rather than siege engines or military strategy, God commands a liturgical procession. Seven priests shall carry seven trumpets of rams' horns before the ark... Now the LORD gets more specific on the order of the march. It will be the priests carrying trumpets and preceding the Ark.

This profound silence was to be observed, to add to the gravity and solemnity of the procession; and on account of the surprising miracle that was to be wrought The silence for six days, followed by the great shout, demonstrates that when the Lord fights for us, it best becomes us to let all men see that the battle is not ours, but His. This principle continues in chapter 10, where the LORD God of Israel fought for Israel: This was obviously the most important factor. This illustrates the key aspect in the battle against spiritual enemies for believers.

Grace Amidst Judgment

The Rahab narrative reveals a crucial theological point: the story of Jericho shows that the book of Joshua is about more than a battle between Israelites and Canaanites. All who turn to Yahweh and follow his ways, whether Israelite or Canaanite, receive his blessings. But those who turn away from him, whether Israelite or Canaanite, face the consequences of their choice. Even in conquest narratives, God's mercy extends to those who demonstrate faith—a theme that connects to yesterday's reading about Noah finding grace in God's eyes despite universal judgment.

The alliance with Gibeon (chapter 9) further demonstrates this principle. Their deceptive approach reveals human frailty, yet the inhabitants of Gibeon had made peace with Israel and were within their land... Isn't it interesting that the Gibeonites were seeking peace, but their efforts backfired and brought war! When five Amorite kings attack Gibeon for making peace with Israel, God honors the covenant even when it was obtained through deception.

Historical and Cultural Context

Archaeological evidence provides intriguing correlations with the biblical account. Recent reexamination of these earlier findings, and a closer look at current evidence indicates that not only was there a city that fits the biblical chronology, but that its remains coincide with the biblical account Evidence includes clear evidence of fallen mud brick walls... evidence of a rapid defeat in springtime... evidence that the walls were leveled in such a way to provide access into the city... evidence that the city was not plundered by the invaders... evidence that the city was burned.

The famous miracle in chapter 10—Joshua's long day—demonstrates God's cosmic sovereignty. The significance of this event is not only in its immediate military implications but also in its broader theological implications. It serves as a reminder that the battle was not won by the strength or cunning of men but by the supernatural intervention of God. Whether understood as miraculous intervention in natural law or poetic description of God's perfect timing, the passage emphasizes divine control over creation itself.

Reflection: When God Fights For Us

These chapters challenge us to examine our own battles. There are still all kinds of walls out there that need to be broken down. People are extremely good at building walls around their heart. And those walls keep us from being the people God wants us to be. Like the Israelites circling Jericho in silence, we often must wait on God's timing rather than forcing our own solutions.

The passage invites us to consider: Where do we need to trust God's strategy over our wisdom? What walls in our lives require divine intervention rather than human effort? How does remembering God's faithfulness in past "impossibilities" encourage us in present challenges? Like Joshua's generation moving from promise to possession, we're called to step forward in faith, knowing that victories come through dependence on God, not human strength. Believers are called to resist compromise, remember God's faithfulness, and live as people set apart for Him.
