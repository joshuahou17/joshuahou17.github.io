Day 149 of 365

1 Kings 1-2; Psalms 37, 71, 94

A King Dies, a Kingdom Settles

Read 1 Kings 1-2 in the NIV → Read Psalms 37, 71, 94 in the NIV →

Context

First Kings opens in the shadows of David's final days. The king is bedridden and failing, and into that vacuum steps Adonijah, an older son who proclaims himself king without his father's blessing. The prophet Nathan and Bathsheba move quickly to remind David of his oath that Solomon would reign, and David acts, having Solomon anointed at Gihon. The premature coup collapses.

Chapter 2 records David's deathbed charge. He urges Solomon to be strong, to walk in God's ways, and to keep the law of Moses, then leaves practical instructions about unfinished accounts with Joab and Shimei. David dies after forty years as king, seven in Hebron and thirty-three in Jerusalem. Solomon then consolidates his rule, dealing decisively with Adonijah, Abiathar, Joab, and Shimei, so that the kingdom is firmly established in his hand.

The three psalms paired with this reading speak into the same season of aging and mortality. Psalm 37 is wisdom from an old man counseling patience with evildoers; Psalm 71 is the prayer of the gray-haired believer; Psalm 94 cries to God as the just judge of the earth.

Key themes

Succession and the cost of power. The handoff from David to Solomon is neither tidy nor sentimental. Solomon's wisdom shows early when he reads Adonijah's request for Abishag as a veiled claim on the throne. The executions that follow are sobering; the establishment of a kingdom in a fallen world involves hard justice and the settling of old debts, even as the larger promise to David's line moves forward.

Finishing well. David's charge to Solomon places obedience above strategy. Be strong, show yourself a man, keep the commandments. A life that began with a slingshot ends with a father pressing his son toward faithfulness to God. The reading invites reflection on what we hand to those who come after us.

Patience with the wicked. Psalm 37 answers the ache that runs through the whole reading: why do the wicked prosper? Its refrain, do not fret because of evildoers, uses a Hebrew word that means to get heated or worked up. The psalm counsels the long view, trusting that the wicked fade like grass while the meek inherit the land.

Trust when strength fails. Psalm 71 is the voice of old age, pleading do not cast me off in the time of old age, do not forsake me when my strength is spent. Psalm 94 adds the cry for justice, addressing God as the one to whom vengeance belongs and as the defense of the oppressed when human courts fail.

Takeaways

  • God's promise to David's house advances even through messy, violent human politics; the kingdom is secured but not by clean hands.
  • David's last words prize faithfulness over cleverness, modeling how to pass on what matters most to the next generation.
  • The paired psalms reframe aging and injustice as occasions for trust: do not fret over evildoers, and do not fear being forsaken when strength fails.

Connections

This succession fulfills the covenant promise of 2 Samuel 7, where God pledged that David's offspring would build a house for his name and have an established throne. Solomon's accession is the first installment of that promise, a line the New Testament traces all the way to Jesus, the son of David. David's charge to keep the law of Moses echoes the king's instructions in Deuteronomy 17.

The psalms reach back across the plan as well. Psalm 71's plea not to be forsaken in old age sits naturally beside David's own declining days in 1 Kings 1. Psalm 37's counsel that the meek inherit the land is taken up directly by Jesus in the Beatitudes, and Psalm 94's portrait of God as judge of all the earth anticipates the final reckoning promised throughout Scripture.

2 Samuel 7:12-16 → Deuteronomy 17:18-20 → 1 Chronicles 29:26-28 → Matthew 5:5 → Romans 12:19 →

Reflection

David's deathbed and the psalms of old age put a question to us that comfort and youth let us avoid: will I trust God when my strength is spent and when the wicked seem to win? Psalm 71 does not pretend the fear away. It names it, then answers it with memory, recalling a God who has been a refuge since the womb.

If you are tempted to fret over those who prosper by doing wrong, sit with Psalm 37 slowly. Its instruction is not passivity but a reordered confidence: commit your way to the Lord, be still before him, and wait. The long arc belongs to him, not to the evildoer.
