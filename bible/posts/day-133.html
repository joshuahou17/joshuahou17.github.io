Day 133 of 365

2 Samuel 11-12; 1 Chronicles 20

The King Who Took: David, Bathsheba, and Uriah

Read 2 Samuel 11-12 in the NIV → Read 1 Chronicles 20 in the NIV →

Context

Second Samuel 11 opens with a quiet indictment: it was the season when kings go out to battle, but David stayed in Jerusalem. From his roof he sees Bathsheba, sends for her, and takes her; the Hebrew verbs pile up his actions while she remains the object of them. When she conceives, David tries to cover his deed by recalling her husband Uriah the Hittite, and when the loyal soldier refuses the comfort of home while his comrades camp in the field, David arranges his death at the front. The chapter ends with the chilling note that the thing David had done displeased the Lord.

Chapter 12 brings Nathan, who tells a parable of a rich man who slaughters a poor man's only lamb. David's hot judgment, that the man deserves death, becomes the trap: you are the man. The parallel account in 1 Chronicles 20 records the same Ammonite campaign that frames these events but pointedly omits the scandal, focusing instead on the public victories of David's reign.

Key themes

Power that takes. Nathan's parable frames the sin precisely as theft: the rich man with many flocks seizes the one ewe lamb of the poor man. David, who had everything, took the one thing that was not his, and the prophet exposes how privilege can curdle into predation.

Sin against God. When David finally breaks, he says he has sinned against the Lord. Though real human beings were grievously wronged, the narrative insists that all sin is ultimately an offense against the God whose word David despised.

Forgiveness and consequence. Nathan announces both that the Lord has put away David's sin and that the sword will not depart from his house. Pardon is immediate and free, yet the wreckage of the deed will ripple through David's family for the rest of the book.

Two ways of telling history. Chronicles records the war and the crowns but not the rooftop and the cover-up, a reminder that what a record omits can be as revealing as what it includes.

Takeaways

  • The text refuses to soften David's actions; calling sin by its real name is the first step the prophet and the king both finally take.
  • Repentance does not erase consequences: God forgave David freely while the harm set loose in his household still unfolded.
  • Nathan's method, leading David to condemn himself before revealing the target, shows the disarming power of a well-told story to pierce a hardened conscience.

Connections

David's confession here becomes the spoken prayer of Psalm 51, traditionally tied to this very moment, which the plan pairs with this reading in the following days. The promise that the sword would not leave David's house sets in motion the family violence of the coming chapters: Amnon, Tamar, and Absalom. The contrast with 1 Chronicles, which celebrates David's victories while passing over his fall, recalls how Samuel and Kings tell Israel's story with an unsparing eye, while Chronicles writes for a returned community in need of hope.

Reflection

It is easy to read this as the fall of a uniquely flawed king, harder to hear Nathan's word land on us. Where do we, like David, judge in others what we excuse in ourselves? And when the parable turns and says you are the one, is our instinct to defend and minimize, or to do what David finally did and say plainly, I have sinned?
