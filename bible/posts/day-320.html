Acts 1-3: The Spirit Poured Out

Read Acts 1-3 in the NIV →

Context

Acts is the sequel to Luke's Gospel, addressed to the same Theophilus and picking up where the Gospel ended. Chapter 1 recounts the forty days of resurrection appearances, the ascension, and the disciples waiting in Jerusalem as Jesus commanded. The replacement of Judas by Matthias restores the symbolic number twelve, signaling the renewal of Israel around the apostles.

Pentecost in chapter 2 is the hinge of the book. The Spirit comes with wind and fire, the disciples speak in other tongues, and pilgrims from across the diaspora hear the wonders of God in their own languages. Peter, the same man who denied Jesus, stands to preach the first Christian sermon, and the church grows from a frightened band to thousands. Chapter 3 shows the new community in action, healing a man lame from birth and using the moment to preach Jesus again.

Key themes

Promise fulfilled. Pentecost delivers what Jesus promised in the farewell discourse, the Spirit poured out. Peter interprets it through the prophet Joel: this is the last days, when God pours his Spirit on all flesh, sons and daughters, young and old.

The kerygma. Peter's sermons follow a clear pattern: Jesus was crucified according to God's plan, God raised him, the apostles are witnesses, scripture foretold it, therefore repent and be baptized. The resurrection is the engine of the proclamation, not a footnote to it.

A new kind of community. The believers devote themselves to teaching, fellowship, the breaking of bread, and prayer, sharing possessions so none is in need. The Spirit's coming produces not just experience but a visible, generous common life.

Takeaways

  • Pentecost is the promised arrival of the Spirit, reversing Babel by uniting many languages around one message of God's works.
  • The earliest Christian preaching centers on the death and resurrection of Jesus as the fulfillment of scripture, calling for repentance.
  • The Spirit creates a tangible community marked by shared teaching, worship, prayer, and radical generosity toward the needy.

Connections

Peter's Pentecost sermon quotes Joel's prophecy of the Spirit poured out on all flesh and Psalm 16's hope that God's Holy One would not see corruption, applied to the resurrection. The scattering of one language into many at Babel in Genesis is reversed as the gospel is heard in every tongue. And the healing of the lame man, who enters the temple walking and leaping, echoes Isaiah's vision of the lame leaping like a deer in the age of salvation.

Joel 2:28-32 → Psalm 16:8-11 → Genesis 11:1-9 → Isaiah 35:6 → Psalm 118:22 →

Reflection

The man who preaches three thousand into the kingdom at Pentecost is the same man who, weeks earlier, swore he never knew Jesus. That is the quiet miracle underneath the loud one. The Spirit does not wait for impressive material; it fills failed disciples and makes them witnesses. The early church's generosity flows from the same source. Where the Spirit is genuinely poured out, it shows up not only in tongues but in open hands.
