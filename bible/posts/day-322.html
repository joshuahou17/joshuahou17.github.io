Acts 7-8

The Stones and the Scattering

Read Acts 7-8 in the NIV →

Context

Acts 7 is the longest speech in the book, and it is not really a legal defense. Hauled before the Sanhedrin on charges of speaking against the temple and the law, Stephen answers with a sweeping retelling of Israel's history from Abraham to Solomon. His point is cumulative: God has always met His people on the move, outside the land and apart from the temple, and Israel has always resisted the messengers He sends.

When Stephen reaches the climax he turns the charge back on his accusers, calling them stiff-necked betrayers of the Righteous One. They stone him outside the city. Luke notes, almost in passing, that the witnesses laid their coats at the feet of a young man named Saul, who approved of the killing.

Acts 8 shows the consequence. A great persecution scatters the Jerusalem believers, and the scattering becomes mission. Philip preaches in Samaria, confronts Simon the sorcerer, and is then sent down a desert road to explain Isaiah 53 to an Ethiopian eunuch returning from Jerusalem.

Key themes

The God who cannot be boxed in. Stephen's history lesson presses one claim: God appeared to Abraham in Mesopotamia, to Joseph in Egypt, to Moses at a burning bush in the desert. The Most High does not dwell in houses made by hands. The temple was never meant to contain Him, and the gospel will not stay in Jerusalem.

Resistance to the Spirit as a pattern. Stephen reads Israel's story as a recurring refusal. They rejected Joseph, rejected Moses, persecuted the prophets, and now have betrayed and murdered the Messiah those prophets foretold. The sin in the room is not new; it is the family habit reaching its peak.

Persecution as engine of mission. The very violence meant to stamp out the church instead disperses it. What looks like defeat fulfills Jesus' commission to be witnesses in Judea, Samaria, and to the ends of the earth.

Dying like the Master. Stephen's last words mirror Jesus on the cross: he commits his spirit to the Lord and asks forgiveness for his killers. The first martyr's death is shaped like Christ's.

Takeaways

  • God's presence and purpose were never confined to one place or people; the temple pointed beyond itself, and so does the gospel.
  • Opposition often advances God's plan rather than halting it, as the scattering of believers spread the message they were meant to carry.
  • Stephen and the Ethiopian both show that understanding Scripture rightly leads to Jesus; the law and the prophets were always pointing to Him.

Connections

Stephen's dying words deliberately echo the crucifixion accounts read earlier in the Gospels, where Jesus committed His spirit to the Father and prayed for His executioners. The disciple now suffers as his Lord did. His charge that Israel always persecuted the prophets reaches back across the entire Old Testament narrative this plan has already traveled, from the rejection of Joseph in Genesis to the killing of the prophets. And when Philip opens Isaiah 53 to the Ethiopian, he draws on the suffering-servant passage to show that the One led like a lamb to slaughter is Jesus, tying the gospel directly to Israel's own Scriptures.

Reflection

Stephen saw heaven open and the Son of Man standing as the stones fell, and he forgave the men killing him. It is worth asking what lets a person hold that kind of peace under that kind of pressure. Where do you find yourself resisting what God is plainly doing because it does not fit the shape you expected? And can you trust that even your hardest losses might be the means God uses to carry His work further than comfort ever would?
