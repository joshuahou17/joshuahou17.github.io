Romans 5-6

From Death's Tyranny to Grace's Triumph

Paul's theological masterpiece in Romans 5-6 stands as one of Scripture's most profound explorations of salvation's dual nature: what God has done for us (justification) and what He now accomplishes in us (sanctification). These chapters form a crucial bridge in Paul's argument, transitioning from the foundation of justification by faith to its inevitable fruit in holy living.

Context and Structure

Romans 5 bridges justification and sanctification by showing that the same grace that declares believers righteous also transfers them into a new realm of life, with Romans 5:12-21 acting as the introduction to chapters 6-8 and a segue from Romans 5:1-11. Paul has spent Romans 1-4 establishing that all humanity stands condemned before God's holiness, yet justified freely through faith in Christ. Now he must address the inevitable question: if grace is so abundant, why not continue in sin to make grace abound even more?

The Adam-Christ typology in Romans 5:12-21 provides the theological framework for understanding this impossibility. The concepts of the "one" and the "many" are united in Adam and in Christ, albeit in different ways. Adam and Christ are both representatives, so that Adam is the type and Christ is the antitype. Yet Paul uses the phrase "is not like" and concludes each comparison with "how much more" showing how Christ's work "is greater in every way than that of Adam."

Key Theological Themes

Universal Headship and Representation: Paul's Adam-Christ typology reveals that all humanity falls under one of two heads. Sin has come through the headship of Adam and sanctification comes through the headship of Christ. Because of the natural headship of Adam, sin was imputed to the human family. But there is another head of the human family, and that is Christ. This is not merely individual choice but cosmic reality—we are all represented by someone.

The Necessity of Transformation: The apostle having at large asserted, opened, and proved, the great doctrine of justification by faith, for fear lest any should suck poison out of that sweet flower, and turn that grace of God into wantonness and licentiousness, he, with a like zeal, copiousness of expression, and cogency of argument, presses the absolute necessity of sanctification and a holy life, as the inseparable fruit and companion of justification. Paul's famous question "Shall we continue in sin that grace may abound?" receives an emphatic "God forbid!" not because of human effort, but because of what believers have already become in Christ.

Union with Christ's Death and Resurrection: There is no work which you or I can perform to attain to sanctification. Our sanctification is accomplished only by our identification with Christ in His death to sin and in His resurrection to newness of life. Baptism symbolizes this reality—we have been united with Christ in his death, burial, and resurrection, making our old relationship to sin not merely damaged but decisively ended.

Historical and Cultural Background

Paul writes to Roman believers who would have understood concepts of slavery, legal representation, and imperial headship. The imagery of "reigning" that Paul employs—death reigning through Adam, grace reigning through Christ—draws from political realities his audience knew well. Many associate Romans 6 with the idea of "sanctification," or "making holy." It certainly seems that Paul is moving in this direction by saying that we are no longer bound to sin now that we are bound to Christ. In being bound to Christ, we are allowed to participate in a new reality.

The theological sophistication of Paul's argument reflects his audience's familiarity with Jewish concepts of corporate representation (Adam representing all humanity) while addressing Gentile concerns about moral transformation. Wrath and justification (of Romans 3-5) yield to the discussion of sin and sanctification. Justification is restoration to life, while sanctification is restoration to health. Justification brings us from the tomb; sanctification delivers us from the old "threads."

Cross-References and Connections

The Adam-Christ parallel connects powerfully to previous readings in our journey. Genesis 4-7 showed us humanity's descent from the first murder to global corruption—now we see that this descent began even earlier, with Adam's representational fall. The Immanuel prophecy in Isaiah 7-11 takes on new depth when we understand Christ as the second Adam, the true human who succeeds where the first failed.

Jesus' temptation in the wilderness (Matthew 3-4) becomes even more significant when seen as the second Adam facing Satan successfully where the first Adam failed. The confidence David expresses in the Psalms (Psalms 3-5) now has its ultimate ground—not merely in God's covenant faithfulness, but in our union with Christ the conqueror.

Reflection: The Scandal of Grace

Paul's teaching scandalizes both legalists and libertines. To those who think good works earn God's favor, he proclaims justification by faith alone. To those who think grace eliminates moral obligation, he declares that grace creates new slaves—slaves to righteousness. Because of Jesus, we no longer have to be enslaved to sin; rather, we have available to us a new way of life in which we can live in the power of the resurrection. What Paul is emphasizing is that we do not have to approach sin after conversion the way we approached sin before conversion. Something changes in us, in our status, and in our reality.

The Christian life is not about trying harder to be good—it's about living from the reality of who we already are in Christ. We have been sanctified (made holy) and are in a continual, dynamic reality of sanctification in which our decisions and actions matter. We no longer have to be slaves to sin, but we can live into a reality of freedom, a freedom pivoting off of a condition of slavery to Christ. This is the beautiful paradox of Christian freedom: we are most free when we are Christ's slaves, most human when we abandon the futile project of self-salvation and rest in the finished work of the second Adam.

Today's passage invites us to live from this settled reality rather than striving toward it. The question is not "How can I become righteous?" but "How shall I live as one who has already been declared righteous and united to the Righteous One?" The answer: by presenting ourselves to God as those alive from the dead, trusting that the same power that raised Christ from the dead is at work in us.
