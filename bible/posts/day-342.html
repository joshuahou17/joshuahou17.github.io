Day 342 of 365

Romans 4-7

Counted Righteous, Set Free

Read Romans 4-7 in the NIV →

Context

These four chapters sit at the heart of Paul's argument to a church he had not yet visited. Having declared in chapters 1-3 that every person, Jew and Gentile alike, stands guilty before God and that righteousness comes apart from law-keeping, Paul now proves and unfolds that claim. Chapter 4 reaches back to Abraham, the founding father of Israel, to show that he was reckoned righteous by faith before he was circumcised and before the law was given. Circumcision was a seal of a faith he already had, not its cause.

From there the argument moves outward and inward. Chapter 5 names the fruit of being justified and sets Adam against Christ as the two heads of humanity. Chapter 6 answers the obvious objection that grace might license sin, anchoring the believer's new life in baptismal union with Jesus' death and resurrection. Chapter 7 then defends the law's holiness while exposing its inability to rescue, ending in the raw cry of a person who knows the good and cannot perform it.

Key themes

Righteousness reckoned, not earned. Paul's key verb is to count or credit. Abraham did nothing to earn his standing; he trusted the God who gives life to the dead and calls into being things that are not. The same God credits righteousness to all who believe in the one who raised Jesus. Faith is not a new kind of work but the empty hand that receives.

Two men, two humanities. In chapter 5 Adam and Christ stand as representatives whose single acts determine the destiny of all joined to them. Through one man's trespass condemnation spread; through one man's obedience the free gift of righteousness abounds all the more. Grace does not merely match the damage of sin, it overflows it.

United to Christ in death and life. Chapter 6 insists that those baptized into Christ have died to sin's reign and been raised to walk in newness of life. Grace is not a permit to keep sinning but the very power that breaks sin's mastery. The believer is to count this true and present the body to God.

The law is good but cannot save. Chapter 7 clears the law of blame: it is holy, just, and good, and it exposes sin for what it is. Yet the divided self of verses 14-25 shows that knowing the good does not supply the strength to do it. The cry for rescue sets up the answer that chapter 8 will give.

Takeaways

  • Acceptance with God rests on trusting his promise, not on a record of performance, just as it did for Abraham.
  • Grace does not loosen the grip of sin; through union with Christ's death and resurrection it breaks sin's claim on us.
  • The law can diagnose the disease but cannot cure it, which is why Paul drives us beyond rule-keeping to rescue.

Connections

Paul's whole case rests on a verse the plan met long ago: God credited Abraham's faith as righteousness in Genesis 15:6, spoken before circumcision in Genesis 17 and long before Sinai. By citing David's blessing on the forgiven in Psalm 32, Paul shows that grace to sinners was never a New Testament novelty. The Adam-Christ contrast carries the reader back to the garden of Genesis 3, where one disobedience opened the door to death that Christ's obedience now reverses. The inner war of chapter 7 also echoes the law given through Moses in Exodus and Deuteronomy, which named the good without granting the power to keep it, and anticipates the new heart promised in Jeremiah and Ezekiel.

Genesis 15:6 → Genesis 3:17-19 → Psalm 32:1-2 → Habakkuk 2:4 → Exodus 20:17 →

Reflection

It is tempting to read these chapters as a ledger we still hope to balance, and just as tempting to hear grace as permission to coast. Paul refuses both. Where do you still try to earn what Abraham simply received, and where have you treated freedom as license rather than as a death you have already died?

The honest groan of Romans 7, the wanting good and doing otherwise, is not a sign that the gospel has failed but the very place it meets us. Sit with that cry today, and let it carry you toward the deliverance Paul is about to name.
