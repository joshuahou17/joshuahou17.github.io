Day 329 of 365

Galatians 4-6

Free to Walk by the Spirit

Read Galatians 4-6 in the NIV →

Context

Having argued that faith, not law, makes a person right with God, Paul now turns to what that freedom looks like in daily life. He contrasts two ways of being: the slavery of trying to earn standing through the law, and the inheritance that belongs to those who are God's adopted sons through Christ. The Spirit himself testifies that they are no longer slaves but heirs.

The second half of the letter answers an obvious worry. If we are free from the law, what keeps freedom from collapsing into self-indulgence? Paul's reply is that freedom is meant for love, and that the Spirit, not a rulebook, produces the life God desires. The flesh and the Spirit pull in opposite directions, and the believer's calling is to keep step with the Spirit.

Paul closes with practical instructions about carrying one another's burdens, doing good to all, and boasting in nothing but the cross. The goal of the whole letter surfaces in a single phrase: what counts is a new creation.

Key themes

Sons and heirs, not slaves. Through Christ and the Spirit, believers are adopted children who cry out to God as Father. They have moved from the status of minors under guardians to full heirs of the promise.

Freedom for love, not license. Christ set them free to be free, but that freedom is not a license for self-indulgence. The whole law is fulfilled in one command, to love your neighbor as yourself, expressed through serving one another.

Spirit against flesh. The flesh and the Spirit are at war, each opposing the other. To walk by the Spirit is to refuse the cravings of the flesh and to let the Spirit set the direction of one's life.

The fruit of the Spirit. Where the works of the flesh divide and destroy, the Spirit grows a single fruit: love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control. Against such things there is no law.

The new creation. Neither circumcision nor its absence counts for anything; what matters is a new creation. The old marks of identity give way to the transforming work of God.

Takeaways

  • Christian freedom is not freedom from love but freedom for it; we are set free in order to serve.
  • The fruit of the Spirit grows from a changed heart rather than from gritted-teeth rule-keeping.
  • Bearing one another's burdens and restoring the fallen gently is how the law of Christ is fulfilled in community.

Connections

The Spirit-versus-flesh conflict develops a tension running through the plan from the start, from the human heart bent toward evil in the early chapters of Genesis to the prophets' promise of a new heart and a new spirit placed within God's people. Paul presents the indwelling Spirit as the fulfillment of that promise.

His summary of the law as love of neighbor takes up the command from Leviticus that Jesus himself named, in the earlier Gospel readings, as second only to loving God. Where yesterday's chapters of Galatians established that the law was a guardian leading to Christ, today's reading shows the life that follows once the guardian's job is done, a life directed not by external rules but by the Spirit who produces what the law always aimed at.

Reflection

The fruit of the Spirit is singular, not a menu to pick from; we do not get to cultivate patience while excusing our lack of kindness. Which part of this fruit is most obviously missing in me right now, and what would it look like to keep in step with the Spirit there rather than gripping harder on my own willpower? And whose burden, today, am I being asked to help carry?
