Psalms 1-2, 15, 22-24, 47, 68

The Gates Lift Up Their Heads

Read Psalms 1-2, 15, 22-24, 47, 68 in the NIV →

Context

These seven psalms are not read in canonical order tonight but as a deliberate arrangement around David's reign and the ark's arrival in Jerusalem. Psalms 1 and 2 stand together as the gateway to the whole Psalter, a paired prologue: the first opens with a blessing on the one who delights in the law of the LORD, the second closes with a blessing on all who take refuge in the LORD's anointed king. The Hebrew word for happy or blessed brackets the pair, inviting the reader to enter the book through two doors at once, the way of torah piety and the hope of God's messianic reign.

The remaining psalms carry the superscription of David and cluster around Zion and its worship. Psalm 15 asks who may dwell on God's holy hill. Psalms 22 and 23 hold suffering and trust side by side, the cry of dereliction and the quiet confidence of the shepherded soul. Psalm 24 reads as a liturgy for the ark entering Jerusalem's gates, and Psalms 47 and 68 enlarge the scene until God ascends his throne over all the nations.

Key themes

Two doors into one book. Psalm 1 and Psalm 2 were placed first to teach the reader how to read everything after them. One commends meditation on God's instruction day and night; the other announces a king set on Zion whom the nations rage against in vain. Torah piety and messianic kingship are not rival themes but the single hope of a people who live by God's word under God's anointed.

The clean hands that may ascend. Psalm 15 and Psalm 24 both ask who may stand in the holy place, and both answer with integrity rather than ritual: clean hands, a pure heart, truth in the inward parts. The question is not merely liturgical but moral, and it presses on every worshiper who would draw near.

The suffering and shepherding king. Psalm 22 begins in the abyss of abandonment and ends in a congregation's praise; Psalm 23 answers it with green pastures and an overflowing cup. Read together they trace a single arc from forsakenness to fellowship, and the church has long heard in them the voice of the Messiah who is both the slain shepherd and the living one.

God reigns over the nations. Psalms 24, 47, and 68 lift the horizon beyond Israel. The King of glory enters the ancient gates, God ascends amid shouting and trumpet, and the LORD comes from Sinai into his sanctuary leading captives in his train. The local act of bringing the ark up to Jerusalem becomes a sign of a cosmic enthronement.

Takeaways

  • The Psalter is meant to be entered through Psalms 1 and 2 together: a life shaped by God's word and bent toward God's anointed king.
  • Psalm 22 and Psalm 23 belong side by side; the cry of abandonment and the confession of trust are both true on the same lips.
  • The question Psalm 15 and Psalm 24 ask is who may draw near to God, and the answer is integrity of hands and heart, not mere ceremony.

Connections

This cluster gathers up the long road already traveled in the plan. The procession of Psalm 24 and the scattered enemies of Psalm 68 echo the moment when the ark of God was carried up to Jerusalem, the very event the next reading recounts; Psalm 68 deliberately borrows the cry that announced the ark on the march from Sinai, tying David's day to the wilderness journey out of Egypt read earlier. Psalm 2's anointed son and Psalm 22's pierced sufferer were both heard by the New Testament writers as the voice of Jesus, quoted at his baptism, his crucifixion, and his resurrection, so that these David songs become Christ's own.

Reflection

Sit for a moment with the strange neighborliness of Psalm 22 and Psalm 23. Most of us would rather skip the forsaken cry and rest only in green pastures, yet David places them next to each other without apology. Where in your own life are you tempted to silence the lament because you think faith requires only the shepherd song? What might it mean to let both be true at once, to bring the honest cry and the steady trust to the same God in the same week?
