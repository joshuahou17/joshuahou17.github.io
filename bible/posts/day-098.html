1 Samuel 1-3

A Prayer, a Boy, and a Voice in the Night

Read 1 Samuel 1-3 in the NIV →

Context

The book begins with a familiar Scriptural pattern: a barren woman in distress. Hannah, mocked by her husband's other wife, weeps at the Shiloh sanctuary and vows that if God gives her a son, she will dedicate him as a lifelong Nazirite. Eli the priest, watching her silent prayer, mistakes her for drunk, an early hint that even the religious leadership has lost the ability to discern God at work. God hears, and Samuel is born; Hannah's song in chapter 2 celebrates a God who reverses fortunes, lifting the lowly and bringing down the proud.

Against Samuel's quiet growth stands the corruption of Eli's sons, Hophni and Phinehas, who treat the offerings with contempt and abuse their office. A man of God pronounces judgment on Eli's house. Then in chapter 3, in a time when the word of the Lord was rare, God calls Samuel three times in the night. Eli finally recognizes the voice, and Samuel receives his first prophetic word, a hard one of judgment against the very household that raised him.

Key themes

God hears the lowly. Hannah's name means grace, and her story shows a God attentive to the powerless and overlooked. Her song anticipates the great reversals of the gospel, the hungry filled and the powerful brought low.

The decline of inherited religion. Eli's priesthood is failing; his sons are scoundrels and he is too passive to restrain them. Religious position without reverence is worthless, and God will not let a corrupt office endure simply because it is established.

God still speaks. When His word is rare and visions are not widespread, God begins again with a child who simply says, speak, for your servant is listening. Revelation resumes not with the powerful but with the willing.

Takeaways

  • God pays attention to the prayers of those the world ignores, and He often answers in ways that bless far beyond the one who prayed.
  • A religious title or family heritage cannot substitute for genuine reverence; Eli's house shows how hollow it becomes.
  • Hearing God begins with availability, a willingness to listen and obey even when the word is hard.

Connections

Hannah's prayer joins the chain of barren women, Sarah, Rebekah, Manoah's wife in Judges 13, who receive children by God's intervention, and her dedication of Samuel as a Nazirite directly echoes Samson's calling read earlier this week. Her song of reversal is closely mirrored by Mary's Magnificat in Luke 1, and her line about the Lord's anointed looks ahead to the kings Samuel will anoint. The judgment on Eli's house begins a theme of priestly accountability that runs through the historical books.

1 Samuel 2:1-10 → Luke 1:46-55 → Judges 13:5 → 1 Samuel 3:10 → 1 Samuel 2:35 →

Reflection

This opening asks what we do with what we have longed for and finally received. Hannah gave Samuel back. It also asks whether we are positioned to hear God, like the boy who answered, or numbed by routine, like the priest who at first did not recognize the voice. Is my heart saying, speak, your servant is listening?
