Revelation 1-5

The Revelation of Jesus Among the Lampstands

Read Revelation 1-5 in the NIV →

Context

Revelation announces itself as the revelation of Jesus Christ, given to show his servants what must soon take place. John writes from exile on the island of Patmos to seven real churches in Asia Minor, in a form known as apocalyptic, dense with symbol and drawn from the prophets, meant to unveil heavenly reality behind earthly appearances. It opens with a vision of the risen Christ among seven golden lampstands, one like a son of man with eyes like fire and a voice like rushing waters, holding the keys of death and Hades.

Chapters two and three contain seven letters, each tailored to a specific congregation, mixing commendation, rebuke, and promise to the one who overcomes. Then in chapters four and five the scene shifts to heaven, where John sees God enthroned amid worship, holding a sealed scroll that no one is worthy to open. As John weeps, an elder announces the Lion of the tribe of Judah has conquered, but when John looks he sees a Lamb standing as though slain. Heaven erupts in worship of the Lamb who is worthy, because by his blood he ransomed people for God from every tribe and tongue and nation.

Key themes

The exalted, present Christ. Before any drama of judgment, Revelation shows the risen Jesus walking among his churches, intimately aware of their faithfulness and failures. He is no distant figure but the living one who holds the keys of death and stands in the midst of his people.

Honest words to real churches. The seven letters address actual struggles, persecution, compromise, lukewarm complacency, with searching honesty. The repeated call is to hear what the Spirit says and to overcome, with rich promises attached to faithful endurance.

The slain Lamb is worthy. The pivot of these chapters is stunning: the conquering Lion turns out to be a slaughtered Lamb. God's victory comes not through raw force but through sacrificial love, and it is the crucified and risen Christ who alone is worthy to open and enact the meaning of history.

Takeaways

  • The risen Jesus is present among his people, knowing them fully, which is both comforting and searching; he addresses the church as it actually is.
  • Faithful endurance matters; the promises of Revelation are repeatedly given to the one who overcomes, encouraging perseverance under pressure and against compromise.
  • God conquers through the slain Lamb, so true power is cruciform; the center of heaven's worship is the One who gave himself to ransom people from every nation.

Connections

Revelation is woven from earlier readings. The vision of one like a son of man draws directly on Daniel 7 and 10, and the throne room scene with four living creatures echoes Ezekiel 1 and Isaiah 6, where the seraphim cry holy, holy, holy. The Lamb who was slain reaches back to the Passover lamb of Exodus 12 and the suffering servant of Isaiah 53, while the new song that the Lamb ransomed a kingdom of priests recalls God's purpose for Israel in Exodus 19. John's commission to write what he sees stands in the long line of prophetic callings.

Daniel 7:13-14 → Isaiah 6:1-3 → Ezekiel 1:4-28 → Exodus 12:5-7 → Exodus 19:5-6 →

Reflection

Revelation begins not with terror but with Jesus, present among the lampstands, knowing each church by name and circumstance. That should shape how we read everything that follows: the One who unveils the future is the same Lord who walks among his people now. When John weeps that no one is worthy to open the scroll of history, the answer is not a warrior but a Lamb still bearing the marks of slaughter. Where are you tempted to think God's purposes advance only through power and dominance? Heaven's worship corrects us: the slain Lamb is worthy, and faithful, self-giving love is the very shape of his victory. Hear what the Spirit says, and hold fast.
