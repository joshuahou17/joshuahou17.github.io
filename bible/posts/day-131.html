2 Samuel 10; 1 Chronicles 19; Psalms 20

Some Trust in Chariots

Read 2 Samuel 10 in the NIV → Read 1 Chronicles 19 in the NIV → Read Psalms 20 in the NIV →

Context

This reading pairs narrative and prayer. Second Samuel 10, retold in First Chronicles 19, recounts how David's goodwill toward Hanun, the new Ammonite king, is grotesquely repaid. Suspecting espionage, Hanun shaves off half the beards of David's envoys and cuts their garments at the buttocks, a humiliation aimed through the ambassadors at the king himself. In that culture the beard was a mark of dignity, so the insult was a declaration of war.

Realizing they have made themselves a stench to David, the Ammonites hire thousands of Aramean mercenaries. Joab, caught between two armies, divides his forces and commits the outcome to God, and Israel routs both foes. Psalm 20 sits naturally alongside this scene: a royal psalm, the congregation's intercession for the king on the eve of battle, ending in confidence that the LORD saves his anointed.

Key themes

Kindness met with contempt. David intends hesed toward Hanun for his father's sake, the same covenant loyalty he showed Mephibosheth. Suspicion turns the gesture into an outrage. The episode is a sober reminder that goodwill is not always received as offered, and that pride can manufacture an enemy out of a friend.

Courage that commits the outcome to God. Pressed front and rear, Joab makes his plan and then speaks the heart of the matter: be strong, and may the LORD do what seems good to him. Strategy and trust are not rivals here. He acts with resolve while leaving the verdict in God's hands.

Prayer for the king. Psalm 20 is the people praying over their leader before he fights: may the LORD answer you in the day of trouble, may he send you help from the sanctuary. The community's strength is bound up with the king's, and their first weapon is intercession.

The name of the LORD versus chariots. The psalm's hinge is its sharpest line: some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the LORD our God. Israel's kings were forbidden to multiply horses precisely so that confidence would rest in God's character rather than in military hardware. Faith speaks its Save, LORD before the battle, not only after.

Takeaways

  • Acts of kindness can be misread as threats, and pride often turns potential friends into foes.
  • Faithful courage plans wisely and acts decisively while still entrusting the result to God.
  • Real trust names its confidence before the outcome is known, choosing the LORD over visible sources of strength.

Connections

David's intended kindness to Hanun mirrors the covenant loyalty he extended to Mephibosheth in the previous narrative reading, showing the same instinct to honor a father's friendship. The Aramean and Edomite struggles here are the wars that lie behind Psalm 60's lament for teaching read in the previous cluster. Psalm 20's contrast between chariots and the name of the LORD restates the conviction of Psalm 33, that a king is not saved by his great army, and echoes the law's prohibition against kings amassing horses. It also looks forward to Zechariah's vision of a King who comes humble and cuts off the war horse, and finds its deepest fulfillment in the Anointed One whom God truly saves.

Reflection

Joab arrayed his troops and then said, may the LORD do what seems good to him; the congregation of Psalm 20 prayed before a single sword was drawn. Both refuse the false choice between diligent effort and dependence on God. Where are you tempted to put your trust in your own chariots, the resources and contingencies you can see and count, and what would it mean to name the LORD as your confidence before you know how the battle turns out?
