Day 82 of 365

Joshua 1-4

Crossing Into the Promise

Read Joshua 1-4 in the NIV →

Context

The book of Joshua opens at a hinge in Israel's story. The forty years of wilderness wandering are over, the generation that left Egypt has died, and Moses himself has just been buried east of the Jordan. The land promised to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob lies just across the river. Everything now depends on whether a new leader and a new generation will trust the God who brought them this far.

God's opening charge to Joshua is striking for what it repeats. Three times he is told to be strong and courageous, and the command is tethered not to Joshua's competence but to two promises: God will never leave him, and the same word given to Moses now rests on him. Joshua is not asked to invent a path but to meditate on the law day and night and to walk in it.

Chapters 2 through 4 then narrate the entry itself. Two spies are sheltered in Jericho by Rahab, a Canaanite prostitute who confesses Israel's God as Lord of heaven and earth. The crossing of the Jordan at flood stage follows, with the ark of the covenant leading the way and twelve stones lifted from the riverbed as a lasting witness.

Key themes

Succession and the unbroken word. The narrative goes out of its way to bind Joshua to Moses. The God who spoke at the burning bush and on Sinai speaks again, and the promise does not die with the prophet who carried it. Leaders pass; the covenant word endures.

Courage rooted in presence. The repeated call to be strong and courageous is not a pep talk about self-confidence. Its ground is the assurance that God goes with Joshua as he went with Moses. Obedience to the written law and confidence in the divine presence are held together, not opposed.

Unexpected faith. Rahab is everything an Israelite reader might dismiss: a Canaanite, a woman, a prostitute. Yet she alone in Jericho reads the signs rightly and casts her lot with Israel's God. The conquest narrative opens with grace extended to an outsider who believes.

Memory and witness. The twelve stones taken from the Jordan exist so that children will ask what they mean. Israel is to be a people who remember, who turn deliverance into testimony for the next generation rather than letting it fade.

Takeaways

  • God's promises outlast the leaders who carry them; the same word entrusted to Moses passes intact to Joshua, so courage rests on God's presence rather than human ability.
  • Faith can appear where it is least expected. Rahab, an outsider by every measure, reads God's hand rightly and is woven into Israel's story.
  • Deliverance is meant to be remembered and retold; the memorial stones turn a one-time crossing into a permanent witness for generations who did not see it.

Connections

The Jordan crossing deliberately echoes the Red Sea deliverance in Exodus 14. Once again waters stand back and Israel passes on dry ground, but now the people enter the land rather than fleeing toward it. The text even says God exalted Joshua in Israel's sight as he had exalted Moses, framing the two crossings as bookends to the wilderness years read just before this.

God's charge to meditate on the law day and night picks up the language of Deuteronomy, the book Israel heard most recently, where Moses commissioned Joshua and promised that the Lord would go before him. The continuity is intentional.

Rahab's confession reaches forward as well as back. She is named in the genealogy of Jesus in Matthew 1 and praised for her faith in Hebrews 11 and James 2, a Canaanite grafted into the line of the Messiah.

Reflection

The opening of Joshua speaks to anyone standing at a threshold after a season of waiting. The promise had been spoken for centuries, yet a whole generation died without seeing it. The question facing Joshua is whether he will trust that God's word is as alive today as it was in Moses' hands.

Notice that courage here is not the absence of fear but obedience anchored in presence. Joshua is told to act, to cross, to lead, and at the same time to keep returning to the word God has already given. The two are not in tension.

And the chapter refuses to let deliverance become private. The stones are set up so that questions will be asked and the story retold. What has God carried you across, and who is meant to hear about it?
