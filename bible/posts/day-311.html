Matthew 24

When the Son of Man Comes: Matthew's Olivet Discourse

Read Matthew 24 in the NIV →

Context

Matthew 24 records the same sermon as Mark 13, delivered the same afternoon, but in a longer and more carefully framed version. Where Mark is terse and breathless, Matthew slows down to structure the teaching and to add material Mark lacks. The setting is shared: Jesus has just left the temple, predicted that not one stone will be left on another, and crossed to the Mount of Olives. The disciples come privately with a question.

Matthew sharpens that question in a way Mark does not. The disciples ask, 'What will be the sign of your coming and of the end of the age?' Matthew uses two loaded Greek words. One is parousia, 'coming' or 'presence,' used in the ancient world for the official arrival of a king; Matthew is the only Gospel writer to use it, and only in this discourse. The other is sunteleia, the 'close' of the age. By recording both, Matthew shows the disciples tangling two distinct events, the temple's fall and the end of history, and the chapter works to pull them apart.

Key themes

The fuller eschatology of Matthew. Matthew's account is not a contradiction of Mark but an expansion. He keeps the birth pains, the abomination of desolation, and the warning against deception, then adds the lightning flashing from east to west: the parousia will be no secret rumor in the wilderness but unmistakable and public. The structure moves from signs of the temple's fall, readable like the budding fig tree, to a coming with no advance sign at all.

As in the days of Noah. Here Matthew leaves Mark behind. Jesus compares his coming to Noah's flood, when people ate, drank, and married, absorbed in ordinary life and unaware until the water swept them away. The point is not that the last days will be uniquely wicked but that they will be deceptively normal. Then the paired sayings: two men in a field, two women grinding at the mill, one taken and one left. In context, to be taken is to be swept away in judgment, as the flood took the unprepared.

The faithful and wise servant. Matthew closes with a parable Mark only gestures at. A master leaves a servant in charge of the household, then delays. The faithful servant keeps feeding the household; the wicked one says, 'My master is delayed,' and turns to beating his fellows and feasting with drunkards. Matthew's interest is squarely on faithfulness during the master's absence, on what a servant does while no one watches.

AD 70 or the end, or both. Readers have long debated whether this chapter describes Jerusalem's fall in AD 70, the future second coming, or both. The 'this generation' sayings and the temple focus point near; the global gathering and cosmic upheaval point far. Many read it as already-and-not-yet: the judgment on Jerusalem becomes the pattern and pledge of the final parousia.

Takeaways

  • Matthew frames the disciples' question as two things at once, the temple's fall and the end of the age, then distinguishes a near event with readable signs from a final coming with none.
  • Matthew alone calls Jesus' return the parousia, the royal arrival, and pairs it with the days of Noah: judgment falls on a world busy with ordinary life and caught unprepared.
  • The chapter's deepest concern is not dating the end but readiness, faithful service rendered precisely because the timing is unknown and the master is delayed.
  • Matthew 24 sets up the three readiness parables of chapter 25 (ten virgins, talents, sheep and goats), all answering one question: what does the waiting servant do?

Connections

Yesterday's Mark 13 gave us the same sermon with a single hammered keyword, 'watch, stay awake'; Matthew keeps that urgency but widens the lens with the Noah analogy and the servant parable. The Son of Man coming on the clouds reaches back to Daniel 7, where one like a son of man receives dominion and an everlasting kingdom; Jesus claims that vision as his own arrival. The 'abomination of desolation' echoes Daniel 9, the language of the desecrated sanctuary. The days of Noah send us to Genesis 6 and 7, to a world eating and marrying while the rain began, and to the ark that divided the saved from the swept-away. And the parousia, the visit of the king, gathers up the prophets' longing for the day of the Lord, now centered on the returning Son of Man.

Reflection

The whole chapter narrows to one posture: faithful readiness. Jesus does not hand his disciples a calendar; he hands them a job. The faithful servant is simply found feeding the household when the master returns, doing the ordinary work he was given at the proper time. The wicked servant's downfall begins with one quiet thought, 'My master is delayed,' the assumption that absence means no one is coming back. That thought frees him to cut corners and mistreat the people around him.

Most of us will not live in dramatic days of lightning across the sky; we will live in the days of Noah, ordinary mornings of working and loving the people near us. Matthew's question is not whether we can decode the signs but whether the master, returning at an unexpected hour, would find us faithful in the small assignment already in our hands. Readiness is not anxious scanning of the horizon. It is steady, humble service today, as if he might come this afternoon, and as if he might tarry a thousand years.
