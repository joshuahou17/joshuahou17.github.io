Day 349 of 365

Colossians; Philemon

The Supreme Christ and a Runaway Brother

Read Colossians in the NIV → Read Philemon in the NIV →

Context

These two letters belong together. Both were written during Paul's Roman imprisonment, both name the same circle of companions, and both were delivered to Colossae by Tychicus accompanied by Onesimus. Onesimus is the hinge: in Colossians Paul commends him as a faithful and beloved brother who is one of you, and the whole letter to Philemon is Paul's personal appeal on his behalf.

Colossae was a small city in the Lycus Valley of Asia Minor. Paul had not founded its church; Epaphras had. A teaching had taken root there that diminished Christ, mixing Jewish ceremonial rules, ascetic self-denial, and the worship of cosmic powers, promising a deeper spirituality through observances Christ had not required. Philemon, by contrast, is the shortest and most personal of Paul's letters, a single sustained request rather than a sermon.

Key themes

The supremacy of Christ. At the heart of Colossians stands a soaring hymn: Christ is the image of the invisible God, the one in whom and through whom and for whom all things were created, in whom the fullness of God dwells, who holds all things together and reconciles all things through the blood of his cross. Against any teaching that treats him as one power among many, Paul insists Christ is supreme and complete.

Sufficiency over religious add-ons. Because believers are complete in Christ, they need no extra rules, visions, or rituals to be acceptable to God. Paul dismantles food laws, festival-keeping, and harsh treatment of the body as having an appearance of wisdom but no real power to restrain sin. Fullness is found in a person, not a program.

The new life made practical. Doctrine becomes conduct. Believers put off the old self and put on compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, patience, and forgiveness, because the Lord has forgiven them. This is exactly the posture Philemon is asked to take.

Reconciliation in the flesh. Philemon tests whether Colossians is true. Paul does not command; he appeals, even offering to repay any debt himself, asking Philemon to welcome Onesimus no longer as a slave but as a beloved brother. Cosmic reconciliation in Colossians comes down to two estranged men in one household.

Takeaways

  • Christ is not a supplement to spiritual life but its source and sum; nothing needs to be added to him.
  • Genuine doctrine always lands in changed relationships, not just changed beliefs.
  • The gospel reframes social status: in Christ a runaway slave becomes a beloved brother.

Connections

The Christ of the Colossian hymn, the agent of creation in whom all things hold together, reaches back to the opening of Genesis and to the wisdom that was present when God made the world, and forward to John's witness that all things were made through the Word. Paul's appeal to forgive because you have been forgiven echoes the heart of Jesus' own teaching on forgiveness in the Gospels. And the prison setting of both letters connects directly to the previous reading: it was in the Roman house arrest that closed Acts that Paul wrote them, sending Tychicus and Onesimus back to the very region where his missionary work had borne fruit.

Reflection

It is easy to admire the grand vision of Colossians, Christ filling and reconciling the whole cosmos, and never let it touch the person we are estranged from across the hallway. Philemon refuses that gap. The same gospel that holds the universe together asks a man to tear up a debt and embrace someone the world said he could own. Is there a relationship where you are still treating someone as a category, a slave, an enemy, a debtor, when Christ is asking you to receive them as a brother or sister?
