Titus

Grace That Trains Us for Good Works on Crete

Read Titus in the NIV →

Context

Titus is the third of the Pastoral Epistles, written to Paul's trusted coworker whom he left on the island of Crete to set in order what was unfinished and to appoint elders in every town. Crete had a reputation for unruliness, and Paul quotes one of its own poets to underscore the moral disorder Titus must address. The letter is short, urgent, and intensely practical.

Paul outlines the character of elders, warns sharply against deceivers who upset whole households for dishonest gain, and then instructs older men, older women, younger women, younger men, and slaves in conduct fitting to sound doctrine. Twice the letter pauses for some of the New Testament's most concentrated statements of the gospel, declaring that the grace of God has appeared bringing salvation and training believers to renounce ungodliness, and that God saved us not because of works done in righteousness but according to his mercy through the washing of rebirth.

Key themes

Grace teaches a new way of life. Paul never sets grace against good works; rather, grace is a teacher that trains believers to say no to ungodliness and yes to self-controlled, upright, godly living while they wait for the appearing of Christ. Salvation produces a transformed community, not mere private belief.

Sound doctrine shapes ordinary roles. The instructions to each age and station show that the gospel reaches into marriage, work, speech, and reputation. A people zealous for good works adorns the teaching of God and gives outsiders nothing legitimate to slander.

Mercy, not merit, saves. The heart of the letter insists that God saved us according to his mercy, through the washing of rebirth and renewal by the Holy Spirit, so that we might become heirs with the hope of eternal life. Good works flow from salvation; they never earn it.

Takeaways

  • The grace that saves also schools; genuine faith shows up as a changed life that renounces ungodliness and pursues good in the everyday.
  • Leaders and members alike commend or discredit the gospel by their conduct, so a quiet life of integrity can adorn the message more than fine words.
  • Salvation rests entirely on God's mercy and the renewing work of the Spirit, freeing us to do good not to earn favor but in grateful response to it.

Connections

Titus closely parallels 1 Timothy from the previous day, sharing the same concern for qualified elders and the silencing of false teachers, evidence of Paul's common pattern for ordering young churches. The phrase that God saved us through the washing of rebirth recalls the new heart and new spirit promised in Ezekiel 36, where God pledges to cleanse his people and put his Spirit within them. Paul's hope of the blessed appearing of our great God and Savior Jesus Christ looks toward the same return that closes the whole biblical story in Revelation, and his stress on a people zealous for good works echoes Jesus' own call in Matthew 5 to let our light shine before others.

Reflection

It is easy to imagine grace as a soft permission and good works as a separate burden, but Paul binds them together: the grace that pardons is also the grace that trains. Consider one area of your life where you have received mercy freely and yet still live as though nothing changed. What would it look like for grace to teach you there, to renounce an old habit and take up a quiet good work? Paul reminds Titus that ordinary faithfulness, in the home and the workplace, adorns the gospel before a watching world. You do not do good to be saved; you do good because, by sheer mercy, you already are.
