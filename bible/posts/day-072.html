Deuteronomy 5-7

Love the LORD With All Your Heart

Read Deuteronomy 5-7 in the NIV →

Context

Deuteronomy is set on the plains of Moab, as a second generation of Israelites stands at the edge of the promised land. The first generation died in the wilderness for their unbelief, and Moses now re-presents the covenant to those who will actually cross the Jordan. These chapters open the long central sermon of the book.

Chapter 5 repeats the Ten Commandments first given at Sinai, with one notable shift: the Sabbath is grounded not in creation but in Israel's rescue from slavery in Egypt. Chapter 6 gives the Shema, named for its first Hebrew word, shema, meaning hear or listen. Chapter 7 commands Israel to dispossess the Canaanite nations and avoid intermarriage and idolatry, framed as the jealous love of a God who chose Israel not for its size but out of sheer faithfulness to His promise.

Key themes

Hearing as the heart of faith. The Shema does not begin with doing but with listening. To hear rightly is already to be drawn toward loving response. Israel is summoned first to attend to who God is before being told what to do.

Whole-person devotion. Loving God with heart, soul, and strength refuses to wall off religion from the rest of life. The command is total, reaching into thought, desire, and physical effort alike.

Formation through ordinary repetition. The words are to be taught to children, spoken at home and on the road, bound to hand and forehead, written on doorposts. Faith is handed down by saturating daily routine, not by occasional ceremony.

Chosen by grace, not merit. Israel is reminded that God set His love on them not because they were impressive but because He loved them and kept His oath to their ancestors. Election is gift, and it grounds the call to faithfulness.

Takeaways

  • The deepest command in Israel's faith is to love, and that love is meant to engage every part of a person rather than a religious compartment.
  • Faith is transmitted across generations through ordinary, repeated speech and practice in the home, not through extraordinary events.
  • Israel's standing rests on God's gracious choice and covenant promise, which is why obedience flows from gratitude rather than earning favor.

Connections

The repetition of the Ten Commandments looks back to Exodus 20, while the warnings against Canaanite idolatry echo the covenant warnings already given there. The Shema's call to love God with heart, soul, and strength is the command Jesus later names the greatest of all, and His own resistance to temptation in the wilderness quotes directly from these chapters. The grounding of Sabbath in the exodus connects to the recurring memory of slavery that shapes Israel's whole ethic of mercy.

Reflection

The Shema asks a quiet but searching question: what would it look like for the love of God to reach the parts of life we usually keep separate from Him, the conversations at the table, the drive to work, the words we leave on our children. Consider one ordinary corner of today where that love has not yet been spoken or practiced, and bring it deliberately into view.
