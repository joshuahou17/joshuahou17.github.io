Romans 1-3

The Righteousness of God Revealed: All Have Sinned

Read Romans 1-3 in the NIV →

Context

Romans, written from Corinth around AD 57 to a church Paul had not founded, is his most systematic exposition of the gospel, sent ahead of a hoped-for visit and a planned mission to Spain. The opening chapters lay the diagnostic foundation. After greeting the Roman believers and stating his theme, that the gospel reveals the righteousness of God from faith for faith, Paul turns to the bad news that makes the good news necessary.

From 1:18 onward he describes the wrath of God revealed against human suppression of the truth. The Gentile world, having known God through creation, exchanged His glory for idols and was given over to its desires. In chapter 2 Paul turns to the moralist and the Jew, warning that having the law without keeping it brings no advantage, since God judges impartially. Chapter 3 gathers a chain of Old Testament citations to declare that none is righteous, all are under sin, and the whole world is accountable to God, before unveiling the heart of the letter: justification freely by grace through faith in Jesus, whom God put forward as a sacrifice of atonement.

Key themes

The gospel as God's righteousness. The gospel reveals a righteousness from God received by faith, the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes, Jew first and also Greek. This sets the agenda for the whole letter.

Universal guilt. Both Gentile idolatry and Jewish presumption fall under judgment; God shows no partiality. By the law comes knowledge of sin, so that every mouth is stopped and the whole world held accountable.

Justification by faith. Apart from the law, the righteousness of God is now revealed through faith in Christ for all who believe. Sinners are justified freely by grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus, set forth as an atoning sacrifice.

Takeaways

  • The gospel is fundamentally about God's own righteousness, offered as saving power to everyone who believes, with no ethnic or moral precondition.
  • Guilt is universal: idolatrous Gentiles and law-possessing Jews alike stand condemned, so no one can claim a privileged exemption before an impartial God.
  • Justification comes freely by grace through faith in Christ's atoning death, not by works, leaving boasting excluded and grace magnified.

Connections

Paul's catena in 3:10-18 stitches together Psalms and Isaiah to prove universal sin, the same prophetic indictment of human corruption found across the Writings. His thesis that the righteous shall live by faith quotes Habakkuk, a verse that anchors his whole argument. The atonement imagery of Christ as the place of mercy recalls the mercy seat of the tabernacle in Leviticus, where blood was sprinkled for the people's sins on the Day of Atonement.

Habakkuk 2:4 → Psalm 14:1-3 → Isaiah 59:7-8 → Leviticus 16:14-15 → Genesis 1:20 →

Reflection

Romans begins by leveling the ground: the obviously wicked and the respectably moral both stand silenced before God. Which side of that line have you quietly assumed protects you? Only when the mouth is stopped does grace astonish rather than merely reassure. Where might you need to stop defending yourself and simply receive a righteousness you could never earn?
