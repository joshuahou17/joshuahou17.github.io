Day 24 of 365

Genesis 35-37

Return to Bethel, Death on the Road, a Dreamer Sold

Read Genesis 35-37 in the NIV →

Context

Genesis 35 brings Jacob full circle to Bethel, the site of his ladder dream decades earlier. He buries the foreign gods, builds an altar, and God reaffirms the name Israel and the covenant given to Abraham and Isaac. The chapter is shadowed by death: Rachel dies giving birth to Benjamin, and the patriarch Isaac is laid to rest by his reconciled sons Esau and Jacob.

Genesis 36 is a long register of Esau's descendants, the Edomites, signaling that God's promise of nations is being kept even outside the chosen line. Genesis 37 launches the longest sustained narrative in Genesis. Jacob's favoritism toward Joseph, the ornate robe, and Joseph's dreams of dominance ignite his brothers' hatred. They throw him into a pit, sell him to traders, and deceive their father with a blood-soaked coat.

Key themes

God keeps covenant across the generations. At Bethel the promise passes intact from Abraham to Isaac to Israel. Even the genealogy of Esau testifies that God's word of fruitfulness does not fail.

The corrosive fruit of favoritism. Jacob, himself the product of a favoring household, repeats the pattern with Joseph and reaps fraternal hatred. The dysfunction of one generation seeds the next.

Hidden providence in catastrophe. Joseph's dreams hint that God is at work even as the boy descends into slavery. The reader is invited to trust a plan the characters cannot yet see.

Takeaways

  • Returning to the place of an earlier encounter with God can be a way of renewing and reaffirming his promises over our lives.
  • Parental favoritism breeds resentment and division; love that plays favorites wounds a whole family.
  • What looks like the end of a story to those inside it may be the hidden beginning of God's deliverance.

Connections

Jacob's return to Bethel completes the vow he made there in the day 21 reading, when he first fled Esau. Rachel's death near Bethlehem and the birth of Benjamin set the stage for the twelve tribes now complete. Joseph's dreams of sheaves and stars bowing down will be fulfilled in the coming chapters when his brothers stand before him in Egypt. The selling of Joseph for silver anticipates a later betrayal for silver in the Gospels, a parallel early readers noticed.

Reflection

Joseph's brothers could see only the arrogant favorite they wanted gone; they could not see the deliverer they were unwittingly sending ahead of them. When you are tempted to write someone off, or when you feel thrown into a pit yourself, can you hold space for the possibility that God is weaving something you cannot yet perceive?
