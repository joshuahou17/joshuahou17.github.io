Jeremiah 26-29

Jeremiah 26-29: On Trial, the Yoke, and a Letter to the Exiles

Read Jeremiah 26-29 in the NIV →

Context

These chapters are largely narrative, giving us scenes from the conflict between Jeremiah and the religious establishment. In chapter 26 the priests and prophets demand his execution for preaching that the temple could fall like Shiloh; the elders save him by recalling the precedent of the prophet Micah, while another prophet, Uriah, is killed for the same message.

Chapters 27-28 stage the contest over the wooden yoke. Jeremiah wears one to say Judah must submit to Nebuchadnezzar; Hananiah breaks it and predicts the exile will end in two years. Jeremiah replies that the wooden yoke will become iron and that Hananiah will die within the year, which he does. Chapter 29 preserves Jeremiah's letter to those already deported in 597 BC.

Key themes

Truth on trial. The temple sermon exposes a false security: the people assumed God would never let his own house fall. Jeremiah's near-execution, and the actual killing of Uriah, show how dangerous it is to puncture a comfortable national religion.

Competing prophets, one fulfilled word. Hananiah's message is everything the people want, immediate relief and restored prestige, and Jeremiah even says amen to the hope of it. But the test of a prophet, as Deuteronomy taught, is whether the word comes true, and only Jeremiah's does. Sincerity is not the same as truth.

Settling into exile. The letter of chapter 29 is astonishing: build houses, plant gardens, marry, multiply, and seek the peace of Babylon, because in its welfare you will find your own. God's people are to be a blessing even in the city of their captors, not a resentful enclave waiting to leave.

A future and a hope. Within the same letter comes the famous promise of plans to prosper and not to harm, plans for a future and a hope. Its setting matters: it is spoken to people facing seventy years away from home, a hope that does not cancel the exile but carries them through it.

Takeaways

  • A religion that assumes God must protect our institutions can become an idol; Jeremiah's temple sermon shatters the illusion that sacred buildings guarantee divine favor.
  • The popular, comforting prophet is not necessarily the true one. Time and fulfillment, not crowd approval, vindicate the word of God.
  • God calls his people to seek the good of the very places they would rather escape. Faithfulness in exile means investing in, not withdrawing from, the surrounding city.

Connections

Jeremiah's appeal that the temple could become like Shiloh recalls 1 Samuel 4, where the ark's presence did not save Israel from defeat. The test for distinguishing true and false prophets is drawn straight from Deuteronomy 18:21-22. The call to seek the welfare of Babylon anticipates the New Testament posture toward governing authorities in 1 Peter 2 and Romans 13, and the letter's promise of a future and a hope reappears thematically in the Book of Consolation that follows in chapters 30-31.

Deuteronomy 18:21-22 → 1 Samuel 4:10-11 → 1 Peter 2:11-12 → Jeremiah 29:11 →

Reflection

Jeremiah 29:11 is often lifted off a greeting card, but its first readers were prisoners of war told to unpack their bags for seventy years. The hope is real precisely because it does not promise quick rescue; it promises that God's intentions outlast the exile. And the instruction just before it is harder than the promise: pray for the city that took everything from you, because your good is bound up with its good. Where is God asking you to settle in and bless a place you would rather leave?
