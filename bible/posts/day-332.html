Acts 18-19

Corinth and Ephesus: The Word Takes Root in the Cities

Read Acts 18-19 in the NIV →

Context

Acts 18 opens with Paul arriving in Corinth, a bustling Roman colony notorious for commerce and moral laxity, rebuilt by Julius Caesar a century earlier. There he meets Aquila and Priscilla, Jewish tentmakers recently expelled from Rome under Claudius, and joins them in the trade. The mention of Gallio, proconsul of Achaia, is one of the firmest anchors for dating Paul's ministry, since an inscription at Delphi places his term around AD 51-52.

The narrative then carries Paul to Ephesus, capital of the province of Asia and home to the temple of Artemis, one of the seven wonders of the ancient world. Acts 19 describes a ministry of roughly three years there, marked by extraordinary signs, a confrontation with itinerant exorcists, and finally a riot stirred by silversmiths whose idol trade was threatened. Luke frames both cities as strongholds where the gospel meets and overcomes entrenched cultural power.

Key themes

Divine encouragement in fear. In a night vision the Lord tells Paul not to be silent, for He has many people in the city. The promise sustains a frightened apostle and reframes evangelism as gathering people God already claims, not winning an uncertain gamble.

The word against magic and money. In Ephesus former practitioners of sorcery burn scrolls worth fifty thousand silver coins, and the riot erupts precisely because faith in Jesus threatens the economy of idol-making. The gospel does not stay private; it reorders what people trust and what they spend.

Right teaching completed. Apollos, eloquent but knowing only John's baptism, is taken aside by Priscilla and Aquila and taught more accurately. The episode shows the church correcting the gifted gently and shows that fervor needs fullness.

Takeaways

  • God's reassurance to Paul reframes mission as a harvest among people God already intends to save, which steadies courage rather than guaranteeing ease.
  • Genuine conversion in Ephesus touched wallets and worldviews; the burned scrolls show repentance that is costly and concrete, not merely sentimental.
  • The church's health depends on humble correction, seen in a couple privately deepening a gifted preacher's understanding without shaming him.

Connections

The night vision echoes God's repeated charge to earlier servants not to fear, given to Joshua before Jericho and to Jeremiah at his call. The burning of magical scrolls recalls the decisive break with idolatry urged throughout the prophets and Israel's covenant warnings in Deuteronomy. Gallio's refusal to judge a religious dispute parallels the pattern in Acts where Roman officials repeatedly find no crime in the Christians, a thread Luke develops from Paul's earlier trials.

Reflection

Where has fear made you go silent, and how might it change things to believe that the people around you are ones God may already be calling? The Ephesians counted the cost in coin and craft; consider what in your own life would have to be set on the fire if your trust in Christ became fully concrete rather than merely held in private.
