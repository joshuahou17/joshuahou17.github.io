Day 257 of 365

Daniel 4-6

Kings Brought Low

Read Daniel 4-6 in the NIV →

Context

These three chapters trace the rise and fall of two kings and the constancy of one servant. In chapter 4 Nebuchadnezzar boasts of his greatness, is struck with a humbling madness that drives him to live like a beast, and is restored only when he lifts his eyes to heaven and praises the Most High who rules over human kingdoms.

Chapter 5 shifts to Belshazzar, whose feast with the temple vessels is interrupted by a hand writing on the wall. Daniel reads the words: God has numbered the kingdom, weighed the king, and divided his realm. That night Babylon falls to the Persians. Chapter 6 then shows Daniel under Darius, framed by jealous officials into a death sentence for praying, thrown to the lions, and preserved by the God he never stopped serving.

These chapters are bound together by a single refrain that the Most High rules over the kingdoms of men and gives them to whomever he wills. Two proud kings learn this the hard way, while one faithful servant lives it out, so that the contrast between Babylonian arrogance and Daniel's steady devotion becomes the lesson the narrator most wants us to absorb.

Key themes

Pride invites the fall. Nebuchadnezzar and Belshazzar both grasp at glory that belongs to God, and both are brought down. The repeated lesson is that the Most High humbles those who walk in pride and raises those who acknowledge his rule.

God writes the final verdict. The hand on the wall makes visible what is always true: kings and kingdoms are weighed by God. Belshazzar's confidence collapses in a single night, exposing how thin earthly power really is.

Faithfulness costs and God keeps. Daniel prays as he always has, knowing the law is a trap, and accepts the den rather than hide his devotion. His deliverance is not a reward for cleverness but the keeping of a God who honors steady, public loyalty.

Takeaways

  • God opposes the proud and is willing to humble even the mightiest until they look up and acknowledge him.
  • Earthly power and security can be revoked in a night; only God's kingdom is unshakable.
  • Open, consistent devotion may invite danger, but the God who is served in private is the God who keeps in the den.

Connections

Nebuchadnezzar's humbling dramatizes the proverb that pride goes before a fall and Mary's song that God brings down rulers from their thrones in Luke 1. Belshazzar's profaning of the temple vessels recalls their theft in 2 Kings 25 and 2 Chronicles 36. Daniel's deliverance from the lions foreshadows the angel who shuts the lions' mouths in the language of Hebrews 11 and echoes the protection of the three friends in the previous reading. The fall of Babylon fulfills the prophecies of Isaiah and Jeremiah against that city.

Proverbs 16:18 → 2 Chronicles 36:18 → Jeremiah 51:39 → Luke 1:52 → Hebrews 11:33 →

Reflection

Belshazzar partied as his kingdom was being weighed. Where might you be living confidently on borrowed time, mistaking stability for permanence? And Daniel's habit of prayer was set long before the den; what daily devotion are you building now that would hold under real pressure?
