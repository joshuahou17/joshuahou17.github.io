Day 284 of 365

Matthew 9; Luke 7

Authority to Forgive, Power to Raise

Read Matthew 9 in the NIV → Read Luke 7 in the NIV →

Context

These chapters gather scenes that display Jesus' authority across the full range of human need: sin, sickness, social exclusion, death, and shame. Matthew records his own call from the tax booth, a striking inclusion since tax collectors were despised as collaborators with Rome and ritually unclean. Luke widens the frame with the centurion of Capernaum and the funeral at Nain, then the dinner at Simon the Pharisee's house.

Across both Gospels Jesus is repeatedly challenged on whom he associates with and what he claims for himself. To forgive sins is to do what only God can do; to dine with sinners is to scandalize the religious; to commend a Gentile soldier's faith above Israel's is to redraw the boundaries of God's people. The objections come fast, and Jesus turns each into an unveiling of mercy.

Key themes

Jesus forgives as God forgives. Healing the paralytic, he first says your sins are forgiven, prompting the charge of blasphemy, then heals the body to show his authority to forgive. The visible miracle authenticates the invisible mercy. The same authority appears when he tells the sinful woman her sins are forgiven, again drawing the question who is this.

The physician comes for the sick. Called to account for eating with tax collectors, Jesus answers that the healthy need no doctor; he came to call sinners, not the righteous. His table is for those who know they are ill, not those confident in their own health.

Faith and love rise from grace received. The centurion trusts a mere word from a distance, and Jesus marvels at faith greater than any in Israel. The woman weeps over Jesus' feet because she has been forgiven much; her love is the evidence, not the cause, of grace. Simon, forgiven little in his own estimation, loves little.

Takeaways

  • Jesus' power over disease and death is the outward sign of a deeper authority to forgive sins, the thing humanity most needs and only God can grant.
  • The gospel reaches outsiders first; a Roman officer's faith and a tax collector's call show the kingdom opening to those religion had written off.
  • Love for God grows in proportion to the forgiveness we know we have received; those who think they owe little tend to love little.

Connections

The raising of the widow's son at Nain deliberately echoes Elijah raising the widow of Zarephath's son in 1 Kings 17 and Elisha in 2 Kings 4, and the crowd rightly senses a great prophet has arisen. Jesus' answer about new wine and old wineskins looks back to the freshness of the kingdom breaking into old structures. Matthew's call extends the pattern begun with the fishermen, and the charge of blasphemy for forgiving sins anticipates the trial before the council. The centurion's faith foreshadows the Gentile mission that Acts will carry forward.

Reflection

Simon the Pharisee and the weeping woman were in the same room, near the same Jesus, but only one of them knew she needed him. The difference was not the size of their sin but the honesty of their self-knowledge. Consider where you stand: do you sense you have been forgiven much, or do you quietly assume you owe little? The measure of your love for Christ may tell you the answer more truly than your words do.
