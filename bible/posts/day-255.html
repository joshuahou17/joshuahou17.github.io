Day 255 of 365

Joel

When the Locusts Came

Read Joel in the NIV →

Context

Joel gives us almost no biographical detail, which has made his date debated, but his occasion is vivid. A locust swarm of unprecedented scale has stripped the land bare, ruining the harvest and even the grain and wine offerings of the temple. Joel reads this catastrophe as more than an agricultural disaster; it is a summons to repent before the Day of the LORD, a day of darkness and reckoning he sees approaching like an army.

The book moves in three movements: the plague and the call to lament and return to God; God's compassionate response, restoring the land and promising to pour out his Spirit on sons and daughters, old and young, servants and free; and a final judgment of the nations in the Valley of Decision, ending with the land flowing with abundance and God dwelling in Zion.

Joel's refusal to date himself sharply has freed his message to speak to every generation that faces sudden ruin. He takes a concrete, datable disaster and lets it become transparent to a larger reality, so that the swarming locusts and the advancing army and the coming Day of the LORD all bleed into one another in his urgent poetry.

Key themes

The Day of the LORD cuts both ways. For those who presume on God it is terror; for those who return to him it is rescue. Joel holds judgment and salvation together in a single day, refusing to let either swallow the other.

Repentance is inward before it is outward. Joel's most famous summons is to rend the heart and not the garments. He grounds the appeal in God's character: gracious, compassionate, slow to anger, and abounding in love, who may yet relent.

God restores what was lost. The promise to repay the years the locusts have eaten reframes ruin as reversible in God's hands. Devastation is real, but it is not the end of the story for a repentant people.

Takeaways

  • Disaster can be a megaphone calling us back to God rather than only a punishment to endure.
  • Genuine repentance is a matter of the heart, leaning on God's mercy rather than performing grief.
  • God specializes in restoring lost years, and his promised Spirit democratizes access to him across age, gender, and status.

Connections

Joel's promise that God will pour out his Spirit on all flesh is quoted by Peter at Pentecost in Acts 2 as fulfilled in the gift of the Holy Spirit. The description of God as gracious and compassionate and slow to anger draws directly on the self-revelation at Sinai in Exodus 34. The Day of the LORD theme links Joel to Amos, Zephaniah, and Malachi, and his closing river flowing from the house of the LORD anticipates Ezekiel 47, read just before this in the plan.

Exodus 34:6-7 → Acts 2:16-21 → Amos 5:18-20 → Ezekiel 47:1 → Romans 10:13 →

Reflection

Joel asks his people to read a hard season as an invitation rather than an abandonment. What loss in your life might God be using to call you back to himself? And do you tend to perform repentance outwardly while leaving your heart untouched, when the prophet pleads for exactly the reverse?
