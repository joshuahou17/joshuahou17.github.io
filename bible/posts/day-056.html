Day 56 of 365

Numbers 5-6

A Pure Camp, a Costly Vow, and the Face That Shines

Read Numbers 5-6 in the NIV →

Context

With the camp organized around God's presence, Numbers 5 addresses what threatens that holiness from within. The ritually unclean are sent outside the camp, wrongs against a neighbor require confession and restitution plus a fifth, and a strange ritual addresses a husband's jealousy when no witnesses exist, placing the accused wife's vindication or guilt in God's hands rather than human accusation alone.

Numbers 6 introduces the Nazirite vow, a voluntary consecration open to any man or woman who wishes to draw nearer to God for a set time. The Nazirite abstains from grape products, leaves the hair uncut, and avoids all contact with the dead, even of close family. Breaking the vow, even accidentally through proximity to death, requires starting over with new offerings. The chapter ends with the Aaronic benediction, the priestly words by which God's blessing and protection are spoken over Israel.

Key themes

Holiness within the community. The purity and restitution laws insist that sin against a neighbor is also sin against the LORD who dwells in the camp. Wrongs must be confessed and repaid. A holy God in the midst requires honesty and restored relationships among his people.

Voluntary consecration. The Nazirite vow is not commanded but offered, a way for ordinary Israelites to set themselves apart in heightened devotion. Its costliness, even forfeiting the right to mourn the dead, shows that drawing near to the living God reorders one's deepest loyalties.

Blessing freely given. The Aaronic benediction comes after all the laws of dedication, yet blessing is not earned by them. God says he will put his Name on the people and he himself will bless them. The repeated you underscores a God who delights to turn his shining face toward his own.

Takeaways

  • A holy God among his people makes honesty and restitution toward one another a spiritual matter, not merely a social one.
  • Costly, voluntary devotion has a place in faith, but it flows toward God rather than earning his favor, as the freely spoken blessing makes clear.
  • The Aaronic blessing reveals God's heart, he longs to make his face shine on his people and to give them peace, putting his very Name upon them.

Connections

The Aaronic blessing of Numbers 6 echoes through the Psalms, where the worshiper prays for God to make his face shine, and it reappears as the oldest written biblical text yet found, on the silver scrolls of Ketef Hinnom. The Nazirite vow shapes the births of Samson and Samuel and the description of John the Baptist. The longing for God's shining face finds its fullest answer in Paul's claim that God's glory shines in the face of Jesus Christ, the one in whom God truly turns toward us.

Psalm 67:1 → Judges 13:4-5 → 1 Samuel 1:11 → Luke 1:15 → 2 Corinthians 4:6 →

Reflection

Numbers 6 ends with words still spoken in worship today, the LORD bless you and keep you, make his face shine upon you, and give you peace. After chapters of laws guarding holiness, the final note is God's eagerness to bless. Sit with the image of God turning his face toward you, not in anger but in favor. Whom in your life might you bless with words of grace, becoming a channel of the very peace God desires to give?
