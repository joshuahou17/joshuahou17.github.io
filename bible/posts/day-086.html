Day 86 of 365

Joshua 16-18

The Slow Work of Possession

Read Joshua 16-18 in the NIV →

Context

These chapters continue the long survey of tribal allotments that fills the second half of Joshua. Chapters 16 and 17 hand the central hill country to the descendants of Joseph, split between his two sons, Ephraim and Manasseh, whose names had been formally adopted as tribes back in Genesis. Their territory stretches from the Jordan toward the Mediterranean, a broad and fertile heartland that would later hold Shechem and Shiloh.

Two friction points surface. First, both Ephraim and Manasseh fail to drive out the Canaanites living among them, settling instead for forced labor. Second, the house of Joseph complains that one allotment is too small for so numerous a people. Joshua does not enlarge their portion. He tells them to clear the forested highlands and dispossess the Canaanites despite their iron chariots, exposing that the real shortage is courage, not acreage.

Chapter 18 marks a turning point. The whole assembly gathers at Shiloh and sets up the tent of meeting, and seven tribes have still not received their inheritance. Joshua's question lands like a rebuke: how long will you put off going in to take possession of the land the LORD has given you? Surveyors map the remaining territory, lots are cast before the LORD, and Benjamin receives the first of these final portions.

Key themes

Gift and effort held together. The land is repeatedly called something the LORD has given, already settled by divine grant. Yet it still must be entered, cleared, and held. The allotments are promise made concrete, but possession requires the people to act on what is freely given rather than waiting passively for it to fall into their hands.

Fear disguised as grievance. Joseph's tribes frame their problem as too little land, but Joshua hears it as too little faith. The Canaanite chariots of iron loom large in their imagination. The text quietly insists that the obstacle to inheritance is often internal, a reluctance to engage the hard work that promise demands.

Worship at the center. Pitching the tent of meeting at Shiloh plants God's presence in the middle of the tribes before the final distribution proceeds. The name Shiloh evokes rest, and the move signals that the conquest phase is yielding to settled life organized around the LORD's dwelling.

Delay and its cost. The seven idle tribes embody a spiritual hazard: holding an inheritance in title while never enjoying it in practice. Joshua's prod toward action treats lingering as a failure of stewardship, not a neutral pause.

Takeaways

  • A promise from God can be fully secured in title yet only partly enjoyed in practice, and the gap is usually closed by faithful effort rather than by waiting.
  • When we complain that our portion is too small, the deeper issue is sometimes fear of the work or the opposition involved in claiming what we already have.
  • Placing worship at the center, as Israel did at Shiloh, reorders a community's life so that everything else, including how resources are divided, flows from God's presence.

Connections

The adoption of Ephraim and Manasseh as full tribes reaches back to Genesis, where the aged Jacob crossed his hands to bless the younger Ephraim above the firstborn, and that reversal echoes here as Ephraim takes the leading role. The daughters of Zelophehad, who reappear in chapter 17, were first granted their inheritance in Numbers, the case that established daughters could inherit when there were no sons.

The unconquered Canaanites recall the warnings of Deuteronomy, where God promised to drive out the nations little by little and cautioned that any left behind would become snares. The same complacency surfaces in Judges, where the tribes' failure to dispossess the land becomes the engine of that book's downward spiral.

Joshua's question, how long will you wait, anticipates a recurring biblical summons against spiritual procrastination, the call to act on grace already given.

Reflection

It is striking that the danger in these chapters is not defeat but drift. Seven tribes hold a deed signed by God and do nothing with it. The land is theirs, mapped and assigned, and still they linger at Shiloh as if possession will happen on its own. Joshua's pointed question is a mercy: it names the inertia that comfort breeds.

Most of us know this temptation. We can be secure in a promise, even grateful for it, and yet never step into the territory it opens. The forested hills go uncleared because the chariots look formidable, and we rename our fear as a complaint about circumstances.

The invitation is to treat what God has given as something to be entered, not merely admired. Where has grace already granted you ground you have not yet walked? The work of taking hold is rarely glamorous and often slow, but it is the shape faith takes once the gift has been promised.
