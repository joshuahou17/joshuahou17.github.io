Jeremiah 32-34

Jeremiah 32-34: A Field Bought in a Doomed City

Read Jeremiah 32-34 in the NIV →

Context

The setting is the final siege, in the ninth year of King Zedekiah, with Jeremiah confined in the courtyard of the guard for predicting the city's fall. Into this hopeless moment comes a strange command: his cousin will offer to sell him the family field at Anathoth, and Jeremiah is to buy it.

He does, carefully signing and sealing the deed and storing it in a clay jar for safekeeping. Chapter 33 expands the hope into promises of healing, cleansing, and a renewed Davidic and Levitical line. Chapter 34 turns darker, recounting how the people freed their Hebrew slaves under pressure of the siege, then re-enslaved them when the threat seemed to lift, profaning God's name.

Key themes

Hope you can put money on. Buying a field in a country about to be overrun is economic nonsense, and that is the point. The signed, sealed, witnessed deed is a tangible bet on God's promise that fields will be bought here again. Faith here takes the form of a legal transaction.

Nothing too hard for God. Jeremiah prays in chapter 32 and confesses that nothing is too hard for the Lord, the Creator who made heaven and earth. God answers by repeating the phrase back to him as a question, anchoring the whole promise of restoration in his power over what looks impossible.

Restoration in full color. Chapter 33 piles up images of return: health, cleansing, joy, the voices of bride and bridegroom again in the streets, a righteous Branch for David, and priests to serve. The judgment is severe but it is not the last word.

A covenant of freedom betrayed. Chapter 34 is a grim case study. Under siege the people obey the law releasing Hebrew slaves; once Babylon's army withdraws briefly, they take their freed servants back. God treats this reversal as covenant treachery, proclaiming them a bitter freedom: liberty to fall by sword, plague, and famine.

Takeaways

  • Real faith sometimes looks irrational to the world. Jeremiah's land purchase staked his money on a promise no eye could yet see, turning hope into a concrete act.
  • The God who made heaven and earth finds nothing too hard, which is the ground for trusting restoration even when the situation appears terminal.
  • Obedience under pressure that is reversed once the pressure lifts is not obedience at all; chapter 34 exposes how crisis-driven repentance collapses when fear recedes.

Connections

Jeremiah's confession that nothing is too hard for God echoes the word to Abraham and Sarah in Genesis 18:14 and reappears in Gabriel's words to Mary in Luke 1:37. The slave-release law the people abused comes from the sabbatical provisions of Exodus 21 and Deuteronomy 15, read earlier in this plan. The righteous Branch promised again in 33:15 continues the thread from Jeremiah 23 and points to the Davidic covenant of 2 Samuel 7.

Genesis 18:14 → Deuteronomy 15:12-15 → Jeremiah 23:5 → Luke 1:37 →

Reflection

Jeremiah signed a deed for land he might never walk, in a city about to burn, because God said houses and fields would be bought here again. That is what hope looks like when it costs something: not a feeling but a signature. The contrast in chapter 34 is just as pointed. It is easy to do right when the enemy is at the gate and hard to keep doing it when the danger eases. Which kind of obedience has your fear been producing, and would it survive the army pulling back?
