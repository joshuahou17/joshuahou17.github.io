Ezekiel 34-36

Ezekiel 34-36: The True Shepherd, the New Heart, and a Land Reborn

Read Ezekiel 34-36 in the NIV →

Context

These chapters open the restoration half of Ezekiel. Chapter 34 condemns Israel's leaders as bad shepherds who exploited the sheep, then unveils God as the shepherd who will seek the lost and bind the injured, with a coming Davidic shepherd over them. The shepherd image in the ancient Near East commonly meant kings, and Ezekiel extends it to all who are entrusted with God's people.

Chapter 35 pronounces judgment on Edom (Mount Seir) for its hostility, clearing the ground for chapter 36's promise to the mountains of Israel. There God pledges to gather his scattered people, sprinkle clean water on them, replace their heart of stone with a heart of flesh, and put his own Spirit within them so they can finally walk in his ways.

Key themes

Shepherds who fed themselves. Israel's leaders ate the fat, wore the wool, and let the flock scatter. God holds leadership accountable for the weak it neglected. Authority exists to feed the sheep, not the shepherd.

God the seeking shepherd. I myself will search for my sheep, God says. He will bind up the broken and strengthen the sick. The tenderness here, the God who goes out after the lost one, runs straight into Jesus' parable of the lost sheep and his claim to be the good shepherd.

A new heart and a new spirit. God promises to remove the heart of stone and give a heart of flesh, putting his Spirit within so the people will obey from the inside. This is regeneration, not reform; the cure for disobedience is a transformed interior, the work of the Spirit.

For the sake of my name. God restores not because Israel has earned it but to vindicate his holy name among the nations. Grace here is rooted in God's own character, which both humbles the recipient and makes the gift secure.

Takeaways

  • Leadership is stewardship of others' wellbeing; God measures leaders by how the weakest in their care are treated.
  • God does not wait passively for the lost; he goes out and seeks, binds wounds, and carries the weary home.
  • Real change starts inside; God's promise is not merely better behavior but a new heart and his own Spirit within us.

Connections

The good-shepherd promise flowers in John 10, where Jesus claims the title, and in Luke 15's parable of the lost sheep. The new heart and new spirit join Jeremiah 31's new covenant and are fulfilled at Pentecost in Acts 2, when the Spirit is poured out. Sprinkling clean water anticipates the language of baptism and Hebrews 10:22. The Davidic shepherd over the flock points to Jesus, son of David.

John 10:11-16 → Jeremiah 31:31-34 → Luke 15:4-7 → Ezekiel 11:19-20 → Acts 2:38-39 →

Reflection

If I lead anyone, at work, at home, in a community, chapter 34 asks whether I am feeding the flock or feeding myself. And chapter 36 offers a deeper hope for the parts of me that will not change by willpower: God promises not just to demand a better heart but to give one, exchanging stone for flesh and placing his own Spirit within. What in me am I still trying to reform that he is offering to make new?
