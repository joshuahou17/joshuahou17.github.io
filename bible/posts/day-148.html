Psalms 111-118

The Songs Jesus Sang

Read Psalms 111-118 in the NIV →

Context

These six psalms travel together. Jewish tradition groups Psalms 113-118 as the Egyptian Hallel, from the Hebrew word hallel, meaning praise. They were chanted at the major festivals, above all at Passover, the meal that rehearsed God's rescue of Israel out of Egyptian slavery. By long custom Psalms 113-114 were sung before the meal and Psalms 115-118 afterward.

That liturgical placement is why these psalms reach into the Gospels. When Matthew reports that Jesus and the disciples sang a hymn before going out to the Mount of Olives, the hymn was almost certainly the closing portion of this Hallel. The words about deliverance, the cup of salvation, and a rejected stone made the head of the corner were on Jesus' lips as he walked toward his arrest.

The collection moves from a wide-angle hymn to the God who stoops down, through the cosmic drama of the exodus, into trust over idols, gratitude for rescue from death, a brief summons for all nations to praise, and finally a processional thanksgiving that the early church read straight through as a portrait of the Messiah.

Key themes

The Egyptian Hallel. The unifying note across all six psalms is praise grounded in memory. Israel did not praise in the abstract; it praised because God had heard slaves cry and had brought them out. Psalm 113 sets the key by describing a God enthroned on high who nevertheless bends down to lift the poor from the dust and seat them with princes. Greatness and tenderness meet in the same God.

Exodus as cosmic upheaval. Psalm 114 retells the rescue in vivid, almost playful imagery. The sea looked and fled, the Jordan turned back, the mountains skipped like rams. Creation itself recoils before the presence of the Lord. The point is not poetry for its own sake but the claim that when God acts to save, the whole order of nature answers to him.

Rescue from death and the cost of devotion. Psalms 115-116 turn from history to the worshiper's own life. Idols have mouths but cannot speak; the living God acts. The singer of Psalm 116 has been brought back from the brink of the grave and asks what he can give in return, lifting the cup of salvation and calling precious the death of God's faithful ones.

The rejected stone. Psalm 118 closes the set with a festal procession of thanksgiving. Its central image, the stone the builders rejected becoming the cornerstone, became one of the most quoted texts in the New Testament, applied directly to Jesus' rejection and vindication. Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord rang out at the triumphal entry from this very psalm.

Takeaways

  • Biblical praise is rooted in remembered rescue; the Hallel teaches Israel to celebrate by recalling what God has actually done.
  • The same exalted God who is enthroned on high is the one who stoops to lift the poor and the dying, holding majesty and mercy together.
  • When Jesus sang Psalm 118 about the rejected stone on the night before his death, he was reading his own coming rejection and vindication into Israel's oldest songs of deliverance.

Connections

The whole Hallel looks back to the events the reading plan has already covered in Exodus, when God brought Israel through the Red Sea and Moses sang the Song of the Sea. Psalm 114's fleeing sea and parted Jordan also recall Joshua leading the people across the riverbed into the land. The festival these psalms accompany was instituted in Exodus 12 at the first Passover.

The New Testament saturates Psalm 118 with messianic weight. Jesus quotes the rejected-stone verse against the religious leaders in the Synoptic Gospels; Peter repeats it before the Sanhedrin in Acts; and it shapes the cornerstone language of Ephesians and 1 Peter. The crowd's Hosanna at the triumphal entry is drawn word for word from this psalm, so that Israel's Passover song became the soundtrack of Christ's passion.

Reflection

Jesus sang these psalms knowing where the night would lead. He praised the God who lifts the poor from the dust and turns back the sea while walking deliberately toward betrayal and a cross. The Hallel reminds us that worship is not a denial of suffering but a memory of rescue strong enough to carry us into it.

Consider singing or praying through Psalm 116 when life feels precarious. Its question, what shall I render to the Lord for all his benefits, is answered not with payment but with a lifted cup and a renewed vow, the same posture Jesus took at the table that night.
