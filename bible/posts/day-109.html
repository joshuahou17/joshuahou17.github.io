Day 109 of 365

1 Samuel 28-31; Psalms 18

The King's Last Night and the Song of Deliverance

Read 1 Samuel 28-31 in the NIV → Read Psalms 18 in the NIV →

Context

Saul's story reaches its grim end. In chapter 28, with Samuel dead and God silent through dream, prophet, and Urim, a terrified Saul disguises himself and seeks out the very mediums he had outlawed, summoning the witch of Endor on the eve of battle. Samuel appears and pronounces final judgment: tomorrow Saul and his sons will be with him, and Israel will fall to the Philistines.

Chapter 29 returns to David, mercifully spared the impossible position of fighting against Israel when the Philistine commanders distrust him and send him home from the battle line. Chapter 30 finds Ziklag burned and its families captured by Amalekite raiders; David, strengthening himself in the Lord his God, inquires of God, pursues, recovers everyone, and establishes a just rule for sharing spoil. Chapter 31 narrates the slaughter at Gilboa, where Saul, wounded and surrounded, falls on his own sword, and his body is fastened to the wall of Beth Shan until the men of Jabesh Gilead reclaim it.

Psalm 18, also preserved in 2 Samuel 22, is David's mature retrospective, composed when the Lord had delivered him from all his enemies and from Saul. It piles up titles for God, rock, fortress, deliverer, shield, and describes a cosmic rescue: God parting the heavens and drawing David out of mighty waters.

Key themes

The end of self-willed rule. Saul's seance is the final symptom of a life that turned from God's word; having forsaken the Lord, he ends by consulting the dead. His tragedy is self-authored, the harvest of long rebellion.

Strengthening oneself in the Lord. At Ziklag, with his men ready to stone him, David does the opposite of Saul: he turns to God, inquires, and acts. The contrast between the two kings is sharpest at their lowest points.

God the warrior-deliverer. Psalm 18 portrays salvation as God's mighty intervention. The many names for God testify to a deliverance both known intellectually and felt in the bones after years of danger.

Vindication of the upright. The psalm reflects on God rewarding David's integrity, his refusal to seize the throne by violence, with deliverance, framing the whole fugitive ordeal as God keeping faith with the faithful.

Takeaways

  • A life that persistently turns from God's voice can end by grasping at counterfeits in the dark.
  • When everything collapses, the decisive move is to strengthen ourselves in the Lord and seek his direction.
  • God's deliverance is worth remembering in detail; naming who he has been builds faith for what is ahead.
  • Integrity maintained through long testing is something God sees and ultimately vindicates.

Connections

Saul's recourse to the medium fulfills Samuel's earlier word that rebellion is as the sin of divination, closing the arc of rejection begun several readings ago. David strengthening himself in the Lord at Ziklag stands in deliberate contrast to Saul's despair at Endor. Psalm 18 gathers up the refuge and fortress language of all the wilderness psalms into one grand thanksgiving, and its near-identical twin in 2 Samuel 22 will frame David's reign. The dramatic rescue from deep waters echoes the exodus deliverance and anticipates God's pattern of saving his anointed.

Reflection

Two kings hit bottom in this reading, and the difference is everything: one seeks the dead in the dark, the other strengthens himself in the living God. When the ground gives way beneath me, which instinct rules? Psalm 18 invites me to rehearse, name by name, the God who has reached down before, and to trust that the same hand still draws his people out of deep waters.
