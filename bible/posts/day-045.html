Leviticus 5-7

Guilt, Restitution, and the Priest's Portion

Read Leviticus 5-7 in the NIV →

Context

These chapters complete the catalog of offerings begun in chapter 1 and then shift audience. The first half addresses the worshiper, describing the guilt offering for specific trespasses, including those against a neighbor's property, where the wrong must be repaid in full plus a fifth.

Notably, the law makes provision for the poor. Those who cannot afford a lamb may bring two birds, and the truly destitute may bring a measure of flour. Access to atonement is not gated by wealth.

The second half is a priest's manual. It revisits each offering from the perspective of the officiant, specifying which portions are burned, which the priests eat, and how holiness is maintained. The fire on the altar is to burn continually and never go out.

Key themes

Restitution joins repentance. The guilt offering pairs a sacrifice with concrete repayment to the wronged party. Reconciliation with God is not divorced from making things right with people; both are required.

Atonement scaled to means. The graduated provisions for the poor reveal a God whose forgiveness cannot be priced out of reach. Sincerity, not wealth, is what matters.

The perpetual fire. The command to keep the altar fire always burning pictures worship as continual, not occasional. Devotion is meant to be tended like a flame rather than lit only in emergencies.

Takeaways

  • True repentance restores broken relationships with people, not only with God, so confession and restitution belong together.
  • God's forgiveness is accessible to everyone regardless of resources, which means no one is too poor or too far gone to draw near.
  • Worship is to be continual like the ever-burning altar fire, a tended devotion rather than a sporadic gesture.

Connections

The restitution principle here, repaying plus a fifth, anticipates Zacchaeus restoring fourfold in Luke 19 and reflects the broader biblical link between love of God and love of neighbor that Leviticus 19 will state outright. The priests eating their portion of the offerings is the background Paul draws on when he says those who serve at the altar share in the altar. The graded offerings for the poor recur when Mary and Joseph bring two birds at Jesus' presentation.

Reflection

Is there a relationship where you have sought forgiveness from God but never made things right with the person you wronged? The guilt offering insists the two belong together. And consider the perpetual fire: what would it mean to tend your devotion daily rather than only when crisis strikes?
