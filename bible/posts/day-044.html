Leviticus 1-4

How to Draw Near

Read Leviticus 1-4 in the NIV →

Context

Exodus left Moses unable to enter the tent because the glory filled it. Leviticus answers the question that silence raised: how can sinful people live near a holy God without being consumed? The book opens with God speaking from the very tent Moses could not enter, and the first word of instruction is about offerings.

The Hebrew word often translated "sacrifice" is rooted in the idea of drawing near. These are not primarily mechanisms to appease an angry deity but God's gracious provision for nearness. He tells his people exactly how to come.

Chapters 1 through 4 lay out the burnt offering of total dedication, the grain offering of thanksgiving, the fellowship offering shared as a meal, and the sin offering that addresses unintentional wrongdoing. Each has its own logic, and together they form a vocabulary of worship.

Key themes

Sacrifice as drawing near. The offerings are God's answer to the longing to be close to him. Far from arbitrary ritual, they are an invitation, structuring a relationship that would otherwise be impossible between the holy and the unholy.

Total dedication. The burnt offering is consumed whole, holding nothing back. It dramatizes a life given entirely to God and becomes a "pleasing aroma," the fragrance of devotion rather than the smell of meat.

Sin has a cost, and atonement is provided. The sin offering teaches that even unintentional wrongdoing disrupts fellowship and requires blood. Yet the same God who names the problem provides the remedy, so the system is finally about grace.

Takeaways

  • Worship is fundamentally about drawing near to God, and he himself supplies the way, so access is a gift before it is a duty.
  • The burnt offering models a life held back from nothing, asking whether we offer God the whole or only the leftovers.
  • Sin is costly even when unintentional, and the gracious provision of atonement points beyond itself to a fuller answer.

Connections

These offerings presuppose the tabernacle just completed in Exodus 40 and the glory that filled it. The repeated "pleasing aroma" recalls Noah's sacrifice after the flood in Genesis 8. The New Testament reads the whole sacrificial system as a shadow fulfilled in Christ, who offered himself once for all, and Romans calls believers to be "living sacrifices," echoing the burnt offering's logic of total surrender.

Exodus 40:34-35 → Genesis 8:20-21 → Hebrews 10:1-10 → Romans 12:1 → Ephesians 5:2 →

Reflection

Leviticus assumes that the deepest human need is to be near God and that nearness costs something. As you read these ancient instructions, where do you sense a desire to draw near, and where do you hold back? Consider what it means that the offering you could never make has been made for you.
