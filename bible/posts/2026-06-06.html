The Heart of the Gospel: From Passion to Commission

Context: The Culmination of Matthew's Gospel

These final three chapters form what commentators call "the final and longest continuous narrative section that reaches its climax with another address, in this case a very brief but important one (Matthew 28:18-20)"—the Great Commission. The key phrase "And it came about that when Jesus had finished" (Matthew 26:1) indicates another major transition (cf. Matthew 7:28; Matthew 11:1; Matthew 13:53; Matthew 19:1), moving from discourse to the most significant events in human history.

Matthew has carefully structured his Gospel around Jesus' identity as the promised Messiah-King. The passion narrative demonstrates how Jesus assumes his throne not through political conquest but through suffering, death, and resurrection. This dramatic reversal challenges every human assumption about power and authority.

The Institution of the New Covenant (26:26-28)

At the heart of the Last Supper stands Jesus' radical reinterpretation of the Passover meal. "The Jewish Passover was made to melt into the Lord's supper, as the stars of the morning dissolve into the light of the sun. As they were eating, while the paschal supper was proceeding, Jesus instituted the new memorial which is to be observed until he comes again". The blood of the new covenant explicitly echoes "the history of Exodus 24:4-8. The blood which the Son of Man was about to shed was to be to the true Israel of God what the blood which Moses had sprinkled on the people had been to the outward Israel".

This covenant language is crucial—"Christ's blood was not shed because men were already forgiven but in order that they might be forgiven. Christ did not die because men were already saved but in order that they might, as a result of his death, receive salvation". The phrase "for the remission of sins" becomes the theological foundation for understanding both Jesus' atoning work and the church's sacramental life.

The Mystery of Divine Sovereignty and Human Weakness

Gethsemane reveals the profound mystery of Christ's humanity and divinity converging in ultimate submission to the Father's will. Jesus' agonized prayer, "Nevertheless, not as I will, but as thou wilt" (26:39), demonstrates that salvation was accomplished not through divine coercion but through willing obedience—even unto death. Meanwhile, the disciples' repeated failure to "watch one hour" exposes "the general weakness and defenselessness of men apart from Christ... The inference in this passage, then, is not the relative weakness of his disciples as compared with others, but the awful weakness of all people apart from their only Saviour and Redeemer".

Peter's threefold denial—predicted by Jesus yet devastating in its fulfillment—illustrates this spiritual reality. The cock's crow becomes a symbol of both human failure and divine foreknowledge, pointing toward the restoration that resurrection will bring.

The Cosmic Significance of the Cross (27:45-54)

Matthew presents the crucifixion as a moment of cosmic upheaval. The three hours of darkness, Jesus' cry of dereliction quoting Psalm 22:1, and the temple veil's tearing from top to bottom signal that "would have been clear signs to the Jews that the present age was ending and the age to come had begun... The Gospel according to Matthew answers with a resounding yes. Jesus' crucifixion was the deathblow for a world system founded on pretensions of human power and wisdom. His resurrection marks the definitive intrusion of God's ways into the world".

The centurion's confession—"Truly this was the Son of God"—represents the first Gentile recognition of Jesus' divine identity, foreshadowing the universal scope of the gospel that will emerge in chapter 28.

Resurrection and Authority (28:1-20)

Matthew's resurrection account emphasizes divine power triumphing over human authority. The earthquake, the angel's appearance "like lightning," and the guards becoming "like dead men" demonstrate that "Matthew's resurrection story contrasts the life-giving power of God with death-dealing human authority". The women's faithful witness—despite their "fear and great joy"—establishes the pattern for gospel proclamation: testimony born from personal encounter with the risen Christ.

The Great Commission (28:18-20) represents the culmination of Matthew's entire Gospel. "The Great Commission sets forth the reason and the way to make disciples of Jesus. The reason is because Jesus now has all authority in heaven and on earth". This commission fulfills "God's promise that all peoples on earth would be blessed through His people (Genesis 12:3). These words of Jesus Christ also reflect what the Old Testament says about the 'light' of salvation reaching 'the ends of the earth' (Isaiah 49:6)".

Cross-References to Previous Readings

These chapters culminate themes present throughout our reading journey. The Abrahamic covenant's universal scope (Genesis 12:1-3, Day 23) finds its fulfillment in the Great Commission's call to "all nations." The Passover imagery recalls Israel's exodus (celebrated in Joshua's conquest narratives, Days 10, 17, 24, 31), now superseded by the greater exodus Christ accomplishes through his death and resurrection.

The theme of covenant blood connects to the Mosaic covenant (referenced in our Law readings) while establishing the "new covenant" Jeremiah prophesied (echoing Isaiah's Servant Songs from Day 62, 69). Jesus' submission in Gethsemane fulfills the obedient faith modeled by Abraham at Moriah (Day 37) and contrasts with Adam's disobedience that Paul explored in Romans 5-6 (Day 15).

Reflection: The Gospel's Transformative Power

Matthew 26-28 forces us to confront the scandalous nature of the gospel—that God's greatest victory comes through apparent defeat, that divine power manifests supremely in weakness, and that the world's salvation hangs on a crucified Messiah. The resurrection doesn't merely reverse the crucifixion; it validates its necessity and demonstrates its efficacy.

For contemporary disciples, these chapters challenge us to embrace the same paradox: our greatest strength comes through acknowledging our weakness, our authority flows from submission to Christ's lordship, and our mission—making disciples of all nations—depends not on our capability but on Jesus' promise: "I am with you always, even unto the end of the world." The Gospel doesn't end with resurrection but with commission, calling us into the ongoing work of extending Christ's kingdom until he returns in glory.
