Hebrews 1-6

God Has Spoken in His Son

Read Hebrews 1-6 in the NIV →

Context

Hebrews reads less like a letter than a carefully crafted sermon, addressed to Jewish Christians tempted to drift back from Christ under the pressure of persecution and the pull of their ancestral faith. Its opening sentence sets the trajectory of the whole book: in the past God spoke through the prophets, but in these last days he has spoken by his Son, the radiance of God's glory and the exact imprint of his nature, who made purification for sins and sat down at the right hand of God.

These early chapters build a layered argument for the supremacy of Christ. He is greater than the angels, through whom the law was given, yet he was made lower than the angels for a little while so that, by the grace of God, he might taste death for everyone and become a merciful and faithful high priest. He is greater than Moses, faithful as a son over God's house rather than as a servant within it. Interwoven with the argument are urgent warnings drawn from Israel's wilderness rebellion: do not harden your hearts as in the day of testing, but strive to enter the rest that remains for the people of God.

Key themes

The Son is God's final word. Hebrews stacks up the ways God spoke before, then declares them all surpassed in the Son who is heir of all things and sustains the universe by his powerful word. Revelation reaches its climax not in a new message but in a person.

The danger of drifting. The sermon repeatedly pauses to warn. Because so great a salvation has been announced, neglect and unbelief are deadly serious; the generation that fell in the wilderness stands as a sobering pattern for any who would turn away from the living God.

A high priest who shares our weakness. Astonishingly, the exalted Son became fully human, was tempted as we are, and suffered, so that he could be a merciful and faithful high priest able to help those who are being tempted. His glory and his solidarity with us belong together.

Takeaways

  • God's clearest and final self-revelation is Jesus himself; to know the Son is to hear what God has most wanted to say.
  • Faith can be lost not only by dramatic rejection but by quiet neglect, so the call is to pay closer attention and not drift away from what we have heard.
  • Because Jesus became fully human and was tempted yet without sin, he is not a distant figure but a sympathetic high priest you can approach with confidence in your weakness.

Connections

Hebrews is saturated with the Old Testament read through Christ. Its warning against hardening hearts quotes Psalm 95 and reaches back to the wilderness rebellion of Numbers 14, where a whole generation forfeited the promised rest. The exaltation of the Son draws on Psalm 2 and Psalm 110, the royal psalms that picture God's anointed seated at his right hand. The comparison with Moses recalls Numbers 12, where Moses is called faithful in all God's house, a faithfulness Hebrews says Jesus surpasses as the Son over the house rather than a servant within it.

Psalm 95:7-11 → Psalm 110:1 → Psalm 2:7 → Numbers 14:20-23 → Numbers 12:7 →

Reflection

The peril Hebrews names is not loud apostasy but slow drift, the way a small boat slips from its mooring while no one is watching. Where might you be drifting, not by deciding against Christ but simply by neglecting him? The remedy the writer offers is not gritted willpower but a clearer look at Jesus, the one who is both the radiance of God's glory and the brother who was tempted as you are. Because he has gone before you and sympathizes with your weakness, you can draw near rather than pull away. Pay closer attention today to the word God has spoken in his Son.
