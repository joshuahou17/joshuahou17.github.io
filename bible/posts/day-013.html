Day 13 of 365

Job 35-37

Does God Care, and Can We Touch Him?

Read Job 35-37 in the NIV →

Context

These chapters continue Elihu's uninterrupted speech. Having introduced himself, he now turns to answer Job's deepest complaints directly: that righteousness seems to gain nothing, and that God will not respond to his cries.

Elihu argues that human sin and human virtue do not enlarge or diminish God; they affect other people. He then explains why God sometimes seems silent: people cry out under oppression but do not truly seek God, asking Where is God my Maker, who gives songs in the night?

The final movement of this section builds toward a sustained meditation on God's power in the natural world. Elihu watches a gathering storm and reads in the thunder, lightning, and rain the fingerprints of a God too great to summon or to fully comprehend.

Key themes

God's transcendence. Elihu insists the Almighty is beyond our reach, exalted in power, and not obligated to explain himself. Human beings cannot put God in the dock. This is both humbling and, oddly, freeing.

The motive behind our prayers. Elihu observes that people cry out in pain but rarely cry out for God himself. There is a difference between wanting relief and wanting the One who gives it.

Creation as sermon. The thunderstorm becomes a pulpit. Elihu reads God's majesty in clouds and lightning, arguing that if we cannot grasp the weather, we have no standing to second-guess God's governance of our lives.

Takeaways

  • Our goodness does not put God in our debt, and our failures do not diminish him; both ripple outward to the people around us.
  • There is a difference between crying out for relief and seeking the God who gives songs in the night.
  • The created world is meant to enlarge our sense of God, putting our demands for explanation in proportion.

Connections

Elihu's doxology of the storm prepares directly for the next reading, where God answers Job out of a whirlwind using the same strategy: a tour of creation that dwarfs human understanding. His phrase about God who gives songs in the night resonates with the night-vigil laments of the Psalms. The insistence that God is too great to be summoned echoes the warnings against presuming upon God that recur throughout the wisdom literature.

Reflection

Elihu's hardest question is not about God's power but about our motives. When we pray under pressure, are we seeking rescue or seeking God? It is an uncomfortable distinction, because relief is a perfectly human thing to want. Yet the question lingers: would we still want God if he offered himself without immediately fixing our circumstances?
