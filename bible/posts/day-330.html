Day 330 of 365

Acts 17

To the Unknown God

Read Acts 17 in the NIV →

Context

Acts 17 traces three stops on Paul's second missionary journey. In Thessalonica he reasons in the synagogue and some believe, but jealous opponents stir up a mob and drive him out. In Berea he finds listeners of nobler character who examine the Scriptures daily to test whether his message is true, until trouble follows him from Thessalonica and he moves on again.

The chapter's centerpiece is Athens, the intellectual capital of the ancient world. Paul is provoked by a city full of idols and ends up debating Epicurean and Stoic philosophers, who bring him to the Areopagus, the council that met on Mars Hill, to explain his strange new teaching about Jesus and the resurrection.

There Paul delivers his most fully recorded speech to a pagan audience. Rather than quoting Scripture they do not know, he begins with their own altar inscribed to an unknown god and with their own poets, building a bridge from what they grope after to the God who made and now summons them.

Key themes

Testing the word. The Bereans are commended for receiving the message eagerly yet examining the Scriptures daily to verify it. Openness and careful scrutiny are presented not as opposites but as marks of an honest hearer.

God known and unknown. The Athenians hedge their worship with an altar to an unknown god, sensing a reality they cannot name. Paul announces that this God is not unknown at all but the Creator who has made himself known.

The Creator who needs nothing. The God Paul proclaims does not live in temples built by hands or depend on human service. He gives life and breath to all and determines the times and boundaries of every nation.

Repentance and judgment. The times of ignorance are over; God now commands all people everywhere to repent, because he has fixed a day to judge the world through the risen man he appointed. The resurrection is the proof and the turning point.

Takeaways

  • Faithful hearing tests every teaching against Scripture, as the Bereans did, rather than accepting or rejecting it on impulse.
  • Paul meets the Athenians where they are, using their altar and poets as a bridge, without diluting the call to repent and believe in the risen Christ.
  • The resurrection of Jesus is the hinge of Paul's gospel even before philosophers; some mocked, some delayed, and some believed.

Connections

Paul's portrait of God as the Maker of heaven and earth who needs nothing from human hands echoes the opening of Genesis and the prophets' mockery of idols that must be carried because they cannot move themselves. He is preaching the God of Israel's Scriptures to people who have never read them.

The Berean practice of searching the Scriptures recalls how Jesus, in the earlier Gospel readings, opened the law and the prophets to show they spoke of him, and how earlier in Acts the apostles reasoned from Scripture in synagogue after synagogue. Paul's claim that God now commands repentance in light of a coming judgment through the risen Christ gathers up the resurrection proclaimed throughout Acts and presses it on the wider Gentile world.

Reflection

Athens still describes the world many of us live in, full of competing devotions and a vague sense that something greater is out there but unnamed. Paul neither retreats from that culture nor flatters it; he honors the longing and then names its true object. Where do I see people reaching toward an unknown god, and can I, like Paul, start from what they already sense rather than from what they do not yet know? And am I, like the Bereans, willing to test what I am taught against the Scriptures?
