Revelation 19-22

Behold, I Am Making All Things New

Read Revelation 19-22 in the NIV →

Context

The final movement of Revelation, and of the whole Bible, brings the long story to its resolution. Heaven sings hallelujah at Babylon's fall and announces the marriage supper of the Lamb. Then Christ appears as the rider on the white horse, called Faithful and True, who judges in righteousness and conquers, and the beast, the false prophet, and finally the dragon himself are decisively defeated and cast into the lake of fire. The dead are raised and judged before the great white throne, and death and Hades, the last enemies, are themselves destroyed.

Then comes the vision the entire biblical drama has been straining toward. John sees a new heaven and a new earth, and the holy city, the new Jerusalem, coming down from God as a bride adorned for her husband. A loud voice declares that the dwelling of God is now with humanity; he will dwell with them, and they will be his people, and God himself will wipe away every tear, with no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the former things have passed away. The one seated on the throne announces, behold, I am making all things new. The city needs no temple and no sun, for God and the Lamb are its temple and its light, and through it flows the river of the water of life, lined with the tree of life whose leaves are for the healing of the nations. The curse is gone; God's servants see his face. The book, and the Bible, ends with the promise, surely I am coming soon, and the church's reply, come, Lord Jesus.

Key themes

The final defeat of evil. The long conflict ends not in stalemate but in the complete and just defeat of the dragon and every power that opposed God. Death itself, the last enemy, is thrown into the lake of fire, so that what entered the world in Eden is at last undone.

God dwelling with his people. The deepest note of the new creation is not merely a perfected place but restored presence: God comes down to dwell with humanity. The longing that ran through tabernacle, temple, and incarnation is fulfilled as God himself becomes the temple, and his servants see his face.

Eden restored and surpassed. The river and the tree of life return, the curse is removed, and every tear is wiped away. Yet this is not merely a return to the garden but a garden-city, Eden taken up and brought to its intended glory, the home God meant for his people all along.

Takeaways

  • The story of God's people does not end in defeat or mere survival but in the total defeat of evil and death and the certain triumph of the Lamb.
  • The heart of heaven is not a place but a Person: God dwelling with his people, wiping away every tear, with no more death, mourning, or pain.
  • What was lost in Eden is not merely recovered but surpassed; the tree of life and unbroken communion with God return forever in the new Jerusalem, the destination of the whole journey.

Connections

This is the homecoming the entire year has anticipated. The new heaven and new earth fulfill the promise of Isaiah 65, and the new Jerusalem with the river and tree of life completes Ezekiel's vision in chapters 40 to 48 of God's restored dwelling. Most powerfully, the ending answers the beginning: the tree of life and the river that watered the garden in Genesis 1 to 3, the very reading that opened this plan on Day 1, return after the long exile from Eden, and the curse pronounced there is finally lifted. The serpent who deceived in the garden meets his end, and God's purpose to dwell with his people, glimpsed at creation and pursued through every covenant, reaches its everlasting fulfillment.

Reflection

If you have walked the whole year, this is where the road has been leading from the very first day. The Bible opened in a garden with a river and the tree of life, with God walking among his people, and then came the serpent, the curse, and the long exile. Today the journey closes by reopening that garden, now a radiant city, the curse gone, the tree of life restored, and God himself dwelling with his people and wiping away every tear. Behold, I am making all things new, he says, and it is the truest word over your own losses and longings. Whatever grief or weariness you carry to the end of this reading, hold it up to that promise. The story does not end in tears but in the face of God, and the fitting final prayer is the church's own: come, Lord Jesus.
