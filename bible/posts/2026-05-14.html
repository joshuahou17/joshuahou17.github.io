Job 13-14

Though He Slay Me: Job's Defiant Faith in the Darkness

Context: The Turning Point in Job's Defense

Job 13-14 marks a crucial pivot in the dialogue. After enduring three rounds of attacks from his friends—who kept telling him to start praying to God and to stop sinning when the problem is that Job is not suffering because of his sin—Job finally turns away from them to address God directly. Job 12:1-14:22 is one long response of Job that is a literary unit and must be interpreted as a whole. In Job 13:1-19 Job addresses the three comforters but in Job 13:20-28 he addresses God. This shift from horizontal to vertical dialogue represents Job's recognition that his friends are "physicians of no value" and that only God himself can provide answers.

Key Themes

The Famous Declaration of Faith: The heart of these chapters lies in Job's stunning declaration in 13:15. "This is the quintessence of Biblical faith, 'Though he slay me, yet will I trust him.'" While some manuscripts create translation disputes, "The KJV is by far the preferable translation of this verse; and it is backed up by the Douay Version and the new Easy-to-Read Version of the Bible by the World Bible Translation Center. This is the quintessence of Biblical faith, 'Though he slay me, yet will I trust him.' We reject as totally unjustified the critical presumption that they may emend any passage they please to make it conform to their theory, but refuse to allow such an emendation as the one here that gives us one of the great passages in the whole Bible." Although Job was unaware of the challenge that Satan made against him, he proved that Satan was wrong to allege to God that his faith was based solely upon the many blessings of wealth, family, and health that God had given him.

The Mystery of Mortality and Resurrection: Chapter 14 grapples with humanity's universal condition. "We can explain Job's lack of knowledge of the afterlife by understanding the principle of 2 Timothy 2:10: that Jesus Christ brought life and immortality to light through the gospel. The understanding of immortality was at best cloudy in the Old Testament, but is much clearer in the New Testament." Yet remarkably, "Job here gives a very clear expression to his belief that, even after he lies down in Sheol, God will call him out to life again (Job 14:15). There is only one reason for the blindness of many scholars on this point; and, as cited by Andersen, it is solely due to, 'Their a priori belief that the idea of a resurrection arose quite late in Israel's thought.'"

Direct Appeal to Divine Justice: "Job wanted restored communication with God (Then call, and I will answer). The fear Job was concerned about was not the good and proper fear of God; instead, this was prompted by dread." Job's request for God to withdraw His terror and speak with him reveals his deep longing for relationship even in judgment.

Cross-References

Job's declaration of faith despite potential death echoes themes we've seen throughout our reading plan. Like Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac (Genesis 22, Day 37), Job demonstrates that true faith persists even when God's actions seem to contradict His promises. This connects to David's trust in Psalms 3-5 (Day 11) where we saw "faith transforms anxiety into assurance." The wrestling with mortality here also anticipates the resurrection hope we encountered in Isaiah's messianic prophecies (Day 13) and finds its ultimate fulfillment in Romans 5-6's treatment of death's defeat through Christ (Day 15).

Historical and Cultural Context

Job's understanding of the afterlife reflects the pre-revelation knowledge of his era. "Essentially, 'Sheol' in the Old Testament is the place where the dead go. There was common belief in the continuing personal existence of one's spirit after death. When the place where unrighteous people go is in view, the reference is to hell. When the righteous are in view, Sheol refers to either death or the grave." His famous question "If a man dies, shall he live again?" (14:14) wasn't philosophical speculation but when "Job poses the question, 'If a man dies, shall he live again?', he immediately answers it: 'I will wait till my change comes' (Job 14:14). Job saw his life as days of hard service to God, and of patience in suffering. At the end of his life on earth, he did not expect oblivion, but a 'change' for the better."

Reflection

These chapters present us with the paradox of faith under fire. Job's "Though He slay me, yet will I trust Him" wasn't spoken in a comfortable sanctuary but from an ash heap of inexplicable suffering. This challenges our own trust—do we serve God for His benefits, or do we serve Him simply because He is God? Job's raw honesty with God also models authentic relationship. He doesn't offer pious platitudes but brings his genuine questions and complaints directly to the throne. Finally, his wrestling with mortality reminds us that faith doesn't exempt us from life's deepest questions, but it does give us Someone to ask them to. As we face our own "ash heap" moments, may we find courage in Job's defiant faith and comfort in knowing that the "change" he hoped for has been secured through Christ's resurrection.
