Day 286 of 365

Luke 11

Teach Us to Pray, and Beware the Hidden Heart

Read Luke 11 in the NIV →

Context

Luke gathers in this chapter teaching on prayer and a sharpening conflict over Jesus' authority. It opens with a disciple's request, prompted by watching Jesus pray, to be taught as John taught his followers. Jesus gives a shorter form of the prayer known from Matthew's sermon, then drives home a single point with the picture of a friend roused at midnight: keep asking, keep seeking, keep knocking.

The mood then darkens. An exorcism provokes the accusation that Jesus works by the prince of demons, and a demand for a heavenly sign. Jesus answers both, then accepts a Pharisee's dinner invitation only to confront, at the table, a religion polished on the outside and rotten within. The woes that follow are among the most pointed words he speaks to the religious establishment.

Key themes

Pray boldly to a giving Father. The model prayer puts God's name and kingdom first, then daily bread, forgiveness, and protection. The persistence Jesus commends does not wear down a reluctant God; it forms in us a deeper desire for what he wants to give, supremely the Holy Spirit, the best gift a Father grants to his children.

The kingdom has arrived in power. A house divided cannot stand, so Jesus cannot be casting out demons by Satan. If he does it by the finger of God, then the kingdom of God has come upon them. The strong man is bound and his goods plundered; neutrality toward Jesus is impossible.

Beware the polished exterior. The woes condemn tithing herbs while neglecting justice and the love of God, loving the best seats, and, worst of all, taking away the key of knowledge so that others cannot enter. Their legalism did not merely fail them; it actively barred the way for others.

Takeaways

  • Prayer rests on the Father's character; we ask boldly and persistently not to overcome his reluctance but because he delights to give good gifts, above all his Spirit.
  • Jesus' power over demons is proof that God's kingdom has broken in; one cannot stay neutral before such a claim.
  • God weighs the heart over the performance; meticulous religion that neglects justice, love, and honesty is a danger to the one practicing it and to those it misleads.

Connections

The Lord's Prayer here parallels the fuller version in the Sermon on the Mount read on day 283. The sign of Jonah points back to the prophet swallowed and restored in Jonah 1-2, and forward to the resurrection. The image of the strong man bound recalls Isaiah 49:24-25, and the woes against the Pharisees anticipate the fuller indictment of Matthew 23. The Beelzebul charge connects to the same accusation answered on day 282, showing how persistent the opposition had become.

Jonah 1:17 → Isaiah 49:24-25 → Hosea 6:6 → Micah 6:8 → Matthew 6:9-13 →

Reflection

It is unsettling that the people most certain of their righteousness drew Jesus' sharpest words, while a request as simple as teach us to pray opened the chapter. The Pharisees had polished the cup's outside; the disciples admitted they did not even know how to ask. Where are you tempted to perfect the appearance of faith rather than tend its inside? And when did you last simply ask, with the honesty of a child, for the one gift the Father most wants to give?
