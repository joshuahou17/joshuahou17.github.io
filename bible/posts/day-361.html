2 John; 3 John

Truth and Love at the Door

Read 2 John in the NIV → Read 3 John in the NIV →

Context

Second and Third John are the shortest books in the New Testament, each about the length of a single sheet of ancient papyrus, written by the one who calls himself simply the elder. They are personal in tone yet apply the great themes of First John to concrete situations involving hospitality, the lifeline of early Christian mission, since traveling teachers depended on the welcome of local households.

Second John is addressed to the elect lady and her children, almost certainly a church and its members, rejoicing that some walk in the truth and urging them to love one another, which means walking according to God's commands. But the elder issues a sharp warning: many deceivers have gone out who deny that Jesus has come in the flesh, and the church must not receive such teachers into their homes or give them any greeting, lest they share in their evil work. Third John, written to a beloved man named Gaius, takes the opposite angle: it commends Gaius for faithfully supporting genuine traveling missionaries, rebukes Diotrephes who loves to be first and refuses to welcome them while expelling those who would, and praises Demetrius as one who is well attested by all.

Key themes

Truth and love belong together. The elder will not separate them. Love means walking in obedience to the truth, and truth must be held in love. Welcoming those who deny Christ is not broad-minded kindness but complicity, while love for the brothers shows itself in generous hospitality to faithful workers.

Hospitality as gospel partnership. In a world without hotels or institutional missions, opening one's home to traveling teachers was how the gospel spread. To support faithful workers was to become a fellow worker for the truth, and to refuse them, as Diotrephes did, was to oppose the mission itself.

The peril of self-importance. Diotrephes, who loves to put himself first, stands as a warning. His refusal of hospitality and his slander of others spring from pride, in contrast to Gaius and Demetrius whose service flows from love and is attested by all.

Takeaways

  • Love and truth are not in tension; real love walks in obedience to the truth, and real truth is always exercised in love.
  • Supporting faithful gospel workers, through hospitality and material help, makes you a partner in their mission and a fellow worker for the truth.
  • Pride and the desire to be first can quietly poison a community; service that seeks the good of others, not its own prominence, is what God commends.

Connections

These letters extend the concerns of First John from the previous day, applying its test, that genuine teachers confess Jesus come in the flesh, to the practical decision of whom to welcome. The deep link between truth and love reaches back to Leviticus 19's command to love the neighbor and to Jesus' new commandment in John 13. The high value placed on hospitality echoes the welcome Abraham showed his visitors in Genesis 18 and the consistent biblical call, as in Deuteronomy 10, to love the stranger, here refined by the discernment the gospel requires.

Reflection

These tiny letters wrestle with a question we still face: how do we hold truth and love together without sacrificing either? The elder refuses both options that tempt us, a love so undiscerning it welcomes anything, and a zeal for truth so harsh it loves no one. He commends Gaius for open-handed support of faithful workers and warns against Diotrephes, whose root sin was simply loving to be first. Ask yourself which figure you more resemble. Whom could you welcome and support today as a partner in the truth, and where might a quiet pride be keeping you from the humble service that God actually commends?
