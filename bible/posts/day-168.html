Proverbs 25-26

Apples of Gold, Folly of Fools

Read Proverbs 25-26 in the NIV →

Context

These chapters open a distinct section. A heading notes that these are proverbs of Solomon which the men of Hezekiah, king of Judah, copied, evidence that Israel's wisdom was lovingly preserved and edited across generations. The collection spanning chapters 25 to 27 leans heavily on similes and earthy images.

Chapter 26 in particular becomes a sustained meditation on the fool and the sluggard, sketching them with almost comic precision: the lazy man who claims a lion is in the street, the fool who repeats his folly like a dog returning to its vomit.

Key themes

The right word at the right time. A word fitly spoken is likened to apples of gold in settings of silver. Wisdom is not only knowing the truth but knowing the timing, restraint, and tact that let truth land well.

Answering a fool, both ways. The proverbs say in one breath do not answer a fool according to his folly, and in the next, answer a fool according to his folly. The apparent contradiction is the point: discernment, not a rule, decides whether silence or rebuttal serves best.

The portrait of the fool. Folly here is not low intelligence but a settled refusal to learn. The fool is dangerous because he is confident, repetitive, and immune to correction, like a thornbush in a drunkard's hand.

Honesty over flattery. Better is open rebuke than hidden love; faithful are the wounds of a friend. The collection prizes the bracing honesty of true relationship over the cheap comfort of flattery.

Takeaways

  • Wisdom includes timing and tact; the same true word can be either a gift or a weapon depending on the moment.
  • The two sayings about answering a fool teach discernment over formula, since real situations require judgment, not autopilot.
  • Folly is mostly a refusal to be taught, which is why honest friends and faithful wounds are worth more than flattery.

Connections

The note about Hezekiah's scribes links these proverbs to the later reforming king whose reign appears further along in the reading plan, showing how wisdom was treasured across the monarchy. The praise of a word fitly spoken resonates with James's later teaching on the power of the tongue. The contrast between the fool and the wise extends the great theme set out in the prologue of Proverbs, where the fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge.

Reflection

The two-sided counsel on answering a fool is oddly freeing. There is no script that guarantees a wise response; there is only the patient work of reading the moment. That means wisdom is less a set of rules to memorize than a sensitivity to cultivate. Where in your conversations this week might silence be the wise answer, and where might honest words be the gift of a faithful friend?
