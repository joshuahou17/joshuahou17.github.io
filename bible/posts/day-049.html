Leviticus 16-18

The Day of Atonement

Read Leviticus 16-18 in the NIV →

Context

This is the theological heart of the book, framed by the deaths of Nadab and Abihu, which showed how dangerous careless approach to God can be. The answer is a single, carefully prescribed day each year when the high priest may enter the Most Holy Place behind the veil that even Moses could not pass.

The ritual centers on two goats. One is slaughtered and its blood used to purify the sanctuary; the other, the so-called scapegoat, receives the confessed sins of the people by the laying on of hands and is driven into the wilderness, carrying the sins away. The destination is rendered "for Azazel," a term scholars debate, most plausibly meaning total removal or a desolate place.

Chapter 17 then insists that the life of a creature is in the blood, which is why blood makes atonement and must not be eaten. Chapter 18 turns to laws of sexual integrity that set Israel apart from surrounding nations.

Key themes

Atonement covers and removes. The two goats picture two facets of one rescue: one absorbs the penalty through its blood, the other carries sin entirely away. Together they show sin both paid for and removed from sight.

Life is in the blood. The prohibition on eating blood rests on a profound claim, that blood represents life, and life belongs to God. Atonement is costly because it is the giving of life for life.

Holiness extends to how we live and love. The sexual laws of chapter 18 ground morality in God's character and in being distinct from the surrounding cultures. Holiness is not only ritual but ethical, touching the most intimate areas of life.

Takeaways

  • Atonement both pays for sin and carries it away, so forgiveness means our wrongdoing is not merely covered but removed.
  • Because life is in the blood, atonement is never cheap; it costs a life, which gives weight to every later mention of sacrifice.
  • God's holiness reaches into ethics and intimacy, calling his people to a distinct way of living rather than mirroring the surrounding culture.

Connections

This day resolves the tension Exodus left when Moses could not enter the glory-filled tent. The torn veil at Jesus' death and the argument of Hebrews 9 through 10, that Christ entered the true Most Holy Place once for all with his own blood, both look directly back here. The scapegoat carrying sin away anticipates Isaiah's suffering servant who bears iniquity, and the two-bird rite of the previous reading rehearsed this same pattern of one slain and one sent away.

Reflection

Two goats: one whose blood covers, one who carries your sin into a place from which it never returns. Which do you most need to hear today, that your guilt is paid or that it is gone? Let the image of sin driven away into the wilderness reshape how you carry what you have confessed.
