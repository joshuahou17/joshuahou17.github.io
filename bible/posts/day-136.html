Psalms 3-4, 12-13, 28, 55

I Lie Down and Sleep: Prayers from the Flight from Absalom

Read Psalms 3-4, 12-13, 28, 55 in the NIV →

Context

This cluster of short, intense psalms reads naturally against the backdrop of David's flight from Absalom. Psalm 3 carries the explicit superscription that David composed it when he fled from his son, and it begins by counting the many who rise against him before turning to confident sleep under God's protection. Psalm 4 is an evening companion, a prayer for relief and for the troubled to be still and trust. Psalms 12 and 13 give voice to a world where faithful people seem to have vanished and where the sufferer asks how long God will hide His face.

Psalm 28 pleads with God not to be silent, and Psalm 55 describes the particular agony of betrayal by a close companion, words many commentators connect to Ahithophel, David's trusted counselor who defected to Absalom. Together these psalms map the inner landscape of a man whose own household has turned against him.

Key themes

Sleep as an act of trust. Psalm 3 makes the astonishing claim that, surrounded by enemies, David lay down and slept because the Lord sustained him. Rest becomes a form of faith, the body's confession that God, not vigilance, is the true defense.

The wound of betrayal. Psalm 55 distinguishes the pain of an enemy's attack from the deeper anguish of a friend's treachery: it was a companion with whom he once took sweet counsel and walked to the house of God. Some betrayals come from inside the circle of trust.

How long? Psalm 13 voices the believer's hardest question, repeating how long until it ends, then pivots within a few verses to trust in God's steadfast love. Lament and confidence are not enemies but neighbors.

God's pure words in a lying world. Psalm 12 contrasts the flattering, double-hearted speech of the wicked with the words of the Lord, refined like silver. When human promises rot, God's word remains trustworthy.

Takeaways

  • Faith can express itself in something as ordinary as sleep; Psalm 3 treats rest amid danger as quiet defiance born of trust.
  • The Psalms give language for betrayal by those closest to us, refusing to pretend that the deepest wounds come only from obvious enemies.
  • Honest questioning, even the raw how long of Psalm 13, lives comfortably alongside renewed trust within the same prayer.

Connections

Psalm 3's superscription anchors it directly to the flight from Absalom narrated in the previous reading, and Psalm 55's lament over a treacherous companion is widely read as David's response to Ahithophel's defection in those same chapters. The cry of Psalm 13, how long will you hide your face, is taken up across the Psalter and into the book of Revelation, where the martyrs ask the same question. The trustworthy, refined words of Psalm 12 stand against the manipulative speech Absalom used at the gate to steal the people's hearts.

Reflection

Psalm 3 asks whether we can sleep when the count of our troubles is rising. What would it mean to lie down tonight and entrust the unresolved to God rather than rehearsing it until dawn? And if you carry the particular ache of betrayal that Psalm 55 names, notice that the psalm does not end in bitterness but casts the burden onto the One who can carry it.
