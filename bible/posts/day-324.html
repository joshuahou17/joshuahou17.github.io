Day 324 of 365

Acts 11-12

A New Name and an Open Door

Read Acts 11-12 in the NIV →

Context

Acts 11 traces two threads. First, Peter returns to Jerusalem and defends his visit to Cornelius, retelling the vision and the Spirit's outpouring until the critics fall silent and conclude that God has granted even the Gentiles repentance to life. The principle of Acts 10 is now formally accepted by the church.

Second, the scattered believers reach Antioch, a great Greco-Roman city, and begin preaching to Gentiles in large numbers. Barnabas is sent to investigate, fetches Saul from Tarsus, and the two teach there for a year. It is in Antioch that the disciples are first called Christians, and from Antioch that famine relief flows back to Judea.

Acts 12 returns to Jerusalem and to danger. Herod Agrippa kills James the brother of John and arrests Peter, intending the same. The church prays, an angel walks Peter out past the guards, and the prayer meeting can scarcely believe he is at the door. Herod, soon after, accepts divine honors at Caesarea and is struck down. The word of God, Luke notes, continued to increase.

Key themes

A new identity. The name Christian appears first at Antioch, given to a community defined not by ethnicity or temple but by belonging to Christ. The mixed Jewish-Gentile church needed a new word because it was a genuinely new thing in the world.

The sending church. Antioch models maturity: it receives teaching, raises up leaders, and immediately looks outward, sending relief to Judea and later sending Paul on mission. Grace received becomes grace extended.

Prayer and the limits of power. Herod's iron chains and four squads of soldiers cannot hold a man the church is praying for. Luke sets human authority against divine sovereignty and shows which one prevails.

Mercy and judgment together. The same God who sends an angel to deliver Peter sends an angel to strike Herod for grasping at glory that belongs to God alone. The chapter holds rescue and reckoning side by side, and ends not with Herod but with the word of God growing.

Takeaways

  • The church's identity is rooted in Christ, not in nationality or place, and a healthy church turns outward to serve and send.
  • Prayer is the church's real power; against it, even the chains and soldiers of a king cannot finally prevail.
  • God both delivers the humble and humbles the proud; Herod's death warns that glory grasped from God does not go unanswered.

Connections

Herod's gruesome death for accepting worship that belongs to God echoes the warnings of the Old Testament against human pride exalting itself, and it stands in deliberate contrast to the apostles in earlier Acts who refused worship and pointed people to God instead. Peter's deliverance recalls the apostles' earlier jailbreak in Acts 5, where an angel likewise opened the prison doors, showing a consistent pattern of God protecting His witnesses. And Antioch's embrace of Gentiles is the direct fruit of the Cornelius episode just read, carrying forward the scattering of Acts 8 and the no-partiality lesson of Acts 10 into a full-fledged multiethnic mission base.

Reflection

While Peter slept chained between soldiers, the church was awake and praying, and yet they could hardly believe their prayer had been answered when he knocked at the gate. There is honesty in that detail; faith and astonishment often sit together. What would it look like for you to pray with real expectation for something that feels as locked as a prison door? And in a world that still grasps for glory, how does it steady you to know that the last word in this chapter belongs not to Herod but to the growing word of God?
