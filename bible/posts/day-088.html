Joshua 22-24

As For Me and My House

Read Joshua 22-24 in the NIV →

Context

These three chapters bring the book of Joshua to its close. The land has been taken and divided, and now the work shifts from conquest to consequence: how will a people keep faith once the dramatic battles are over? Chapter 22 sends the eastern tribes home. Reuben, Gad, and half of Manasseh had received land east of the Jordan but had crossed over to fight alongside their brothers. Now released, they build a great altar by the river, and the western tribes nearly march to war, fearing rebellion against the LORD.

The crisis is defused when the eastern tribes explain that the altar is not for sacrifice but a witness, a copy meant to testify to future generations that they too belong to Israel and worship at the one sanctuary. The priest Phinehas declares them faithful, and war is averted.

Chapters 23 and 24 form Joshua's farewell. First he gathers the leaders and warns them not to mix with the surrounding nations or their gods. Then, at Shechem, he assembles all the tribes and leads a formal covenant renewal, retelling Israel's whole story from Abraham to the present and pressing them to decide.

Key themes

One altar, one people. The near-war of chapter 22 turns on the conviction that Israel worships at a single sanctuary. The eastern tribes' replica altar is dangerous precisely because unity in worship is the thread holding a scattered nation together, and a rival altar would tear it.

Memory as faithfulness. Both the witness altar and the great stone at Shechem exist to make a future generation remember. Joshua knows that forgetting is how a people drifts, so he builds physical reminders that have heard the words spoken and will testify against any who break faith.

Grace before demand. Before Joshua asks anything, he recites what God has done: called Abraham, brought Israel out of Egypt, given a land they did not labor for. The covenant is a response to a Giver, not a bargain struck with a stranger.

Decision and warning. Choose this day whom you will serve. Joshua refuses easy assurances; when the people promise loyalty he answers that they cannot serve the LORD lightly, exposing how hard fidelity will prove.

Takeaways

  • Suspicion almost destroyed Israel in Joshua 22; the eastern tribes were saved by explaining themselves and the western tribes by asking before attacking. Conflict is often a misread motive, and inquiry can avert war.
  • Faith is not inherited automatically. Joshua piles up reminders, stones, and altars because he knows the next generation will forget what it did not witness, and forgetting is the first step toward unfaithfulness.
  • A real choice for God is sober, not sentimental. Joshua warns the people that serving the LORD is costly before they vow, refusing to let commitment rest on a casual promise.

Connections

The covenant renewal at Shechem deliberately echoes Sinai. Just as Moses set up stones and read the law in Exodus and Deuteronomy, Joshua erects a stone witness and recounts the covenant, closing the loop that began with God's call of Abraham in Genesis 12. Shechem itself was where Abram first built an altar when he entered the land, so Israel renews its vow on the very ground where the promise was first given.

Joshua's recital of God's saving acts mirrors the historical prologues familiar from Deuteronomy, grounding obligation in gratitude. His insistence that the people cannot serve the LORD without wholehearted devotion looks ahead to the very failure Judges will record.

The phrase as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD has become a household creed, but in context it is a leader staking his family's loyalty as a model and a challenge to a wavering nation.

Reflection

Joshua dies with the land won but the human heart unresolved. The book ends not in triumph but in a question put to every generation: whom will you serve? The answer cannot be borrowed from parents or leaders; each person and household must choose again.

The witness altar and the standing stone press a quieter point. We are forgetful creatures, and faith left to drift erodes. Joshua's response is to build memory into the landscape, to make remembering deliberate. What reminders do we set up so that we, and those after us, do not forget what we have been given?

And there is the sobering honesty of Joshua's warning. He will not let the people promise loyalty cheaply. Real devotion counts the cost first. To say we will serve the LORD is to accept that it will ask everything, and to mean it anyway.
