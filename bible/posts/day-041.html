Day 41 of 365

Exodus 33-35

Show Me Your Glory

Read Exodus 33-35 in the NIV →

Context

Exodus 33-35 turns the story from sin back toward restoration. The central crisis is no longer survival but presence: God offers to send an angel ahead but warns he will not go up in their midst, and Moses refuses to move without God himself. From this bargaining comes Moses' boldest request, to see God's glory.

God's answer redefines glory. He will not show his face, which no one can see and live, but he will make his goodness pass by and proclaim his name. That proclamation, merciful and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness, yet not clearing the guilty, becomes one of the most quoted self-descriptions of God in all Scripture. The covenant is renewed on new tablets, Moses comes down with a shining face, and chapter 35 begins the people's generous, willing-hearted offerings for the sanctuary.

Key themes

Glory is character. God locates his glory not in raw power but in goodness and mercy. When Moses asks to see glory, God proclaims his name, revealing that his deepest splendor is his steadfast love.

Presence over blessing. Moses will not settle for the land without the Giver. His insistence that God himself go with them names the heart of true faith: wanting God more than his gifts.

Transformation by nearness. Moses' shining face shows that time in God's presence leaves a visible mark. Communion with God changes those who draw near.

Takeaways

  • God defines his own glory as mercy and steadfast love, not merely power, in a self-revelation Scripture returns to again and again.
  • Moses models faith that wants the Giver above the gift, refusing to go forward without God's own presence.
  • Drawing near to God transforms; Moses' shining face is a sign that communion with God reshapes the one who seeks it.

Connections

God's self-proclamation as merciful and gracious is quoted across the Psalms, the prophets, and beyond, becoming Israel's anchor for hope after failure. Paul takes up Moses' veiled, fading radiance to contrast it with the greater, unfading glory believers behold in Christ, who shows God's glory in his face. The renewed covenant after the calf demonstrates the very steadfast love God has just proclaimed, mercy following hard on the heels of sin.

Reflection

Moses asked to see God's glory and was shown his goodness and mercy. If God's deepest glory is his steadfast love rather than his power, how does that reshape what you most long to see and know of him?
