Day 84 of 365

Joshua 9-11

A Treaty, a Long Day, and a Conquered Land

Read Joshua 9-11 in the NIV →

Context

After the high drama of Jericho and Achan, Joshua 9 to 11 narrates the heart of the conquest. The Canaanite kings have heard what Israel did and now band together to resist. Against this backdrop the Gibeonites take a different path. They disguise themselves with worn clothes and moldy bread and pretend to come from a distant country, hoping to secure a treaty Israel would never grant a local people.

The key failure is named plainly. The Israelite leaders sampled the provisions but did not inquire of the Lord. Bound by an oath sworn in his name, they keep the treaty even after the ruse is exposed, making the Gibeonites woodcutters and water carriers for the altar.

Chapter 10 turns to war. A five-king southern coalition attacks Gibeon for defecting, and Joshua marches all night to rescue his new allies. The Lord throws the enemy into panic, kills more with hailstones than Israel kills with the sword, and answers Joshua's bold prayer that the sun stand still. Chapter 11 records the northern campaign, where Joshua routs a vast coalition at the waters of Merom and takes the whole land.

Key themes

The peril of acting without inquiring of God. Israel's blunder with Gibeon traces directly to a single omission. They did not ask the Lord. The narrator wants readers to feel how easily appearances deceive when leaders lean on their own judgment, a lesson that echoes the wilderness failures recorded in Numbers and the warnings of Deuteronomy.

The binding weight of a sworn oath. Even a treaty obtained by fraud holds, because it was sworn in the name of the Lord. Israel honors its word at cost to itself, modeling a covenant fidelity that reflects God's own. Centuries later, in 2 Samuel 21, breaking this very oath brings famine on the land.

The Lord fights for Israel. The victories belong to God, not to military genius. Hailstones, panic, and a lengthened day all underscore that Yahweh wages the war. The refrain there has never been a day like it points away from Joshua to the God who commands even the heavens.

Judgment on Canaan and the mystery of mercy. The herem, the devotion of the cities to destruction, executes long-deferred judgment on entrenched idolatry. Yet Gibeon, like Rahab before it, is spared and grafted in. Judgment and grace stand side by side.

Takeaways

  • Big decisions made on appearances and convenience, without seeking God, can bind us for years; the Gibeonite treaty warns against trusting our own assessment over prayerful discernment.
  • Promises sworn before God carry real weight; Israel kept faith with the Gibeonites even when it was costly and even when the agreement was secured by deceit.
  • The conquest victories are credited to the Lord who fights for his people, reframing courage as trust in God rather than confidence in our own strength.

Connections

The command not to make treaties with Canaan's peoples comes from Exodus 23 and Deuteronomy 7, read earlier in this plan, which is precisely why the Gibeonite deception is so serious. Israel's earlier failure to inquire of God recalls the spies' faithless report in Numbers 13, where only Caleb and Joshua trusted the Lord.

The Gibeonites' fearful faith mirrors Rahab in Joshua 2. Both are outsiders who hear what God has done, throw themselves on Israel's mercy, and are spared, foreshadowing the nations being gathered into the people of God. The lengthened day, drawn from the Book of Jashar, sits alongside other moments when creation itself serves God's purposes, as in the plagues of Exodus and later in Habakkuk 3.

Reflection

It is striking that the one fatal mistake in these chapters is not a battle lost but a prayer skipped. The leaders examined the evidence and reached a reasonable conclusion, and they were still deceived. Where in your own life are you trusting your read of the situation more than you are asking God for wisdom?

Notice too that God does not undo Israel's error. He lets the treaty stand and then weaves even the Gibeonites into the worship of his house. Our mistakes are real, yet they are not beyond his redemption.

Finally, the long day at Gibeon invites a quiet confidence. The God who can hold back the sun to finish a rescue is the same God who fights for his people still. The call is not to summon our own strength but to trust the One who has never lost a battle he chose to fight.
