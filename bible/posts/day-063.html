Numbers 21-22

Looking Up, and a Seer Gone Blind

Read Numbers 21-22 in the NIV →

Context

Chapter 21 marks the turn from wandering to advance. Israel begins to take territory east of the Jordan, defeating Sihon and Og, the first fruits of conquest. The chapter preserves fragments of ancient poetry quoting older sources, including the Book of the Wars of the LORD and the Song of the Well, embedding Israel's story in songs older than the prose around them.

With chapter 22 the camera pulls back to the Moabite king Balak, terrified of Israel, who hires the seer Balaam to curse them. Balaam is no mere invention: an inscription discovered at Deir Alla in Jordan names a seer called Balaam son of Beor who receives night visions, much as Numbers describes. The biblical writer adapts a figure his audience would have recognized.

Key themes

Life by looking. The bronze serpent heals by a paradox. The very image of what is killing Israel becomes, when lifted up, the means of life for all who look. The cure lies not in the object but in the obedient, trusting gaze God commands.

Momentum toward the promise. Sihon and Og fall as the first installment of the land. The wandering generation's death is not the end of the story; God's purpose presses forward, and the embedded victory songs frame these conquests as the LORD's own work.

Sight and blindness. Balaam boasts of open eyes yet cannot see the angel blocking his road until God opens them, after his donkey has seen it three times. The episode reads as pointed satire: the celebrated visionary is less perceptive than his beast. Spiritual sight, the passage insists, is God's gift, not a professional skill.

Takeaways

  • Salvation comes by looking away from ourselves to what God provides, not by managing the danger on our own.
  • God's redemptive momentum continues even after a generation has failed; setbacks do not cancel His promises.
  • Gifting and reputation are no guarantee of true perception; only God opens eyes to see what is really there.

Connections

Jesus makes the bronze serpent the key to His own crucifixion, telling Nicodemus that the Son of Man must be lifted up so that those who look in faith may live. Centuries later the same serpent had to be destroyed as an idol under Hezekiah, a warning that even God-given symbols can be misused. The defeats of Sihon and Og are retold in tomorrow's region of Deuteronomy and celebrated in the Psalms, and the New Testament repeatedly invokes the way of Balaam as a caution.

Reflection

Is there a wound or failure you keep trying to manage on your own rather than lifting your eyes to God's provision? And where might confidence in your own insight be leaving you, like Balaam, blind to what God is plainly placing in your path?
