2 Kings 12-13; 2 Chronicles 24

Joash Repairs the Temple, Then Loses His Way

Read 2 Kings 12-13 in the NIV → Read 2 Chronicles 24 in the NIV →

Context

This reading braids the parallel accounts of 2 Kings and 2 Chronicles around King Joash of Judah, the boy rescued from Athaliah in the previous chapter. Now grown, Joash devotes himself to repairing the temple, devising a collection chest so the people's offerings can fund the long-delayed restoration. Chronicles adds the crucial detail that Joash did right as long as the priest Jehoiada lived.

After Jehoiada dies, Joash listens to the officials of Judah, abandons the temple, and turns to idols. When Jehoiada's son Zechariah confronts the nation as a prophet, Joash has him stoned to death in the temple court, a betrayal of the very family that saved his life. Joash dies by assassination. Interwoven in 2 Kings is the death of the prophet Elisha and the strange epilogue in which a dead man, thrown into Elisha's grave, revives at the touch of the prophet's bones.

Key themes

Borrowed faith fades. Joash's devotion lasts exactly as long as his mentor lives. Faith propped up by another person's conviction, never made one's own, collapses when the support is removed.

Ingratitude and betrayal. The king repays the house that hid and crowned him by murdering its son. The narrative exposes how power and flattering counsel can erase memory and gratitude.

God's power outlasts the prophet. Even Elisha's bones bring life, a final sign that the source of his miracles was never the man himself but the God who worked through him.

Takeaways

  • A faith that depends entirely on a mentor or community, never internalized, rarely survives their absence.
  • Whose counsel we listen to shapes our course; Joash flourished under Jehoiada and fell under the flattering officials.
  • God's life-giving power is not bound to a living prophet; it persists beyond him, pointing past the servant to the Lord.

Connections

Joash is the hidden heir of the previous reading, so the two days form a single arc of rescue and tragic decline. Jesus refers to the murder of Zechariah in the temple as a marker of Israel's long pattern of killing the prophets. Joash's temple-repair collection foreshadows later reforms under Hezekiah and Josiah. Elisha's reviving bones round out the Elijah-Elisha cycle that opened this stretch of readings, where God's power persistently exceeds his human agents.

Reflection

Joash is a sobering portrait of a life that started in rescue and ended in betrayal. He shows how easily we can outsource our convictions to a respected guide and never own them, so that when the guide is gone, so is the faith. The question lingers: if the voices currently steadying me fell silent, would my devotion stand on its own, or would I drift toward whatever flattering counsel came next?
