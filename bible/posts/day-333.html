1 Corinthians 1-4

The Foolishness of God: Wisdom, the Cross, and a Divided Church

Read 1 Corinthians 1-4 in the NIV →

Context

First Corinthians is a real letter to a real congregation Paul founded, written from Ephesus around AD 54-55 in response to reports and questions reaching him. Corinth prized rhetoric, status, and showy wisdom, and the church had absorbed those values, splintering into factions that rallied behind favorite teachers: I follow Paul, I follow Apollos, I follow Cephas.

In chapters 1-4 Paul refuses to take a side in the rivalry and instead diagnoses its root. The problem is not which leader is best but a whole way of measuring worth borrowed from the surrounding culture. Against it he sets the message of the cross, which by every Greek and Roman standard looks like weakness and defeat, yet is the very power and wisdom of God. A chain of Old Testament citations underwrites the argument that God consistently overturns human expectation.

Key themes

The word of the cross. What the world calls foolish, a crucified Messiah, God calls wisdom and power. Paul deliberately came in weakness and trembling, refusing polished eloquence, so that faith would rest on God's power and not on human persuasion.

God chooses the lowly. God selects the weak, the despised, and the nobodies to shame the strong, ensuring that no one can boast before Him. The pattern of the cross becomes the pattern of the whole community's calling.

Leaders as servants, not idols. Paul and Apollos are merely field hands; God gives the growth. To attach one's identity to a human teacher is to shrink the gospel back into the very status game it dismantles.

Takeaways

  • Division in the church often masks a deeper problem: importing the world's scale of value into a community meant to be shaped by the cross.
  • The cross redefines wisdom and power, so that boasting in human teachers, eloquence, or status is exposed as missing the point entirely.
  • Spiritual leaders are servants who plant and water; the growth belongs to God alone, which frees congregations from rivalry and leaders from pride.

Connections

Paul's six Old Testament quotations draw on Isaiah and Jeremiah, where God destroys the wisdom of the wise and invites people to boast only in knowing the Lord. The cross as God's surprising reversal extends the prophetic pattern of the suffering servant in Isaiah, and the warning against trusting human wisdom recalls Proverbs and the contrast between two kinds of wisdom. Paul's role as planter while God gives growth anticipates the agricultural imagery Jesus used for the kingdom.

Isaiah 29:14 → Jeremiah 9:23-24 → Isaiah 52:13-53:12 → Proverbs 3:5-7 → Matthew 13:31-32 →

Reflection

What quietly shapes your sense of who matters in the church, and would it survive contact with a crucified Lord? Consider where you have pinned your identity to a teacher, a tribe, or your own competence, and what it would mean to boast in nothing but the cross that looks like weakness yet carries the power of God.
