1 Corinthians 15-16

If Christ Has Not Been Raised: The Resurrection at the Center

Read 1 Corinthians 15-16 in the NIV →

Context

Some in Corinth were denying a future bodily resurrection, perhaps under the influence of Greek ideas that prized the soul and despised the body. Chapter 15 is Paul's extended answer, the longest sustained treatment of resurrection in the New Testament. He opens by reciting an early creedal formula that Christ died for our sins, was buried, and was raised on the third day, appearing to many witnesses, language widely regarded as predating the letter and reaching back near the events themselves.

Paul then argues by consequence: if there is no resurrection, then Christ is not raised, preaching is empty, faith is futile, and the dead are lost. But Christ has been raised as the firstfruits, guaranteeing the harvest to come. He addresses how the dead are raised, contrasting the perishable body sown with the imperishable, spiritual body raised, and ends in a triumphant taunt at death itself. Chapter 16 closes the letter with instructions for the collection, travel plans, and final greetings.

Key themes

Resurrection as first importance. Paul delivers as of first importance the death, burial, and resurrection of Christ. The bodily resurrection is not an optional add-on but the load-bearing center of the gospel he received and passed on.

Christ the firstfruits. As the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep, the risen Christ guarantees and represents the believer's own future resurrection, just as the first sheaf pledged the whole harvest.

Death swallowed in victory. The perishable will put on the imperishable, and death's sting, which is sin, is undone in Christ. Therefore the believer's labor in the Lord is never in vain.

Takeaways

  • The resurrection is the foundation, not a footnote; if Christ is not raised, Paul says faith itself is futile and believers are still in their sins.
  • Christian hope is bodily and material, not the escape of a disembodied soul; the body is sown perishable and raised imperishable and glorious.
  • Because death is defeated in Christ, present faithfulness has lasting weight, so believers can stand firm knowing their labor is not in vain.

Connections

Paul's victory cry quotes Isaiah and Hosea, drawing the prophets' promises of swallowed death and conquered grave into the resurrection hope. Christ as the last Adam who gives life reverses the death introduced through the first Adam in Genesis. The firstfruits language draws on the Levitical offerings, and the bodily resurrection fulfills the hope glimpsed in Daniel's vision of those who sleep in the dust awakening to everlasting life.

Isaiah 25:8 → Hosea 13:14 → Genesis 3:19 → Daniel 12:2 → Leviticus 23:10 →

Reflection

If the resurrection is really the hinge on which everything turns, does your hope reach all the way to a renewed body and a defeated death, or does it stop short at comfort for the soul? Paul ends not in speculation but in steadiness: because the grave is conquered, nothing done for the Lord is wasted. What labor might you take up again, knowing it is not in vain?
