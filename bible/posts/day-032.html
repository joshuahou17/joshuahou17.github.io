Exodus 7-9

Signs and Wonders Begin

Read Exodus 7-9 in the NIV →

Context

Exodus 7-9 opens the great confrontation that the rest of the book presupposes. The plagues are not random disasters but a structured legal proceeding. God had told Moses he would be made like God to Pharaoh, with Aaron as his prophet, framing the encounter as a clash of sovereignties. Egypt's Pharaoh was held to be divine, the guarantor of cosmic order; the plagues systematically dismantle that claim.

Many readers notice the plagues fall into three sets of three, with the tenth standing apart. Each set escalates, and Egypt's magicians replicate the early signs, fail to reproduce the gnats, and finally cannot even stand before Moses for the boils. The targets are pointed: the Nile, frogs, livestock, and the sun were all woven into Egyptian religion.

Key themes

The LORD against the gods of Egypt. God states his purpose plainly: that Egypt and Israel alike will know that he is the LORD. Each plague strikes a feature of creation that Egypt had divinized, exposing those powers as no powers at all.

The hardening of Pharaoh's heart. The text is deliberately layered. In the earlier plagues Pharaoh hardens his own heart; later the narrator says God hardened it. Scholars read this not as a riddle to solve but as a portrait of how persistent human defiance becomes a fixed condition that God finally confirms.

Knowledge as the goal of judgment. The plagues are revelation, not mere punishment. The recurring phrase that you may know shows that God's aim is for both oppressor and oppressed to recognize who truly rules.

Takeaways

  • The plagues are a theological argument, not a magic show: they declare that the LORD alone is God over creation and history.
  • The back-and-forth language about Pharaoh's heart resists tidy systems; it depicts the mystery of how repeated resistance to God can ossify into a settled refusal.
  • God's judgments here serve revelation: their stated purpose is that people would come to know him.

Connections

The contest echoes the creation account, where God speaks order into being; here he speaks de-creation over a kingdom that enslaves. Pharaoh's death-dealing decree against Hebrew infants earlier in Exodus finds its answering judgment as Egypt itself is undone. The motif of God's signs leading nations to know him reaches forward to the prophets and ultimately to the way the New Testament presents Jesus' signs as disclosures of God's identity.

Reflection

The hardening of Pharaoh's heart is unsettling precisely because it holds together human responsibility and divine sovereignty without flattening either. Where might persistent resistance be hardening into something settled in your own life, and what would it look like to soften while the choice is still yours?
