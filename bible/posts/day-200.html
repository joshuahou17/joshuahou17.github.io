2 Kings 18; 2 Chronicles 29-31; Psalms 48

Hezekiah's Reforms and the Song of the City of God

Read 2 Kings 18 in the NIV → Read 2 Chronicles 29-31 in the NIV → Read Psalms 48 in the NIV →

Context

This reading gathers the parallel accounts of one reign. Second Kings 18 introduces Hezekiah as a king who did right in the eyes of the Lord, removing the high places and the bronze serpent that had become an idol, and facing the rising menace of Assyria under Sennacherib. Second Chronicles 29-31 fills in the religious heart of his reign in detail the Kings narrative passes over quickly.

Chronicles describes a careful, four-stage renewal: the priests and Levites consecrate themselves, the temple is purified, worship is rededicated with sacrifice and song, and the people join in. Hezekiah then calls a great Passover, inviting not only Judah but the remnant of the northern tribes after Samaria's fall, a celebration on a scale and spirit unknown since Solomon, followed by the people's generous offerings for the priests and Levites.

Psalm 48, a song of the sons of Korah, completes the reading by praising Zion as the city of the great King, beautiful in elevation, where God has made himself known as a fortress. Many readers hear in it the relief of a city delivered, fitting the era when Sennacherib's siege failed.

Key themes

Worship restored. Hezekiah's reform begins not with politics but with the temple. Chronicles lingers on the order of consecration, purification, rededication, and participation, showing that national renewal flows from rightly ordered worship rather than the reverse.

An open invitation. The great Passover reaches beyond Judah to the scattered northern tribes, even though some mock the couriers. Grace is offered to those who had fallen under judgment, and many humble themselves and come, prefiguring a gathered and reunited people.

Trust under threat. Set against Sennacherib's advance, Hezekiah's reign tests whether reformed worship will hold when the empire comes knocking. Psalm 48 answers in song: the city is secure not because of its walls but because God is within her.

Takeaways

  • Hezekiah reversed his father Ahaz's apostasy by reopening and cleansing the temple, showing that even deep national decline can be turned around by decisive obedience.
  • Chronicles presents renewal as a deliberate process of consecration, purification, rededication, and participation, not a single emotional moment.
  • The great Passover extended a generous invitation to the surviving northern tribes, picturing the regathering of a divided people.
  • Hezekiah removed even the bronze serpent of Moses once it became an object of worship, a reminder that good things can harden into idols.
  • Psalm 48 locates the city's security in God's presence rather than its fortifications, a confidence vindicated when Sennacherib's siege collapsed.

Connections

This reading sits at the climax of the Assyrian crisis that Isaiah's oracles, just read, had been anticipating. Hezekiah's temptation to trust Egypt against Assyria is precisely what Isaiah 18-22 warned against, and his reign is the historical hinge those chapters orbit. The bronze serpent he destroys was forged back in Numbers, read earlier in the plan, now reduced to an idol called Nehushtan.

The great Passover reaches back to Exodus and forward to the unity Hezekiah longs to restore after the fall of Samaria, covered in recent readings on the northern kingdom in Hosea and Kings. Psalm 48 belongs to the family of Zion songs that celebrate God's dwelling, anticipating the heavenly Jerusalem of Hebrews 12 and Revelation 21.

The pattern of a faithful king cleansing the temple foreshadows later reforms under Josiah and, ultimately, Jesus clearing the temple courts in the Gospels.

Reflection

Hezekiah inherited a temple his own father had nailed shut, and his first act was not military but worshipful: open the doors, cleanse the house, gather the people. There is a lesson in that priority. The threat from Assyria was real and looming, yet the king began by setting right the relationship that mattered most, trusting that a people rightly oriented to God could face whatever came.

The detail about the bronze serpent stings a little. A thing God himself had once given to save lives had, over centuries, become an idol to be burned. Good gifts and old traditions can quietly take the place that belongs to God alone. Psalm 48 ends by urging us to walk about Zion, count her towers, and tell the next generation what God has done. Faithfulness is not only restored in a generation; it is handed on, so that those who come after know the God who is our guide even to the end.
