2 Chronicles 28; 2 Kings 16-17

The Fall of Samaria and the Failure of Ahaz

Read 2 Chronicles 28 in the NIV → Read 2 Kings 16-17 in the NIV →

Context

This reading braids together two strands of the same crisis. In 2 Chronicles 28 and 2 Kings 16 we meet Ahaz of Judah, a king the historians judge harshly. Pressed by a coalition of Israel and Aram, Ahaz refuses to trust God and instead strips the temple and palace of their treasures to buy the help of Tiglath-Pileser of Assyria. The bargain brings trouble rather than rescue. Ahaz even copies a foreign altar he admires in Damascus, installs it in Jerusalem, and sacrifices to the gods of the nation that beat him, reasoning that their gods must be stronger.

2 Kings 17 then narrates the northern kingdom's end. Israel's last king, Hoshea, had been an Assyrian vassal, but when he stopped paying tribute and sought help from Egypt, Shalmaneser besieged Samaria. After three years the capital fell in 722 BC, and the Israelites were deported to Assyrian provinces while foreign peoples were resettled in the land. The narrator then pauses for a long sermon explaining exactly why this happened.

Key themes

Misplaced trust. Ahaz's defining sin is fear that runs to the wrong refuge. Rather than lean on God, he leans on Assyria and on the gods of Damascus, and every alliance he buys deepens his ruin.

Judgment as explanation, not accident. The fall of Samaria is not framed as bad luck or mere geopolitics. The historian insists Israel was exiled because it worshiped other gods, built high places, ignored repeated prophetic warnings, and broke the covenant given at the exodus.

The long patience of God. God did not strike at the first offense. He sent prophet after prophet to both kingdoms, calling them to turn. The exile comes only after generations of warning go unheeded.

A warning to Judah. The chapter holds Judah up to the same mirror, noting that the south walked in many of the same practices. Ahaz's reign shows the disease already at work in Jerusalem.

Takeaways

  • The northern kingdom's fall in 722 BC is presented as covenant judgment, not random conquest.
  • Ahaz models the opposite of faith: under pressure he trusts foreign powers and foreign gods rather than the Lord.
  • 2 Kings 17 functions as a theological sermon, naming idolatry and ignored prophets as the cause of exile.
  • God's repeated sending of prophets shows long patience before judgment finally arrives.
  • Judah is explicitly warned that it is drifting down the same road that destroyed Israel.

Connections

This reading is the grim payoff of warnings sounded throughout the plan. The prophets read recently, Amos and Hosea, addressed exactly this northern kingdom and predicted exactly this collapse, while Micah and Isaiah, working in Judah during Ahaz and Hezekiah, watched it unfold. The disasters of Ahaz also set the stage for the dramatic faith of his son Hezekiah, who will face Assyria very differently. Isaiah's confrontation with Ahaz over trusting God instead of Assyria, including the sign of Immanuel, belongs to this same moment.

The covenant logic of 2 Kings 17 reaches back to the warnings of Deuteronomy, where Moses promised that persistent idolatry would scatter Israel among the nations. The pattern of God sending prophets and the people refusing to listen will be repeated by Jeremiah on the eve of Judah's own exile in 586 BC. The resettlement of foreigners in Samaria also lies behind the later tension between Jews and Samaritans seen in the Gospels.

Reflection

Ahaz is a study in what fear does to faith. Cornered, he does not pray; he negotiates, he plunders the temple, he adopts the religion of whoever seems strongest at the moment. His story asks where we run when we are afraid, and whether our instinct is to trust God or to buy security on someone else's terms.

The fall of Samaria is sobering precisely because the narrator refuses to call it bad luck. It is the slow ripening of choices, made tolerable by God's long patience and made fatal by stubborn refusal to listen. Yet the same patience that delayed judgment for generations is the patience that still calls people to turn. The chapter is a warning, but a warning is itself a mercy.
