Psalms 106-107

Confession and the Song of the Redeemed

Read Psalms 106-107 in the NIV →

Context

These two psalms sit at the seam between Book IV and Book V of the Psalter, and they belong closely together. Psalm 106 is the final psalm of Book IV, ending with the doxology and the call to gather the scattered people from among the nations. Psalm 107 begins Book V with the same vocabulary of gathering, calling on the redeemed whom the Lord has gathered from east and west, from north and south. The book division was placed between two psalms that were originally read as a continuous movement from confession to thanksgiving.

Psalm 106 retells Israel's story as a catalog of failure, from Egypt and the Red Sea through the wilderness and into Canaan. It is, as older commentators called it, a national confession, fit for use in times of exile and judgment. Psalm 107 answers that history of failure with four scenes of rescue, each built on the same refrain, and ends by inviting the wise to ponder the steadfast love of the Lord.

Key themes

Honest confession of national sin. Psalm 106 does not soften Israel's record. It recounts forgetting God's wonders at the sea, craving in the wilderness, worshiping the golden calf, despising the promised land, and mingling with the nations. The psalm prays from within failure, asking to be remembered with God's favor and gathered from among the nations to give thanks.

Mercy that outlasts rebellion. Against every relapse the psalm sets God's covenant faithfulness. He remembers his covenant, relents according to the abundance of his steadfast love, and again and again delivers a people who keep forgetting him. The confession is finally a hymn to mercy, framed front and back by hallelujah.

The fourfold song of the redeemed. Psalm 107 gathers four pictures of distress, wanderers lost in the desert, prisoners in darkness, the sick brought near to death by their own folly, and sailors tossed on a stormy sea. Each group cries to the Lord in trouble, is delivered, and is summoned to thank him for his steadfast love and wondrous works. The structure turns deliverance into a pattern wide enough to hold every human extremity.

Wisdom that ponders steadfast love. The closing lines invite whoever is wise to attend to these things and consider the loving devotion of the Lord. Thanksgiving here is not mere feeling but reflection, a deliberate reading of one's rescue as the work of a faithful God.

Takeaways

  • Psalm 106 models honest corporate confession, recounting sin plainly while appealing to God's covenant mercy.
  • God's steadfast love repeatedly outlasts and answers his people's rebellion and forgetfulness.
  • Psalm 107's four scenes of rescue invite every kind of sufferer to cry out and then give thanks for deliverance.

Connections

Psalm 106 retells events the plan has already worked through, the exodus and Red Sea crossing, the golden calf, the grumbling in the wilderness, and the rebellion that kept a generation from the land. Its closing plea to be gathered from among the nations reaches toward the exile and looks back to the same history Psalm 105 told as triumph. The plea is striking because nearby in the chronological cluster David appoints Asaph to sing words drawn from Psalm 106 on the day the ark enters Jerusalem, turning confession into liturgy. Psalm 107's gathering of the redeemed from every direction anticipates the wider ingathering Jesus speaks of in the Gospels.

Reflection

Set side by side, these psalms teach a rhythm worth keeping: tell the truth about your failures, then tell the truth about God's mercy. Psalm 106 refuses to let confession become despair, because every relapse is met by a love that remembers the covenant. Psalm 107 refuses to let thanksgiving become vague, because it asks the wise to consider, in detail, how they were rescued. Which is harder for you, naming your sin honestly or naming God's deliverance gratefully? The two psalms suggest you will not do either well until you do both.
