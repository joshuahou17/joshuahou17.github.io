Day 278 of 365

Matthew 4; Luke 4-5

Tested in the Wilderness, Rejected at Home

Read Matthew 4 in the NIV → Read Luke 4-5 in the NIV →

Context

Immediately after His baptism, Jesus is led by the Spirit into the wilderness to be tempted for forty days. The three temptations, to turn stones to bread, to leap from the temple, and to seize the kingdoms of the world, target His hunger, His trust, and His mission. Jesus answers each with a quotation from Deuteronomy, the very passages where Israel had failed during its forty years in the desert. He is the true Son and true Israel who passes the test.

Luke then records Jesus' programmatic sermon at Nazareth, where He reads from Isaiah and announces that the prophecy is fulfilled in their hearing. The hometown crowd, at first amazed, turns murderous when He suggests God's favor extends to Gentiles, and they try to throw Him off a cliff. The rejection at Nazareth foreshadows the larger rejection to come.

The reading closes with the call of the first disciples. After a miraculous catch of fish, Simon Peter falls at Jesus' knees, and Jesus calls him to catch people instead. Peter, Andrew, James, and John leave their nets at once.

Key themes

The true Israel overcomes temptation. Where the nation grumbled and rebelled in the wilderness, Jesus stands firm, answering each temptation with the word of God. He is the faithful Son the world has been waiting for.

Jesus' mission reaches beyond the insiders. The Nazareth sermon announces good news to the poor and freedom for the captive, and provokes fury precisely because it includes outsiders. Grace that crosses boundaries offends those who assume they own it.

Following costs everything. The fishermen leave nets, boats, and family. Discipleship in the Gospels is not an add-on to ordinary life but a reordering of it around Jesus.

Takeaways

  • Jesus defeats temptation not by raw willpower but by the strategic use of Scripture, modeling how the Word equips us against the same lies.
  • The Nazareth rejection shows that familiarity with Jesus can breed contempt; those closest geographically were furthest in faith.
  • The call of the disciples reveals that Jesus chooses ordinary, working people and that genuine response means leaving things behind.

Connections

Jesus' three answers to the tempter all quote Deuteronomy 6 and 8, deliberately replaying Israel's wilderness testing read earlier in the plan and reversing its failures. His Nazareth reading is Isaiah 61:1-2, claiming the Jubilee prophecy for Himself. The miraculous catch of fish and Peter's response echo the awe of prophets like Isaiah before God's holiness. Jesus as the new and faithful Israel ties back to the exodus and forward to His role as the obedient second Adam.

Deuteronomy 8:3 → Deuteronomy 6:13-16 → Isaiah 61:1-2 → 1 Kings 17:8-16 → Isaiah 6:5 →

Reflection

Jesus met every temptation with Scripture He had clearly internalized, not as a magic spell but as the word that shaped His allegiance. The same enemy attacks our hunger, our trust, and our ambition; the same word equips us. And the Nazareth scene is sobering: it is possible to be near Jesus your whole life and still take offense at Him. Are there ways His grace toward outsiders offends your sense of who deserves it? The fishermen show the alternative response, leaving the nets to follow.
