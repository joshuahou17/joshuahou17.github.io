Day 83 of 365

Joshua 5-8

Holy Ground and the Cost of the Ban

Read Joshua 5-8 in the NIV →

Context

Having crossed the Jordan, Israel does not march straight into war. Joshua 5 pauses for worship. A whole generation born in the wilderness is circumcised at Gilgal, restoring the covenant sign neglected during the years of wandering, and the people keep the Passover on the plains of Jericho. The day after they eat the produce of Canaan, the manna stops. Provision now comes through the land's own harvest.

Then Joshua meets a sword-bearing figure who identifies himself as commander of the army of the Lord. When Joshua asks whose side he is on, the answer is essentially neither: Joshua is on holy ground and must remove his sandals, just as Moses did at the bush. The battle belongs to God.

Jericho falls not by assault but by obedience. Israel marches around the city for seven days, the priests blow the rams' horns, the people shout, and the walls collapse. The city is placed under the ban, devoted wholly to God, with only Rahab and her household spared. Then chapters 7 and 8 turn dark: Achan secretly keeps plunder that was banned, Israel is routed at little Ai, and not until the sin is exposed and judged does victory return.

Key themes

Worship before warfare. Israel's first acts in Canaan are not military but liturgical: covenant renewal, Passover, and the holy encounter near Jericho. Before God grants the land, he reestablishes Israel's identity as his consecrated people.

The battle is the Lord's. The fall of Jericho is staged as worship, not strategy. Marching, trumpets, and a shout could never topple a fortress; the point is precisely that victory comes from God, so Israel cannot claim the credit.

The seriousness of devotion to God. The ban, or herem, set Jericho's plunder apart as belonging wholly to the Lord. Achan's quiet theft treats holy things as common, and the narrative insists that what looks like one man's private sin implicates the whole community.

Grace and judgment side by side. Rahab the outsider is saved while Achan the insider is destroyed. Belonging to Israel by birth guarantees nothing; what matters is whether one honors or violates the holiness of God.

Takeaways

  • God reorders priorities at the threshold of the promise: covenant renewal and worship come before conquest, because identity precedes achievement.
  • Jericho's collapse by trumpet and shout makes clear that the decisive victories belong to God, leaving no room for Israel to boast in its own power.
  • Achan's hidden sin and its public consequences show that sin is never merely private; treating what is holy as common endangers the whole community.

Connections

The encounter near Jericho echoes Moses at the burning bush in Exodus 3, where holy ground also required removing the sandals. The Passover at Gilgal links directly back to the first Passover in Exodus 12, closing the loop on the generation that came out of Egypt. And the ceasing of the manna ends the wilderness provision that began in Exodus 16, read earlier in the plan.

The principle that one person's sin can bring trouble on the whole community recalls the corporate dynamics of the law in Leviticus and Numbers, where the camp's holiness was a shared responsibility. The Valley of Achor, the place of trouble, is later named a door of hope in Hosea 2.

Rahab's deliverance from Jericho, promised by the scarlet cord, is remembered in Hebrews 11 alongside the falling walls as an act of faith.

Reflection

It is easy to assume that crossing into a promise means the hard work is over. Joshua 5 says otherwise. Before a single wall falls, Israel must stop and remember who they are, renewing the covenant sign and keeping the feast. The land would be a gift, but only to a people set apart for the Giver.

The commander's refusal to take sides is worth sitting with. The question is never whether God is on our side but whether we are on his. Joshua's response is to fall down and worship, and that posture is what makes the victory at Jericho possible.

Achan's story is sobering precisely because his sin was hidden and seemed small. Yet it teaches that what we treat as ours alone can wound those around us, and that honesty before God is the only road back. The same God who spares Rahab the outsider will not be mocked by Achan the insider.
