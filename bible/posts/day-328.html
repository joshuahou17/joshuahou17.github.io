Day 328 of 365

Galatians 1-3

No Other Gospel

Read Galatians 1-3 in the NIV →

Context

Galatians is likely one of the earliest letters Paul wrote, sent to churches he had planted in the region of Galatia. After he left, other teachers arrived insisting that Gentile believers had to be circumcised and keep the Jewish law to belong fully to God's people. Paul writes in alarm because the issue is not a minor adjustment but the heart of the good news itself.

The first three chapters move in two waves. First Paul tells his own story: his gospel did not come from human teachers but from a revelation of the risen Christ, and the apostles in Jerusalem confirmed rather than corrected his message. Then he turns to argument, pressing the Galatians to remember how they actually received the Spirit and reaching back to Abraham to show that faith, not law-keeping, has always been the path to God.

Behind the heat of the letter is a pastor who fears his converts are drifting from grace into a religion of performance. The stakes, as Paul sees them, are nothing less than whether the cross of Christ was necessary at all.

Key themes

One gospel, divinely given. Paul stakes his whole authority on the claim that the gospel is not a human invention to be improved or supplemented. To add law-keeping as a requirement is to preach a different gospel, which is no gospel at all.

Justified by faith, not works of the law. A person is counted righteous before God through trusting Christ, not by performing the law's demands. The law cannot give the life it commands; only faith unites a person to the crucified and risen Lord.

Abraham, father of the believing. Centuries before the law was given, Abraham believed God and was counted righteous. Those who share his faith, Jew or Gentile, are his true children and heirs of the promise.

The law as guardian. The law was not God's final word but a temporary custodian, like a household guardian who supervises a child until the appointed time. Its role was to hold God's people and point them toward Christ, in whom they are justified by faith.

Takeaways

  • The gospel is received, not achieved; adding requirements to grace empties the cross of its meaning.
  • Belonging to God's family has always rested on faith, which is why Abraham, not the law of Moses, is the pattern.
  • The law was good but provisional, a guardian whose job was to lead people to Christ rather than to save them itself.

Connections

Paul's appeal to Abraham reaches back to Genesis, where Abraham believed the LORD and it was credited to him as righteousness, long before circumcision or the law existed. The promise that all nations would be blessed through Abraham, first spoken in those earlier readings, is what Paul says is now reaching the Gentiles through faith in Christ.

His insistence that the law cannot justify echoes the larger storyline of the plan: Israel received the law at Sinai yet repeatedly broke it, and the prophets longed for a deeper change of heart. The letter of James, read earlier, also wrestled with faith and works but addressed a different danger, a dead faith that produces nothing, whereas Paul here confronts a faith burdened with requirements it was never meant to carry.

Reflection

It is easy to begin in grace and quietly slide into scorekeeping, measuring our standing with God by how well we performed this week. Paul's question to the Galatians is worth asking ourselves: did we receive the Spirit by earning it, or by trusting Christ? Where am I tempted to add a requirement, my own or someone else's, to the finished work of the cross? And how might resting in being already accepted change the way I obey?
