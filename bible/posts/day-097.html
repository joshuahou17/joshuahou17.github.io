Day 97 of 365

Ruth

Loyal Love in a Lawless Age

Read Ruth in the NIV →

Context

Ruth opens in famine and death. An Israelite family flees to Moab, where the father and both sons die, leaving three widows. Naomi resolves to return to Bethlehem alone, but her daughter-in-law Ruth refuses to leave, binding herself to Naomi's people and Naomi's God in one of Scripture's most beautiful pledges of devotion. They arrive empty, Naomi renaming herself Mara, bitter, convinced the Lord has dealt harshly with her.

The story turns on Israel's gleaning laws, which let the poor gather grain left at field edges. Ruth gleans in the field of Boaz, a relative of Naomi's late husband, who protects and provides for her beyond what the law required. Naomi recognizes Boaz as a potential redeemer, and at the threshing floor Ruth boldly asks him to fulfill that role. Boaz settles the matter at the town gate, redeeming the family land and marrying Ruth. Their son Obed becomes the grandfather of David.

Key themes

Hesed, loyal covenant love. The Hebrew word hesed, steadfast kindness that goes beyond obligation, runs through the book. Ruth shows it to Naomi, Boaz shows it to Ruth, and beneath them both moves the unseen hesed of God, quietly working through ordinary faithfulness.

The kinsman-redeemer. Boaz functions as a goel, a near relative who buys back lost land and restores a struggling family's future. His redemption of Naomi's line is a small picture of a larger biblical pattern of God reclaiming what His people cannot recover for themselves.

Providence in the ordinary. There are no miracles in Ruth, only famine, fields, harvest, and gates. Yet God is everywhere, guiding a grieving widow into the right field and a foreign convert into the royal lineage. Grace works through the everyday.

Takeaways

  • Faithfulness in small, unglamorous duties is how God often does His largest work.
  • True kindness gives more than duty demands, the way Boaz left extra grain and the way God treats us.
  • An outsider written off by the world, a Moabite widow, can be drawn into the very center of God's redemptive plan.

Connections

The opening phrase, in the days when the judges ruled, ties Ruth directly to the bleak book that precedes it on the reading plan, offering a deliberate contrast to the chaos of Judges 19-21. Ruth's inclusion as a Moabite recalls the law restricting Moabites yet shows God's mercy overriding human boundaries, the same grace later extended to all nations. The closing genealogy points forward to David in 1 Samuel and ultimately to Jesus, whose lineage in Matthew names Ruth explicitly. Boaz as redeemer echoes the redemption language of Leviticus 25.

Ruth 1:16-17 → Ruth 2:12 → Leviticus 25:25 → Ruth 4:17 → Matthew 1:5 →

Reflection

Ruth invites us to trust that God is present in seasons that feel like only loss and labor. Naomi could not see His hand until the harvest was gathered and a child was born. Where am I tempted, like Mara, to read my circumstances as God's harshness, when He may be quietly redeeming my story through ordinary faithfulness, mine and others?
