Luke 14-15

Come to the Feast: The Cost of Following and the Joy of Being Found

Read Luke 14-15 in the NIV →

Context

Luke 14 unfolds at a Sabbath dinner in a Pharisee's house, where Jesus heals a man with dropsy, exposes the guests' jockeying for places of honor, and tells the host to invite the poor who cannot repay him. When a guest blesses those who will eat in the kingdom, Jesus answers with the parable of the great banquet: the invited make excuses, so the master fills his house with the poor, crippled, blind, and lame from the highways. He then turns to the crowds and speaks bluntly of the cost of discipleship, urging them to count the cost like a builder estimating a tower or a king weighing war.

Chapter 15 is set off by the grumbling of Pharisees and scribes that Jesus receives sinners and eats with them. In reply he tells three parables bound by a single thread of seeking and rejoicing: a shepherd who leaves ninety-nine to find one lost sheep, a woman who sweeps her house for one lost coin, and a father who runs to welcome a returning son while pleading with the elder brother who will not come in.

Key themes

The kingdom is a feast for the unlikely. God's invitation goes out widely, and those who excuse themselves over fields, oxen, and marriages miss the table while the overlooked are brought in. Grace is free, but the seat is forfeited by those too occupied with their own lives to come.

Discipleship has a real cost. Between the two banquet parables stands a demand: love for Jesus must surpass even family and life itself, and the would-be follower must carry the cross and renounce all. The feast is by grace, but acceptance carries the weight of allegiance.

Heaven rejoices over the found. The lost sheep, coin, and son all end in celebration, answering the Pharisees' grumbling directly. God is not a reluctant rescuer but a seeking, running, throwing-a-party God, and the danger of the elder brother is to stand outside that joy in self-righteous resentment.

Takeaways

  • The kingdom's invitation is pure grace, but the people most likely to miss it are the respectable and busy who assume their place is secure.
  • Following Jesus is not a casual add-on; count the cost honestly, because he asks for a love that outranks every other loyalty.
  • God actively seeks the lost and throws a party when one is found; resentment at that grace, like the elder brother's, is its own kind of lostness.

Connections

The shepherd who seeks the one lost sheep fulfills God's promise in Ezekiel 34 to seek out his scattered flock himself when the shepherds of Israel failed. The father running to his returning son recalls the homecoming language of restoration, and the celebration over repentance echoes the heavenly joy named in Luke 15:7 and 10. The great banquet that fills with outsiders looks back to the lavish feast Isaiah 25 promises for all peoples on God's mountain, and the call to humble oneself at the table restates the wisdom of Proverbs 25:6-7, do not exalt yourself in the king's presence.

Reflection

The three parables of Luke 15 are answers to a complaint, so ask first which brother you resemble. The younger son's sin is obvious, but the elder's is quieter and more respectable: a refusal to rejoice over grace given to people he judges undeserving. Where in your life are you standing outside the party, arms crossed, sure you earned your place? And what excuse, however reasonable, are you offering the master who has set a seat for you and is asking you to count the cost and come?
