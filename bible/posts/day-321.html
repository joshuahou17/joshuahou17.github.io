Acts 4-6: Boldness, Honesty, and Order

Read Acts 4-6 in the NIV →

Context

Acts 4-6 traces how the church grows under pressure from without and tension from within. The healing of the lame man brings Peter and John before the same Sanhedrin that condemned Jesus, and the council, unable to deny the miracle, can only forbid them to speak in that name. The apostles answer that they must obey God rather than men, and the church prays not for safety but for boldness.

The internal episodes are sobering. Ananias and Sapphira sell property and lie about the proceeds, pretending greater generosity than they gave, and fall dead, a story that troubles modern readers but underscores the seriousness of the Spirit's presence in the new community. Chapter 6 records the first administrative crisis, a complaint that Greek-speaking widows were being overlooked, resolved by appointing seven trusted men so the apostles can devote themselves to prayer and the word.

Key themes

Obedience over fear. Threatened by the authorities, the apostles refuse silence. Their prayer asks not for protection but for boldness to keep speaking, and the place is shaken as the Spirit answers. Opposition does not shrink the church; it emboldens it.

Integrity in the community. Ananias and Sapphira's sin is not keeping back their money, which was theirs to keep, but lying about it to gain a reputation they had not earned. The judgment guards the honesty of a community whose shared life depended on truthfulness.

Servant structure for mission. The neglect of widows is met not with neglect of the problem but with the church's first delegation of responsibility. The seven are chosen to serve tables so the word of God is not crowded out, and the result is that the word continues to spread.

Takeaways

  • The church's prayer under threat is for boldness, not comfort; faithfulness to God outranks the demands of hostile authority.
  • The community's life depends on honesty; the danger of Ananias and Sapphira was hypocrisy, performing generosity rather than practicing it.
  • Healthy growth requires shared responsibility; appointing servants to meet practical needs frees the ministry of the word to flourish.

Connections

The believers' prayer in chapter 4 quotes Psalm 2, the nations raging against the Lord's anointed, now seen fulfilled in the opposition to Jesus and his followers. The apostles' confession that there is salvation in no other name applies Psalm 118's rejected-stone-become-cornerstone, which Jesus himself cited. The judgment on dishonesty echoes the fate of Achan in Joshua 7, who likewise kept back what he claimed to surrender, threatening the whole community.

Reflection

These chapters refuse to romanticize the early church. It is bold and Spirit-filled, and it also has liars in its ranks and overlooked widows in its lines. What marks it is not the absence of problems but how it meets them: opposition with prayer for courage, deceit with hard honesty, neglect with structures of service. The same realism is on offer to any community that wants the Spirit's life without pretending to be already perfect.
