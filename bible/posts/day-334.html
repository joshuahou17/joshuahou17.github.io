Day 334 of 365

1 Corinthians 5-8

Bought With a Price: Holiness, Disputes, and the Limits of Freedom

Read 1 Corinthians 5-8 in the NIV →

Context

Having dismantled the Corinthians' boasting, Paul turns to the practical disorders their misplaced wisdom produced. Chapter 5 confronts a case of incest the church has not only tolerated but is proud of, and Paul calls for discipline meant to restore, not destroy. Chapter 6 rebukes believers who drag each other before pagan courts and then returns to sexual sin, grounding bodily holiness in the indwelling Spirit.

Chapter 7 answers questions the Corinthians had sent about marriage, singleness, and divorce, offering balanced counsel that honors both callings in light of the present pressures. Chapter 8 begins the long discussion of meat offered to idols, a live issue in a city where most public meat had passed through pagan temples. Paul grants that idols are nothing, yet insists that knowledge without love can wound a fellow believer's conscience.

Key themes

Holiness in the body. Your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit and you are not your own, for you were bought with a price. Sexual ethics are not arbitrary rules but flow from union with Christ and the Spirit's residence in the believer.

Disputes and the watching world. To sue a fellow believer before unbelievers is already a defeat; better to be wronged. The church's internal grievances become a public testimony about whether the gospel really changes people.

Knowledge bridled by love. Concerning idol food, knowledge puffs up but love builds up. The mature believer surrenders a real freedom rather than embolden a weaker conscience toward ruin.

Takeaways

  • Church discipline aims at restoration and the protection of the community, not vindictive exclusion; tolerated sin spreads like leaven through the whole.
  • Belonging to Christ reframes the body itself: redeemed at a price, it is meant for the Lord and indwelt by the Spirit, so holiness is relational, not merely moral.
  • Christian freedom is real but not absolute; love for the weaker believer can require gladly setting a liberty aside.

Connections

Paul's leaven imagery and his reference to Christ as our Passover lamb reach back to the Exodus, tying the church's purity to the unleavened bread of that first deliverance. The warning against sexual sin echoes the wisdom of Proverbs and the prophets' marriage metaphors. The principle that the strong defer to the weak anticipates Paul's fuller treatment in Romans 14 and reflects Jesus' teaching that the greatest must become servant of all.

Exodus 12:14-15 → Deuteronomy 17:7 → Proverbs 5:18-20 → Romans 14:13-21 → Mark 9:35 →

Reflection

If your body is not your own but bought at the cost of the cross, how does that change the small daily choices you make about it? And where might love ask you to lay down a genuine freedom, not because it is wrong for you, but because exercising it could harm someone whose faith is still tender?
